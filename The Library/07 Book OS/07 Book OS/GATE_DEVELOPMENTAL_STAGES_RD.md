# Gate Developmental Stages — R&D Spec
**Purpose:** Capture the full developmental arc of each gate (Lower → Wake Up → Clean Up → Grow Up → Show Up) as interview material. This feeds the Gate Voices Canonical update and the hexagram card formula.

**Core insight:** Each gate has TWO WAVE dimensions:
1. **Gate's WAVE slot** — what kind of work this gate primarily contributes to the chapter-level WAVE work (its position in the 8-gate/4-pair structure)
2. **Gate's internal WAVE arc** — how this gate itself evolves through Wake/Clean/Grow/Show stages

The hexagram card draws from both. The gate's WAVE slot orients the question and action. The gate's internal arc tells you which developmental stage the player is at and what move is available.

---

## WAVE/Gate Pairing (Wendell's mapping, confirmed 2026-05-19)

| WAVE Stage | Gate (Internal) | Gate (External) |
|------------|----------------|----------------|
| Wake Up | Skeptic | Victim |
| Clean Up | Emotional Body | Controller |
| Grow Up | Fixer | Vulnerable Child |
| Show Up | Protector | Damaged Self |

**What the slot means:**
- **Wake Up gates:** Awareness/story layer — you become aware you're in a pattern before you act from it
- **Clean Up gates:** Regulatory/metabolizing layer — what you actually feel and how it gets expressed
- **Grow Up gates:** Capacity/integration layer — where the shadow ends and the gift begins
- **Show Up gates:** Identity/embodiment layer — who walks through the door into the world

## Gate 3: Skeptic
**WAVE slot:** Wake Up (Internal)
**Trigram:** Li ☲ Fire — illuminates and consumes; casts light on threat, burns away possibility

### Core Evolution
Lower stage Skeptic **doubts**. Higher stage Skeptic **wonders**.

The Skeptic evolved to discern: is this mushroom food, poison, or psychedelic? Three categories, not two. This is not binary safe/unsafe — it's a full classification system.

**Key prerequisite:** To doubt something, you have to *see* it first. The Skeptic is a perceptual gate. Seeing precedes doubting. This means the Skeptic's first job is actually to look — to notice what's there.

### The Enlightenment Paradox
A perfectly developed Skeptic would achieve enlightenment — it would realize the impermanence of all things and conclude that nothing needs fixing. Therefore, to be an ally, one must be *purposefully deluded* into thinking one is necessary.

This is not a failure of the Skeptic. It is the Skeptic making a deliberate choice: I see the impermanence AND I choose to act anyway. Right-sized doubt + chosen action = the Skeptic in allyship.

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage (Doubter):**
Overused doubt paralyzes. The abjuration spell casts itself automatically. "I'm going to get this wrong." "This isn't safe." "What's the point?" The Skeptic blocks movement before seeing what's actually there.

**Wake Up (Noticer):**
Waking up at the Skeptic gate = learning to see the problems in the world. This is the beginning of learning where allyship is needed. The book's origin story: Wendell's internal Skeptic doubted that allyship was something people knew how to do, AND doubted that existing training was effective. That doubt required seeing first — looking at the current state of allyship and noticing the gap.
*Wake Up move:* Notice the specific problem before doubting whether you can solve it. See first, then discern.

**Clean Up (Discerner):**
Right-sized doubt allows curiosity and exploration. The Skeptic can now ask: "What is the single most likely failure point?" rather than "Will this work?" Doubt applied to specific objects rather than general fields. Doubt metabolized into discernment.
*Clean Up move:* Name the specific thing you're doubting. Narrow from "everything is questionable" to "here is the question worth asking."

**Grow Up (Wonder):**
The higher-stage Skeptic wonders. Wonder is discernment that has opened rather than closed. The question is no longer "is this safe?" but "what is this really?" The mushroom problem solved at full capacity — food, poison, psychedelic, and combinations we haven't named yet.
*Grow Up move:* Convert doubt into a question worth living with. "I wonder what would happen if..." rather than "I doubt this will work."

**Show Up (Seeker/Architect of Inquiry):**
The Skeptic who has grown up shows up as the person who asks the question nobody wants to ask. Uses compressed history of what has failed before. Offers pattern recognition as a gift rather than as an obstacle. The purposeful delusion is chosen consciously and held lightly.
*Show Up move:* Name the question that needs asking. Make the invisible visible for others without collapsing into paralysis.

### Ideal Reader Note
The ideal reader (Green altitude, E2) is likely at the Clean Up stage of the Skeptic — has woken up to problems, can do discernment, but may still be using the Skeptic as self-protection ("I might get this wrong") rather than as wonder. The Wake Up BAR prompt should invite her to locate the specific thing she doubts rather than the general field.

### Allyship Formula
**The Skeptic in allyship:** You have to doubt someone has access to well-being in order to want to help them. The motivation for allyship often begins with the Skeptic noticing that something is wrong. But if the Skeptic never evolves past doubt, the ally is paralyzed — they see the problem but cannot act on it.

**The gift:** The internal Skeptic (directed at self) that has evolved to wonder becomes the person who asks uncomfortable questions of themselves. The external Skeptic directed at systems becomes the diagnostician.

## Gate 5: Victim
**WAVE slot:** Wake Up (External)
**Trigram:** Xun ☴ Wind — penetrates everywhere, circulates story, finds every sympathetic opening

### Core Evolution
Lower stage Victim takes sympathy in. Higher stage Victim (Advocate) gives sympathy to others. Highest stage Victim (Storyteller) gives AND receives — using rapport and sympathy as tools for effective advocacy.

### Structural Relationship to Controller
**The Victim works for the Controller.** The stories the Victim tells are enforcing a standard (the Controller's standard). The Victim's sympathy-seeking is not random — it is in service of maintaining a particular view of what is good, right, and valid. 

This means: **the standard cannot change if the player is addicted to the sympathy they get from upholding it.** The Victim and the Controller are in a co-dependent loop — the Controller sets the standard, the Victim enforces it through story and sympathy-seeking.

### Lower Stage — Egocentric Victim
The lower stage Victim wants sympathy in order to **enroll other people's Protectors.** The play: tell a story of harm → activate the listener's protective instincts → get the listener to protect you/your position. 

Can only receive sympathy. Marks as **Villain** anyone who would or could block that sympathy from flowing. This is the Karpman Drama Triangle operating unconsciously.

People seeking allyship often fall into this category — their entry point into allyship work is through their own victimization narrative, and they want the ally to activate as Rescuer.

### Wake Up — "Woke" Consciousness
"Woke" culture is the waking up of victim consciousness. The Wake Up move is: **becoming aware of the story itself** — seeing that you're inside a narrative rather than inside reality. Not that the victimization isn't real — but that the story about it is distinct from the event.

After Wake Up: the player can take the flow of sympathy as an **object** — can see it, examine it, work with it rather than being driven by it. This is the prerequisite for running it through emotional alchemy (Clean Up).

**Ideal reader note:** The ideal reader has likely moved past the lower stage, but is probably bypassing the *wisdom* of this voice rather than having integrated it. She may have done enough personal development to reject victim consciousness — but in doing so, she's also cut off from what the Victim knows. The Wake Up prompt for her is: notice the story you're currently telling (not the dramatic victim story — the subtle one about why the game is hard for someone like her).

### Clean Up — Metabolizing the Sympathy Flow
After waking up to the story, the player can run the sympathy flow through emotional alchemy. What is this story protecting? What is the emotional charge underneath it? What channel is actually alive here — is it fear, grief, anger, shame, or joy that the story is managing?

The Clean Up move takes the story from social space (external circulation) into the body (internal signal). The Victim's story stops being the only truth available and becomes information about what matters.

### Grow Up — Advocate
The Grow Up stage of the Victim is the **Advocate.** The Advocate can give sympathy to others — can hear and hold someone else's story of harm without needing to be the center of it, without needing reciprocal sympathy.

The Grow Up discernment: **which stories actually increase the well-being of others versus which hold people in unproductive cycles?** This is the Victim's wisdom applied externally — the ability to distinguish empowering narrative from disempowering narrative.

### Show Up — Storyteller
The Show Up stage is the **Storyteller.** The Storyteller gives AND receives sympathy, understanding that without rapport and sympathy, advocacy is less effective than it would be with them. The story is a tool — not an identity, not a prison, not a source of supply. A craft.

The telling of stories IS the Show Up move. The Storyteller knows which story serves the moment, when to tell it, and how to use sympathy as a bridge rather than a manipulation.

### Allyship Formula
**The allyship failure at lower Victim:** The people seeking allyship are already operating from Victim consciousness — they are enrolling Rescuers, not building allies. The allyship work happens at the story layer first. Wake Up: see the story. Clean Up: metabolize the charge underneath. Grow Up: discern which stories serve. Show Up: tell the stories that invite effective allyship.

**The gift:** The Victim's capacity to tell compelling stories about harm — when it has evolved into the Storyteller — is the most powerful recruitment tool for allyship. The story that makes someone feel seen AND capable is the Storyteller's highest move.

## Gate 6: Emotional Body
**WAVE slot:** Clean Up (Internal)
**Trigram:** Kan ☵ Water — depth, danger, what lies beneath the surface

### Core Function (Deepened)
The Emotional Body is not just "feeling emotions" — it is knowing what each emotion **does**. The 5 emotions as functional intelligence:

| Emotion | Job |
|---------|-----|
| **Anger** | Creates boundaries AND creates things (dual function — destroys to make space, builds from that space) |
| **Fear** | Detects threats |
| **Joy** | Takes delight; lightens things up |
| **Sadness** | Shows what you care about and how far away you are from it |
| **Neutrality** | Allows detachment from anything you're experiencing — full-system perspective |

### Key Distinction from Victim
**The Victim locks the grammar.**

If a story is about someone being angry, the Victim locks all grammatical responses to anger — stopping the other emotions from doing their jobs. The Victim can only take in sympathetic charge. Anger rarely generates sympathy (it generates villain-labeling), so the Victim suppresses or bypasses anger, grief becomes the primary vehicle, and the full emotional vocabulary narrows.

**Lower-stage Victim cannot hold multiple emotions simultaneously** because it cannot tell stories with multiple satisfactions. Each emotion has its own satisfaction (what it's trying to do, what it wants). The Victim can only hold the emotional line that its current story supports.

**Emotional Body knows how each emotion is being used to protect the Vulnerable Child.** This is the key developmental difference:
- Victim: tells stories about emotions (manages them through narrative)
- Emotional Body: knows the *function* of each emotion as it relates to protecting the Vulnerable Child

### The Foundational Insight: All Gates Protect the Vulnerable Child
**All gates are protecting the Vulnerable Child.** The Emotional Body is the gate where this becomes visible at the emotional level — you can see which specific emotion is deployed in service of which specific protection. Not "I have emotions" but "this anger is protecting my Vulnerable Child from X, this fear is protecting it from Y."

### Clean Up Move
Once you can see which emotion is running and what Vulnerable Child need it's protecting, you can ask: is this protection still needed? Is the emotion still doing its original job, or has it become the thing that blocks the child?

The Clean Up move: **name the emotion → identify the protection → ask whether the protection still serves.**

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage:**
Emotional confusion — emotions run without being identified. The player acts from emotional charge without knowing which channel is active. The Victim's locked grammar means whole emotional registers are unavailable.

**Wake Up:**
Noticing that an emotion is present — and that it's doing something. Beginning to distinguish between "I feel bad" and "I'm feeling fear right now, and fear is trying to show me something."

**Clean Up (the home stage):**
Naming the channel. Identifying what the emotion is protecting. Running it through the WAVE-Spiral. The Emotional Body's work is primarily Clean Up — metabolizing what's actually there rather than managing it through story.
*Clean Up move:* Name the channel. Ask what it's protecting. Ask whether the protection is still needed. Let the charge move rather than holding it in place.

**Grow Up:**
Knowing which emotion a situation calls for rather than defaulting to the available one. Strategic emotional intelligence — can the player bring joy to a situation that has been only fear? Can they access anger when grief has been the only option? The Grow Up Emotional Body has range.

**Show Up:**
The player can be the emotional presence a room needs. Not performing emotions — bringing the actual charge that serves the moment. The Shaman ally at Show Up: reads the field, knows which emotional channel is alive, and responds from the channel that serves rather than from the channel that's most available.

### Ideal Reader Note
The ideal reader (Green, E2) is likely strong at sadness and fear — these generate the most sympathy and are the most socially acceptable. Anger is probably underdeveloped (she may read her anger as inappropriate or harmful). Joy in allyship contexts may feel frivolous or inappropriate. The Wake Up prompt should invite her to notice which emotions she allows herself in allyship contexts — and which ones the Victim's grammar has locked out.

## Gate 2: Controller
**WAVE slot:** Clean Up (External)
**Trigram:** Dui ☱ Lake — contained, managed field; what is held and what is allowed to flow

### Core Function (Deepened)
The Controller's job is **setting the standard.** It is the creator of the dissatisfaction and satisfaction states — the gate that decides what counts as good, clean, enough, right.

This is why the Controller is in the Clean Up pair: **you need a standard of cleanliness in order to clean up. The Controller is the one who decides what clean is.**

Without the Controller, Clean Up has no direction. With the Controller, Clean Up has criteria. The Controller and the Emotional Body work together in the Clean Up stage: the Emotional Body brings the actual charge, the Controller decides what "metabolized" means.

### The Allyship Question Reframed
When people ask "am I a good ally?" they are really asking one of two questions:
1. **(Lower stage):** Whose Controller is going to protect me? — seeking external validation of their standard
2. **(Developed stage):** Which person will set a satisfying standard for the Vulnerable Child? — seeking a standard that actually serves growth

The entire discourse of allyship is a Controller battle: **who gets to decide how the world should be?** Who owns the definition of "good allyship"? Whoever controls the standard controls the discourse. Much allyship conflict is Controllers fighting for supremacy — not maliciously, but because each Controller genuinely believes it is the one in charge and that its standard is correct.

### The Intelligence of the Controller
The Controller is very smart. It often believes it is the one running the show (and structurally, it is — the Protector is reactive, the Controller is proactive). The Controller builds systems, maintains standards, tracks compliance, enforces consistency. Without it, there is no civilization.

In allyship, the Controller that has integrated the Vulnerable Child sets standards that serve growth, play, learning, and exploration — not just safety and survival.

### Structural Relationship: Controller → Victim → Story
Victim works for the Controller. The Victim's stories enforce the Controller's standard. Sympathy-seeking is in service of maintaining the standard the Controller has set. This means: **if you want to change the standard, you have to work with the Controller directly — not just change the stories.** Changing the story (Victim layer) without changing the standard (Controller layer) produces new stories in service of the old standard.

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage (Enforcer):**
Sets the standard and enforces it through shame, correction, and discourse control. Frames compliance as safety ("it's not safe to say that"). Believes the standard is objective reality, not a standard at all. Battles other Controllers for supremacy. In allyship: polices language, defines who is and isn't a real ally, uses the Victim layer to build the case.

**Wake Up:**
Becomes aware that it IS setting a standard — not just observing reality. Sees that the dissatisfaction and satisfaction states it has been generating are its own creation, not facts about the world. The Wake Up moment: "I'm not describing what allyship is. I'm enforcing what I decided allyship should be."

**Clean Up (the home stage):**
Asks whether the standard is serving the Vulnerable Child or serving the Controller's need for control. Runs the standard itself through scrutiny: where did this come from? What is it protecting? What would it mean to update it?
*Clean Up move:* Name the standard. Ask whose Vulnerable Child it serves. Ask whether the standard can be revised without losing what matters.

**Grow Up:**
Can update the standard — not abandon it, but revise it as the Vulnerable Child's needs evolve. The Grow Up Controller knows the difference between a standard that protects growth and a standard that protects the Controller's position. Discernment about which standards are load-bearing and which are habit.

**Show Up:**
Sets standards that others can adopt voluntarily — not through shame or force, but through demonstrated value. The Show Up Controller is the culture-setter: people adopt the standard because it works, not because they're afraid of what happens if they don't.

### Ideal Reader Note
The ideal reader (Green, E2) has a sophisticated Controller operating in allyship spaces. Her standard of "good allyship" is evolved past the simple compliance model — but it is still a standard, and it still battles other Controllers when challenged. Her Wake Up prompt: notice the specific standard you're enforcing right now. What would you have to give up if that standard turned out to be optional?

## Gate 4: Fixer/Healer
**WAVE slot:** Grow Up (External)
**Trigram:** Zhen ☳ Thunder — sudden arousal, can't sit still when something is broken, gets things moving

### The Grow Up Transition
The Fixer grows up the moment it **stops trying to fix the self.**

In allyship, most fixing is self-directed. People keep trying to become good enough allies and keep failing — because you cannot fix a person (the category error from the canonical file). The endless loop: Controller sets the standard ("be a good ally"), Fixer tries to execute toward it, fails, tries harder, depletes. Identity-fixing is impossible. It was always impossible.

The transition: **when fixing redirects from self-improvement to skill development, AND the player stops being identified with their skills.** You can develop skills. You cannot fix yourself. The grown-up Fixer knows the difference.

### Fixer Works for the Controller
Like the Victim, the Fixer works for the Controller. The Controller sets the standard, the Fixer executes toward it. If the standard is "fix yourself to be a good ally," the Fixer is trapped in an impossible job. If the standard evolves to "steward the conditions for growth," the Fixer has meaningful work it can actually complete.

### The Steward Shift
The Grow Up Fixer becomes a **steward of the conditions for the growth of the Vulnerable Child.** Not the grower — the groundskeeper. Not the one who forces development — the one who clears obstacles and creates conditions.

Trees grow up naturally. The Fixer's job is not to make the tree grow. The Fixer's job is to ensure the soil has what it needs, the light can reach it, and nothing is blocking the path upward. This is structural generosity — the Architect chapter's core move — which is the Fixer grown up applied to systems.

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage (Self-Fixer):**
Fixes the self toward an impossible standard. "I need to be less racist." "I need to do better." Applies repair logic to identity — which cannot be repaired, only developed. Depletes. Cycles. Never arrives at "fixed" because persons cannot be fixed objects.

**Wake Up:**
Notices the fixing is self-directed. Sees the loop: try → fail → shame → try harder → fail. Begins to ask whether the problem is the target (the self) rather than the effort.

**Clean Up:**
Distinguishes between identity (cannot be fixed) and skills (can be developed). Begins redirecting the Fixer energy from "fix me" to "build something." Asks: what specific condition could I improve that would make the right thing easier?

**Grow Up (the home stage):**
Stops being identified with skills. Can develop a skill without it being a verdict on the self. Can also let go of a skill when it's no longer serving. The Fixer as steward: clears obstacles, builds conditions, then steps back.
*Grow Up move:* Redirect from self-fixing to condition-building. Ask: "What is the specific circumstance I can actually change that would make growth easier for the Vulnerable Child here — mine or someone else's?"

**Show Up:**
The Fixer who has grown up shows up as the builder others can enter and flourish in. The structure that runs without the Fixer in the room. Structural generosity: the blueprint as act of love. Success = obsolescence, not indispensability.

### Ideal Reader Note
The ideal reader (Green, E2) is likely a skilled self-fixer who has done enormous amounts of personal work. She has mistaken self-development for self-fixing and exhausted herself. Her Wake Up prompt: notice what you're trying to fix about yourself right now. Is that a skill you're building or an identity verdict you're trying to overturn?

## Gate 8: Vulnerable Child
**WAVE slot:** Grow Up (Internal)
**Trigram:** Kun ☷ Earth — pure yin, receptive, fertile ground; the destination state

### Core Function in Grow Up
The Vulnerable Child is Grow Up (Internal) because **growth is its essential nature.** The Vulnerable Child understands what trees understand: growing up is not something you do to yourself — it is what you do when conditions are right. Trees do not force themselves to grow. They grow when the soil is right, the light reaches them, and nothing blocks the path.

The Vulnerable Child needs the Fixer to trust this. The Fixer's job is not to make the Vulnerable Child grow — it is to create the conditions and then trust the process. The Vulnerable Child will do the growing.

### What the Vulnerable Child Contributes to Grow Up
While the Fixer builds external conditions (structures, skills, circumstances), the Vulnerable Child holds the internal orientation: **trust in natural growth.** Without this trust, the Fixer will over-engineer, over-control, and mistake activity for development. The Vulnerable Child's presence in the Grow Up pair is what keeps the Fixer from becoming a controller of the growth process.

### The Grow Up Pair Working Together
- **Fixer (External):** Stewards the conditions. Builds the soil. Clears the obstacles. Develops the skills.
- **Vulnerable Child (Internal):** Trusts the essential nature. Allows the growth to happen. Receives what the conditions provide.

Grow Up happens when both are present. Fixer without Vulnerable Child = over-engineering, achievement-orientation, growth-as-performance. Vulnerable Child without Fixer = potential without structure, natural growth in hostile conditions.

### When the Vulnerable Child Is Running the Show
From the canonical file: every gate repurposes correctly when the Vulnerable Child leads. In the Grow Up context:
- Fixer repurposes as steward (conditions, not persons)
- Controller repurposes as standard-setter for growth (what conditions serve development?)
- Skeptic repurposes as wonder (what's possible here that I haven't imagined?)
- Victim repurposes as Storyteller (what story invites others into the growth?)
- Protector repurposes as guardian of the growth process (protecting the right to learn, fail, and try again)

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage (Exiled Child):**
The Vulnerable Child in exile: allyship as obligation, credential-collecting, performance. No curiosity. No genuine contact. No learning. Growth is blocked because the Vulnerable Child cannot be seen without risk.

**Wake Up:**
Noticing the exile. Feeling the difference between allyship as performance and allyship as genuine contact. The first moment of "I actually don't know, and I'm curious."

**Clean Up:**
Letting the Vulnerable Child's actual needs come forward. What does this child want that the performance has been substituting for? What would play look like here?

**Grow Up (the home stage):**
The Vulnerable Child trusts natural growth. Allows the Fixer to steward conditions rather than forcing development. Can receive what conditions provide — including challenge, failure, and difficulty as part of the growth process (not as evidence of brokenness).

**Show Up:**
The Vulnerable Child leading means showing up to allyship with genuine curiosity — "what am I going to learn here?" Not "how do I perform this correctly?" The Vulnerable Child who has grown up brings its superpower into the room: the specific unrepeatable medicine that was shaped by this person's specific survival.

### Ideal Reader Note
The ideal reader's Vulnerable Child is under significant pressure from her Controller — the standard is "be a good ally" and the Vulnerable Child knows it keeps failing that standard. Her growth moment is realizing the standard is the problem, not the child. The Grow Up BAR prompt: what would you do in this allyship situation if you weren't afraid of getting it wrong — if it felt like play rather than obligation?

## Gate 1: Protector
**WAVE slot:** Show Up (External)
**Trigram:** Qian ☰ Heaven — pure Yang, first mover; survival is the first question

### The Show Up Failure: Protector Without Damaged Self
When the Protector shows up without the Damaged Self, **it doesn't know its true capacity.**

Most of the time, the Protector is protecting itself from pain and discomfort as a *proxy* for actually understanding the damage being taken. It has never been tested by the Damaged Self's experience, so it doesn't know what it can absorb. It protects against everything that feels threatening — including the encounters that would actually build capacity.

**Allyship failure mode:** Over-protective of emotional and physical resources. Doesn't show up where it costs something. Avoids the depth that would reveal what it's actually capable of. The ally is present but not at risk — and the people being allying with can feel the absence of real skin in the game.

### Protector + Damaged Self Together: Show Up
To show up effectively is to **take damage AND protect yourself from unnecessary damage.** Both are required. Neither alone works.

The Protector's job in the Show Up pair: screen what comes in. Know which damage is unnecessary and deflect it. Protect the interior so the encounter can go deep without becoming destructive.

The Damaged Self's job: calibrate. Know what you can actually survive because you've already survived it. Trust the body's capacity. Provide the Protector with accurate information about real vs. perceived threat.

Together: real-time calibration. The Damaged Self says "I've been through this — I know what this costs and I can absorb it." The Protector says "this specific damage is unnecessary — I'm deflecting it." This is the Challenger chapter's core move: the clean "no" that protects without cutting. That's the Show Up pair working.

### Three Directions of Protection (Revisited in Show Up)
From canonical file: self from others, others from others, self from self (self-policing). In Show Up:
- **Self from others:** Calibrated by the Damaged Self — protects from what has actually been shown to be destructive, not from what merely feels threatening
- **Others from others:** Requires knowing your own capacity first — you cannot sustain protection of others if you don't know what you can take
- **Self from self (self-policing):** The Show Up failure mode — protecting the world from what you might do is over-protection masquerading as virtue

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage:**
Protects from pain and discomfort as proxy for understanding damage. Doesn't know its real capacity. Self-polices rather than engages. The canceller: can identify harm but has no energy left to build anything.

**Wake Up:**
Notices the protection is preventing the very encounters that would reveal capacity. Begins to ask: what am I actually afraid of here, versus what can I actually not survive?

**Clean Up:**
Calibrates with the Damaged Self. Begins to distinguish: necessary damage (that builds capacity, creates real contact, tests the alliance) vs. unnecessary damage (that depletes without return).

**Grow Up:**
The Protector learns to walk with you rather than block you. Gives permission to enter encounters it previously closed. The forest walk metaphor: the Protector doesn't disappear, it accompanies.

**Show Up (the home stage):**
Shows up in the world with the Damaged Self as its compass. Knows what it can take. Protects against what genuinely needs deflecting. Creates real contact because the protection is calibrated rather than total.
*Show Up move:* Enter the encounter with both doors open: willingness to take necessary damage AND clarity about what doesn't need to be absorbed.

### Ideal Reader Note
The ideal reader's Protector is sophisticated — it self-polices rather than self-defends. Her protection is pointed at herself: "I might say the wrong thing, I need to protect others from my potential harm." This is over-protection masquerading as virtue. The Show Up prompt for her: what damage are you actually capable of taking here that you've been protecting yourself from?

## Gate 7: Damaged Self
**WAVE slot:** Show Up (Internal)
**Trigram:** Gen ☶ Mountain — stillness, immovable, has absorbed everything; doesn't perform what shaped it

### The Show Up Failure: Damaged Self Without Protector
When the Damaged Self shows up without the Protector, **it's still doing its job — but it's propping up the Victim's narratives unconsciously.**

Without the Protector's calibration, the Damaged Self takes all damage indiscriminately. It absorbs what should be deflected. It cannot distinguish necessary damage (that builds capacity) from unnecessary damage (that depletes without return). The Damaged Self in this state is resilient but unprotected — it survives everything, but at too high a cost.

**Allyship failure mode:** Not protecting their interior. Taking every hit as part of the job. "I can handle this" repeated until it isn't true. The burnout pattern: the Damaged Self that has been surviving without the Protector filtering what comes in.

The deeper failure: without the Protector, the Damaged Self unconsciously serves the Victim's narrative structure. The Damaged Self's survival stories get harvested by the Victim as evidence for its victimhood. The damage becomes the story rather than the fact.

### The Capacity Insight
The Damaged Self knows its true capacity because **it has already survived.** This is the critical resource it provides to the Show Up pair. The Protector, operating without this knowledge, protects against hypothetical damage it has never tested. The Damaged Self has been tested — it knows what the body can hold.

"Did you die though" is not bravado — it is data. The Damaged Self's answer is always: no. And that answer is information the Protector needs to calibrate correctly.

### The Allyship Calibration
Effective Show Up = take damage AND protect from unnecessary damage. The Damaged Self's contribution:
- Calibrates the Protector with actual survival data rather than fear-projections
- Distinguishes necessary difficulty (what builds resilience, creates real contact, tests the alliance) from unnecessary harm (what depletes without return)
- Carries the accumulated weight of damage without performing it — present but not announcing

### Developmental Stages

**Lower stage:**
Takes all damage. Cannot distinguish necessary from unnecessary. The stories accumulate. The Victim uses them. The Damaged Self is resilient but exhausted, and its resilience is being mined rather than honored.

**Wake Up:**
Notices that some damage is being taken unnecessarily. Begins to ask: "did this have to happen? Was this encounter worth what it cost?"

**Clean Up:**
Works with the Protector to establish criteria for what damage is worth taking. Begins to say "I have survived this — but that doesn't mean I need to keep surviving it."

**Grow Up:**
