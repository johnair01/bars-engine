## The True Origin

**Lizzie Magie** invented **The Landlord's Game** in 1903. She was a follower of Georgist economics — the belief that land monopoly is the root of economic injustice. The game was designed as a teaching tool to demonstrate how monopolistic land ownership concentrates wealth and keeps ordinary people in debt.

The original game had a circular board. Players collected rent from each other as land values rose. But here's the key: the game included a **"prosperity" version** and a **"monopoly" version**. The prosperity version had rules that spread wealth — players could opt into a system where rents fed back into the community pot. The monopoly version was the extraction loop — one winner, everyone else pays rent forever.

Magie believed that playing the game would teach people to reject monopoly in real life. She wanted the game to be free or cheap so it could spread widely.

---

## What Milton Bradley Did

In 1935, **Charles Darrow** — a heating engineer who had played Magie's game — brought it to Parker Brothers (later Milton Bradley). He claimed it as his own design. Parker Brothers already knew about the game. They bought the rights from Magie for $500 (flat fee, not royalties) and renamed it **Monopoly**.

Then they did something specific: they removed the circular board. They removed the prosperity version. They removed the economic lesson. What was left was the extraction loop as the only game in town. One winner. Everyone else pays rent forever. Forever.

Magie spoke up. She said publicly that Monopoly was her game, twisted into the thing she had designed the game to protest. She said the rules that made it brutal were not her invention. Parker Brothers suppressed her. The story got buried. Monopoly became the canonical version. Magie's original game is barely remembered.

## The Emotional Core for Bridge 1

**The scam:** You were taught to play a rigged version of a game that was designed to teach you that the game was rigged. The extraction loop was not the lesson — it was the corruption of the lesson. And you were never told.

**What this makes the reader feel:**
- Angry (that they were played)
- Excited (that they now see the game)
- Triumphant (that the game can be changed)

**Why this works as epiphany fuel:**
The reader didn't fail at allyship. They were handed a broken game and told it was the only game. The anger is not at themselves — it's at the system that gave them the rigged version. But the anger is also empowering: once you know the game is rigged, you can change the rules.

**One line to remember:** *Monopoly was supposed to teach you to hate monopoly. Milton Bradley made it teach you to play one.*
