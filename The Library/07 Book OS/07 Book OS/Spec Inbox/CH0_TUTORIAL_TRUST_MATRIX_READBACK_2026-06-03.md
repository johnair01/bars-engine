# Ch0 Tutorial Trust Matrix Readback — 2026-06-03

## Frame

Trust is being evaluated through three pillars:

- **Capability:** Does the chapter demonstrate the writer's capability or increase the reader's capability?
- **Integrity:** Does the chapter increase the reader's willingness to show up, and does it demonstrate ways the writer has maintained his own integrity?
- **Benevolence:** Does the chapter make the reader feel like a better person, and does it paint the writer in a benevolent light?

The ideal reader's benevolence matrix is distorted by conventional allyship culture. She is primed to believe a good person suffers for the cause, proves moral seriousness through depletion, and distrusts anyone who seems too free, too playful, too unsacrificial, or too willing to enjoy their own aliveness.

This creates the core trust tension of Ch0:

**The author is not performing suffering-for-the-cause, and the book is trying to teach the reader to trust a better benevolence than self-erasure.**

## Summary Verdict

The tutorial pass improves **Capability trust** substantially. The reader now gets to use the systems instead of only reading about them.

It partially improves **Integrity trust** because the writer asks the reader to bring the real issue and make a move. It also strengthens the writer's integrity by refusing vague allyship.

The remaining trust risk is **Benevolence trust**. The reader may still feel that the author is asking her to give up the moral safety of suffering before she fully trusts the replacement: aligned, renewable, playful, discerning benevolence.

The chapter now risks losing trust less because it feels like a manual, and more because it has not yet fully metabolized the reader's suspicion:

> If you are not suffering the way I have been taught good people suffer, how do I know you are good?

## Where Trust Is Still Being Lost

### 1. Benevolence Trust: The Reader's Goodness System Is Being Threatened

The book is asking the reader to stop using suffering as evidence of goodness. That is correct, but destabilizing.

Current chapter message:

> Suffering for the cause is not proof of allyship.

What the reader may hear:

> The thing I have been using to prove I am good does not count.

This is the deepest trust risk. The reader's benevolence matrix is organized around sacrifice, depletion, and moral seriousness. The book is offering play, renewable fuel, discernment, joy, and self-allyship. That is healthier, but it can initially feel morally suspect.

**Editorial implication:** Ch0 needs to explicitly name that the book is not asking her to care less. It is asking her to stop confusing depletion with devotion.

Possible line:

> The goal is not to care less. The goal is to stop using depletion as your proof that the care is real.

### 2. Benevolence Trust: The Author Does Not Yet Fully Define The Replacement Goodness

The author has clearly rejected martyrdom, henchmanship, purity, performance, and saviorism. But the reader also needs to feel what the replacement benevolence is.

Right now the replacement is implied:

- full players
- renewable tokens
- emotional payout
- sustained engagement
- play
- self-allyship

But the suspicious reader may need it named more directly.

**Editorial implication:** Ch0 needs one concise benevolence bridge:

> In this book, goodness is not measured by how much of yourself disappears. It is measured by whether more life becomes possible: in you, in the other person, and in the field between you.

This would align benevolence with well-being rather than suffering.

### 3. Capability Trust: Tutorial Moves Work, But The Chapter Now Has Many Prompts

The tutorial pass increased reader capability. The reader now gets a live issue and repeatedly applies systems to it.

But there is a worksheet risk. Not fatal yet, but real. The prompts work best when they feel like one continuing tutorial, not separate exercises.

Strongest micro-moves:

- "Which private question is hiding inside it?"
- "What would get harder if it actually got better?"
- "What has it been costing you?"
- "After you engage it, do you have more life available or less?"
- "What part is Chance / Skill / Passion?"

Potentially too many if all are left at full white-space emphasis.

**Editorial implication:** Keep the tutorial object, but consider making some later micro-moves more compressed or conversational so the chapter does not feel like repeated interruptions.

### 4. Integrity Trust: The Author's Scars Are Present, But The Integrity Thread Could Be Clearer

The opening confession builds strong integrity because the author admits failure without turning it into theater.

But the middle systems sometimes shift into teacher mode. The reader may ask:

> You showed me your scar. Now are you teaching from it, or teaching over it?

**Editorial implication:** One or two callbacks to the author's own integrity practice would help:

- not "I suffered, therefore trust me"
- but "I had to stop using delay/guilt/scarcity as proof of seriousness"

Possible line:

> Finishing this book required me to give up the private belief that guilt was keeping me honest. It was not. It was keeping me frozen.

This is a scar, but not martyrdom. It models the benevolence shift.

### 5. Benevolence Trust: "I Am Staff" Is Sharp And Good, But May Need A Safety Valve

"If I have to turn off my discernment to help you, I am not your ally. I am staff." is powerful.

It increases Capability and Integrity trust. It may lower Benevolence trust if the reader hears contempt for service, support, or subordinate care roles.

**Editorial implication:** Keep the sharpness, but clarify the target is discernment-abandonment, not service.

Possible follow:

> Service is sacred. Self-erasure is not.

## Opinionated Diagnosis

The tutorial pass changed the trust result in the right direction.

Before the pass, the middle lost trust mainly by becoming abstract. After the pass, the middle gains capability but exposes the real battle: **the reader does not yet know whether she is allowed to trust benevolence that is not organized around suffering.**

That is the book's deepest Ch0 opportunity.

Do not solve it by making the author look more wounded. The author should not cosplay the reader's preferred moral credential. The better move is to teach a new benevolence:

- care that does not require disappearance
- devotion that does not require depletion
- service that keeps discernment online
- play that preserves seriousness without fusion
- self-allyship as the condition for sustainable other-allyship
