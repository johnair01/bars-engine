# SPEC — Ch1 Humane Teaching / Consent Frame

## Problem Statement

Chapter 1 is essentially **how to use this book**.

It introduces the Forest, Six Guides, Eight Gates, gate practice, superpower framing, emotional channels preview, the first Shaman move, chapter moves, reflection prompts, the Second Move, and the first BAR loop.

This teaching load is necessary, but it risks becoming inhumane for the ideal reader if she experiences the chapter as:

- another test she has to pass
- another map she has to master before acting
- another system that diagnoses her
- another demand to become competent too quickly
- another place where language outruns lived contact

The issue is not simply "too much teaching." The issue is whether the teaching preserves agency, dignity, pacing, and consent.

## Core Editorial Decision

Do not compress the Gates first.

First install a humane teaching / consent frame that tells the reader how to receive Ch1:

**The map is not the assignment. The first gate that recognizes you is the assignment.**

This frame should be explicitly gamified. It should contrast the old pedagogical structure of allyship training — school, compliance, court, prove-you-understand, avoid-getting-it-wrong — with the Infinite Arcade's teaching method: recognition, move, feedback, repeat.

Then reread the Gates material. If it still feels over-taught, compress from that clearer baseline.

## Pedagogical Thesis

Ch1 should teach the reader how this book works:

1. The book gives maps.
2. The maps are not tests.
3. The reader does not need to master every framework on first contact.
4. The reader should notice the first gate, guide, or question that recognizes them.
5. That recognition becomes the next move.
6. The move matters more than understanding the whole system.

Chapter 1 should make the reader feel accompanied into the Forest, not evaluated at the trailhead.

## Six Game Master Analysis

### Shaman — Contact Before Explanation

Humane teaching starts with the reader's lived signal.

Ch1 should say, in effect:

> You do not have to know what this feeling means yet. You only have to stop treating it like noise.

Failure mode:

- enforced somatic register
- naming feelings for the reader
- turning the body into an aesthetic rather than a source of evidence

### Challenger — Clean Requirement

Humane teaching does not hide the demand.

Ch1 should say:

> You do not have to master this. You do have to make one move.

Failure mode:

- over-permissioning until the chapter has no edge
- teaching inner work as another way to avoid action

### Regent — Stewarded Pace

Humane teaching hands down the map without making the reader responsible for all of it at once.

Ch1 should say:

> This is the whole map, but not your whole assignment. Your assignment is the next gate.

Failure mode:

- giving all eight gates as an uncontained responsibility
- treating threshold material as a full mastery demand

### Architect — Usable Structure

Humane teaching gives each section a distinct job.

Ch1 should clarify:

- Preview = orientation
- Deep dives = contact
- Journey walk = example
- Five Moves = practice protocol
- BAR = capture

Failure mode:

- teaching the gates several times without role clarity
- making the reader wonder whether she is learning, practicing, recapping, or committing

### Diplomat — Dignity While Confronting Pattern

Humane teaching preserves the reader's relationship to herself while naming the pattern.

Ch1 should keep repeating the principle:

> Every gate began as intelligence. The problem is not that it exists. The problem is that it has been running the whole room.

Failure mode:

- Gates become diagnoses
- reader starts defending herself from the chapter

### Sage — Consent To The Pedagogy

Humane teaching reveals the game beneath the teaching.

Ch1 should explicitly tell the reader:

> This book gives you maps, then asks you to make moves. The map is not the work. The move is the work.

Failure mode:

- Infinite Arcade, Forest, Six Guides, Gates, BAR, Five Moves, and app all arrive as separate tests
- reader mistakes the map for the assignment

## Required Insert

Add a short "how to use this chapter / how to use this book" consent frame before or at the beginning of the Gates section.

Likely placement:

- after `## Section 5: The Six Guides`
- before `## Section 6: The 8 Gates Preview`

Rationale:

- The reader has just met the six guides.
- The Gates section is where teaching load spikes.
- The consent frame can prevent the Gates from feeling like a pathology inventory.

## Candidate Copy

> Before the map gets bigger, let me tell you how to use it.
>
> You are going to meet more language than you can use all at once.
>
> That is normal.
>
> The point is not to memorize the Gates, master the Guides, or turn yourself into a person who can explain the whole system at dinner. The point is to notice the first place the book recognizes you.
>
> When a Gate lands, stop there. When a Guide's question makes something in you lean forward, start there. When a sentence names the thing you have been circling for years, that is not trivia. That is the door.
>

>

>
> Let the first true thing find you. Then make one move from there.

## Revised Candidate Copy — Gamified Frame

> Before the map gets bigger, let me tell you how this game teaches.
>
> Most of the allyship training you've been handed works like school, compliance, or court. Learn the terms. Prove you understand them. Perform the correct response. Avoid getting it wrong. Repeat until the rules change.
>
> That structure trains performance. It does not train play.
>
> A game teaches differently.
>
> A game gives you a map, lets you make a move, shows you what happened, and lets you try again with better information. You do not become capable by memorizing the whole map before touching the controls. You become capable by learning what the controls do while something is actually happening.
>
> Chapter 0 taught you the controls. Chapter 1 teaches you how to walk the book.
>
> You are going to meet more language than you can use all at once. That is normal. You will see the Gates more than once: first as a map, then as a walk, then as a move. That repetition is not a test. It is how the book teaches recognition.
>
> The point is not to memorize the Gates, master the Guides, or become the kind of person who can explain the whole system at dinner. The point is to notice the first place you recognize yourself in the map.
>
> When a Gate lands, stop there. When a Guide's question makes something in you lean forward, start there. When a sentence names the thing you have been circling for years, that is not trivia. That is the door.
>
> The map is not the assignment. The move is the work.
>
> Recognition is not completion. It is where the move begins.
>
> So do not try to carry the whole Forest at once.
>
> Let the first true thing find you. Then do one thing with it.

## Integration Requirements

- The frame should reduce pressure, not soften the book's demand.
- The reader must understand she is not being asked to master the full system immediately.
- The frame must not become an apology for complexity.
- The frame should explain why this process is better than compliance/school/court-style allyship pedagogy.
- The frame should make the book's method feel like tutorial design: map -> move -> feedback -> repeat.
- The frame should make the Gates feel like thresholds, not diagnoses.
- The frame should preserve Ch1's edge: the reader still has to make one move.
- The language should stay clear and direct, not faux-somatic.

- Should the frame mention BAR capture directly, or save that for later?
