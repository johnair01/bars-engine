## How to Build a Poetic Emotional Register

The point is not to describe emotion in neutral terms. The point is to create a repeatable language-space where feeling becomes visible through image, movement, texture, and rhythm.

To do that intentionally:

1. **Start from bodily fact, not abstraction.**
   Ask what the body actually does first. Tightens, lifts, collapses, rushes, widens, buzzes, settles.

2. **Choose an image family that belongs to the feeling.**
   Gravity for sadness. Furnace for anger. Weather or field-sense for fear. Light, taste, or lift for joy. Ground or horizon for neutrality.

3. **Use verbs, not definitions.**
   Poetry gets charged when the feeling is in motion. Let the body *fall*, *buzz*, *open*, *contract*, *sizzle*, *settle*.

4. **Keep the syntax inside the experience.**
   The sentence should sound like someone noticing the feeling while still inside it, not like someone explaining it after the fact.

5. **Let metaphor do the translation work.**
   Don’t name the whole theory. If the reader can feel the image, they can infer the meaning.

6. **Differentiate each channel by motion and texture.**
   Fear is not sadness. Joy is not anger. Neutrality is not blankness. Give each one its own bodily weather.

7. **Use recurrence carefully.**
   Repeating an image family across the manuscript creates a register. Repeating it too often turns it into jargon. The image should deepen, not flatten.

8. **Keep the reader’s experience open.**
   The language should evoke body awareness without prescribing what the reader is supposed to feel in response.

This is the poetics layer: not “how to feel,” but “how to say what feeling is doing in the body.”

## Voice Calibration Guide

### Van-Tweet Voice (Living)

Characteristics:
- Feeling arrives before meaning
- Specific — names the body part, the texture, the timing
- Unpolished — sounds like thinking-in-progress, not processed conclusion
- No "because" followed by reason
- No "I learned that" or "I realized that" as the ending
- Often short sentences
- The paragraph could end in the middle and still communicate

**Example (from corpus):**
> *"The phone was still warm and I set it down and my hands shake."*

**Example (from corpus):**
> *"I don't know what I think until I see what I say."*

---

### Teaching-Mode Voice (Defended)

Characteristics:
- Meaning arrives before or simultaneously with feeling
- General — explains what happens, not what the body did
- Polished — sounds like someone who has processed and is reporting
- "because" followed by a reason
- "I learned that" / "I realized that" / "what this teaches us"
- Often compound sentences with subordinate clauses
- The paragraph needs the ending to communicate

**What it sounds like:**
> *"Fear is the body's early warning system — when we feel that tightness in the chest, we should pay attention to what it might be telling us."*
