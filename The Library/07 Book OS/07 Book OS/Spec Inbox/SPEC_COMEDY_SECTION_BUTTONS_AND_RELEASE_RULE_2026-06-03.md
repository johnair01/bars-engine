# SPEC - Comedy Section Buttons and Release Rule

**Created:** 2026-06-03  
**Status:** Draft — operating rule for the comedy/voice pass  
**Scope:** Book-wide comedy pass; especially Ch0-Ch8 section-level punchlines  
**Related:** `SPEC_COMEDIC_CALIBRATION.md`, `SPEC_COUNCIL_OF_JOANNES_PUNCH_UP_ENGINE_2026-06-02.md`, `COMEDIC_DEPTH_AUDIT_2026-06-03.md`

## Core Rule

The comedy in this book is not here to decorate the manuscript.

It is here to set up a release:

> each milestone and each epiphany should be shaped like a joke whose punchline is that the reader can let go of the things that never worked in allyship and step into a different game.

That means the joke is not the end in itself. The joke is the moment the old burden becomes visible as optional.

---

## What Changes in the Approach

### 1. Comedy becomes section-level, not only chapter-level

We do not wait until the end of a chapter to land the laugh.

Each major section should have:

- a setup,
- an escalation,
- a punchline,
- and a release.

If the chapter has multiple major turns, some of those turns should carry their own comic button.

### 2. The punchline should create permission

The best comedy in this book does one of three things:

- lets the reader stop pretending the old game was working,
- lets the reader stop performing goodness as a survival strategy,
- or lets the reader stop paying for a system that only knows how to collect.

If a line is funny but does not free anything, it is probably not the right line.

### 3. The joke should expose the old game, not the reader

The reader is not the punchline.

The old pattern is the punchline.
The budget drama is the punchline.
The hostile room with better branding is the punchline.
The over-serious approval game is the punchline.

The reader gets to laugh because they can finally see the machinery clearly enough to put it down.

### 4. The laugh should be earned by structure

The strongest jokes so far have come from:

- body vs. script,
- felt truth vs. social consensus,
- costly loyalty vs. absurd loyalty tests,
- structure surviving after the people are gone,
- and the humiliating elegance of trying to be good in a broken system.

Use those collisions.
Do not rely only on commentary or clever phrasing.

---

## Section-Level Test

For each major section, ask:

1. What old allyship move is this section exposing?
2. What is the absurd cost of continuing that move?
3. What lets the reader feel the release of dropping it?
4. Where is the comic button that makes that release felt, not just understood?

If a section has no release point, it probably needs one.

---

## Register Guidance

### Clown

Use for early recognition, self-awareness, and the “oh no, that’s me” laugh.

### Cult Leader

Use when the rule system itself needs to be exposed.

### Jerk

Use later, when the chapter has earned sharper truth and the reader is ready to let go of the old game.

The emotional target is not cruelty.
It is relief.

---

## Practical Adjustment

From here on, comedy passes should look for:

- one section-level joke button,
- one supporting line or echo if needed,
- and one release that says, in effect, “you can stop doing that now.”

In other words:

> the section sets up the cost, the joke names the absurdity, and the punchline lets the reader release the failed strategy.

That is the rhythm we want to preserve as we keep moving through the manuscript.
