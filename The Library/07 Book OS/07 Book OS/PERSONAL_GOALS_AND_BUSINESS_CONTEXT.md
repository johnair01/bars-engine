# Personal Goals & Business Context — Wendell Bernard Britt Jr.
**Built:** 2026-05-11 via interview
**Purpose:** Give AI agents full context on Wendell's direction, not just the manuscript and tools

---

## The Business in One Sentence

Mastering the Game of Allyship is a personal development company that teaches people who are burned out on justice work how to build an effective, sustainable allyship practice — by making it a game instead of a moral obligation.

---

## The Real Customer

**The Achiever Who Paid a Price.**

Not a demographic — a psychological profile. They've done well by conventional measures: career, credentials, community standing. They've read the books (critical race theory, feminist theory, the ones you're supposed to read). They have their favorite podcast. They've gone to workshops and conferences. Some have started nonprofits. Others are doomscrolling to find a new cause to burn out about.

None of it moved the needle on the feeling underneath.

**The feeling:** *I'm not doing enough.* Constant. Undefined. The bar keeps moving. And there's a specific rage — at other people who "aren't doing enough" — that is really just their own self-judgment outsourced to a socially acceptable target.

**The unwinnable game they're playing:** Do more. But more is never defined. The entire DEI industry is structured to make them feel perpetually behind — more reading, more guilt, more obligation, more weight. Everything they've tried has added burden, not capacity.

**What they're actually on thin ice with:** Themselves. Not the movement, not other people. Themselves.

**What the book offers that nothing else has:** It doesn't tell them to do more. It changes the game entirely.

---

## The Amplifier (Secondary Customer)

Podcast hosts, early adopters, influencers, Integral/Teal community members who curate ideas. They buy because it's *new* and *fun* and makes them look sharp for finding it first. They are the distribution mechanism, not the primary reader. The book is not written for them — but they are how the primary reader finds it.

## The Business Model

**Revenue streams (in launch sequence):**
1. **Book** — first dollar, primary vehicle for reach
2. **Allyship Decks** — physical product, add-on to book purchase
3. **Events** — speaking, workshops, game sessions
4. **1:1 Coaching** — small groups and individuals
5. **App Subscription** — bars-engine, the "grown-up" version of the deck

**Current status:** Pre-revenue. Building from scratch after prior work on the project. Kickstarter backers exist (book is ~3 years delayed in shipping — fulfilling this is an early obligation).

**The flywheel:** Book → speaking opportunities → coaching clients → app subscribers. Each layer feeds the next.

**The runway number:** $5,000/month is the threshold where Wendell stops making decisions from scarcity and can go fully all-in.

---

## 12-Month Focus

All three pillars — book, app, coaching — as one integrated system, not separate projects. The book is the front door. The decks and app are the deeper practice. Events and coaching are the live experience.

---

## 3-Year Personal Vision

**Creative fulfillment.** Not financial freedom as the primary goal — the money enables the work, not the other way around.

What creative fulfillment looks like day-to-day:
- Doing podcasts — talking with people, being in conversation
- Designing the game — the mechanics, the rules, the experience
- Working with artists on assets
- Translating big community problems into campaigns people can join
- Researching and developing emotional alchemy
- Running games
- Teaching and coaching 1:1 and small groups

The through-line: **Wendell is most alive when he is in conversation, in collaboration, and in play.** The work that kills him is the work that doesn't have those three things.

---

## The Contrarian Belief at the Core of the Business

**Justice isn't the goal — effectiveness is.**

The brand of justice Wendell was taught to believe in makes you an enemy of reality. Playing the game of justice requires constant high-level moral computation (right side vs. wrong side, good vs. bad) that is too expensive to run while also pursuing a life of well-being. And without well-being, you can't offer it to anyone else.

Allyship works when it's grounded in joy, not obligation. The skills he teaches transcend and include justice — they don't replace it, they make it possible to sustain.

This is the belief most people in the DEI space would push back on. It is also the belief infused into the DNA of MTGOA.

## Competitive Landscape (What the Customer Has Already Tried)

- DEI workshops and conferences
- Books on critical race theory, feminist theory, anti-racism
- Podcasts (including "their favorite angry [voice] telling them what feelings they should be having")
- Nonprofits they've started or donated to
- Doomscrolling — finding new causes to burn out about

**What all of these have in common:** They add weight. More guilt, more obligation, more things to feel behind on. None of them metabolize the underlying feeling or build sustainable capacity.

**What MTGOA does differently:** It changes the game. It treats allyship as a skill to develop, not a debt to pay.

## What This Context Should Inform

- **Voice:** Wendell is writing *for* the Achiever Who Paid a Price, not *at* the DEI industry. The book is a permission slip, not a correction.
- **Positioning:** The competitor frame is not other DEI books — it's the exhaustion those books produce.
- **Revenue sequencing:** Don't skip ahead to app subscription or coaching before the book and decks exist in the world.
- **Creative work prioritization:** Tasks that involve conversation, game design, collaboration with artists, and teaching are the ones that sustain Wendell. Protect those. Automate or delegate the rest.
- **The Kickstarter obligation:** Fulfilling the delayed book to existing backers is both a moral and business priority — they are the first proof-of-concept audience.
