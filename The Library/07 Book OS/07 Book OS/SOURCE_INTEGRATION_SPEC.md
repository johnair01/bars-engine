## Source 2 — The Skillful Helper (Gerard Egan)

### What It Contributes to MTGOA
Egan's Problem Management model is the invisible architecture of the Practice sections:
- **Stage 1: Current Picture** — "Where are you now?" Corresponds to the 8 Gates walk (what's happening, what it costs, what it's protecting)
- **Stage 2: Preferred Picture** — "Where do you want to be?" Corresponds to the "Game" section of each chapter (what does winning look like for this Face?)
- **Stage 3: The Way Forward** — "How do you get there?" Corresponds to the named moves and practice exercises

Additionally: Egan's insistence that the helper's presence is the primary instrument (not their technique) is the basis for the Sage chapter's core claim.

Egan also provides the framework for **not doing the client's work for them** — the skilled helper creates conditions for the client to develop, rather than solving the problem for them. This is the logic behind why the book teaches capacities rather than giving instructions.

## Source 3 — Existential Kink (Carolyn Elliott)

### What It Contributes to MTGOA
Elliott's core insight is that we unconsciously want what we consciously say we don't want — the "kink" is the shadow desire that is generating the unwanted pattern. Her shadow retrieval practice (the "having" exercise) is the model for:
- The 8 Gates walk: specifically, the principle that the gates are not problems to fix but parts of you that have been doing a job — you don't destroy them, you thank them and ask them to step aside
- The Vulnerable Child gate: Elliott's "Genius" (the unconscious part that has been running the pattern) is what MTGOA calls the Vulnerable Child — the real gift underneath the protection strategy
- Shadow-as-desire, not pathology: the principle that the shadow Faces (the distortions) are not failures but information — the village did the best it could with what it had

- The entire shadow work methodology of the book is structurally Elliott; Ch0-Ch2 now carry the needed attribution.

## Source 6 — James Carse (*Finite and Infinite Games*)

### What It Contributes to MTGOA
Carse sharpens the Watts ontology into a practical distinction. A finite game is played to win (fixed rules, defined end). An infinite game is played to continue (rules change to keep play alive). Allyship as currently designed is a finite game — and the experience of burning out, losing, hitting the wall is the experience of finite-game architecture. The book's proposal is to redesign the game as infinite. Used in Ch0 to ground the architecture claim.

Key text: *Finite and Infinite Games: A Vision of Life as Play and Possibility* (James Carse, 1986)

## Source 7 — The I Ching (Wilhelm/Baynes Translation)

### What It Contributes to MTGOA
The oracle card system draws directly from *The I Ching or Book of Changes*, translated by Richard Wilhelm and rendered into English by Cary F. Baynes (Princeton University Press, Bollingen Series XIX). Specifically:
- **Every oracle card's C2 component (Image text)** opens with the hexagram's Image text from the Wilhelm/Baynes translation — "Thunder repeated" (Hex 51), "The Joyous Lake rests on the Joyous Lake" (Hex 58), etc. — rendered into present-tense, somatic, second-person experience
- **The oracle card system's philosophical backbone:** the I Ching's insight that situations have qualities, and naming the quality of the moment is already a form of guidance — this is what makes the cards oracular rather than prescriptive
- **The hexagram names and images used throughout the book** — Emotional Body, Deliverance, Duration, Great Power, etc. — are drawn from this tradition

This is not a decorative citation. The Wilhelm/Baynes Image texts are the "heat source" that makes the oracle card system work (per the Sage distinctiveness analysis). The hexagrams were chosen for structural fit; the Image texts are why the fit is load-bearing.

### Attribution Note
The I Ching is a 3,000-year-old text originating in China. Wilhelm's translation was completed in Germany in 1924; Baynes's English rendering was published in 1950. Using a Western academic translation carries an implicit cultural debt worth naming — not an apology, but an acknowledgment that the wisdom is not Wendell's to claim ownership of, only to apply.

Possible framing in back matter or oracle card intro:
> "The hexagrams that structure the oracle cards come from the I Ching — the Chinese Book of Changes, a three-thousand-year-old system for reading the quality of a moment. I use the Wilhelm/Baynes translation throughout. The situational intelligence in these cards belongs to that tradition. I applied it to allyship. I didn't create it."

### Where It Currently Appears
- **Oracle cards only (not in manuscript chapters):** All 64 oracle cards use hexagram Image texts. The oracle cards are not yet integrated into the manuscript.
- **Zero citation in any chapter draft** — the oracle card system isn't introduced until Ch0's closing section (earn your first card), and the I Ching tradition isn't attributed anywhere in the manuscript text.

### Where It Should Appear
- **Ch0 — Oracle card introduction:** When the oracle cards are introduced as a mechanic, one acknowledgment of the I Ching tradition (brief — doesn't need to be a lecture)
- **Oracle card deck front matter / acknowledgments:** The deck itself should carry an attribution to Wilhelm/Baynes
- **Back matter "Maps I Used":** Full entry acknowledging the I Ching as the situational architecture underneath the card system

## Back Matter — "On the Shoulders Of" Section

**Recommended title:** "The Maps I Used" or "Sources" or "On the Shoulders Of"

**What it does:** Gives readers who want to go deeper a clear map of intellectual lineage. This isn't a bibliography — it's an acknowledgment of which parts of the book are directly indebted to which sources, and what those sources offer to a reader who wants more.

**Proposed format:**

---

### The Maps I Used

This book builds on several bodies of work that each deserve their own reading.

**Ken Wilber's *Integral Life Practice*** (with Terry Patten, Adam Leonard, and Marco Morelli) is the developmental architecture underneath the Six Faces. The altitude sequence — the movement from Red through Amber, Orange, and Green toward what Wilber calls Teal — is Wilber's map. I applied it to allyship practice, but I didn't invent the map. *Integral Life Practice* and *A Theory of Everything* are where to go if you want to understand why development moves in this direction and what each altitude is doing.

**Gerard Egan's *The Skillful Helper*** is the practice methodology underneath every "Try It Now" in this book. Egan spent forty years articulating what skilled helping actually looks like as a reproducible practice — not just a set of good intentions, but a stageable, learnable skill sequence. The three-stage model (current picture → preferred picture → way forward) is the invisible architecture of how this book teaches. *The Skillful Helper* is what to read when you want to go deeper on the practice structure.

**Carolyn Elliott's *Existential Kink*** is the shadow work framework underneath the 8 Gates. Elliott's central insight — that we unconsciously want what we say we don't want, and that the "kink" is information rather than pathology — is what makes the Gates a practice of retrieval rather than elimination. You don't fight the protector gates. You ask them what they've been protecting. Then you go find out. *Existential Kink* is what to read when the Gates feel like they're revealing something bigger than the chapter you're in.

**Yu-kai Chou's *10,000 Hours of Play*** (with Mark Diaz) is the game mechanics tradition this book builds on. Chou's Octalysis framework — mapping the eight core drives of human motivation — is the science underneath the Token and Ticket systems. His quest grammar is the structure underneath the named moves. If you want to understand why the game frame works and not just that it does, *10,000 Hours of Play* is where to go.

**Alan Watts's *You're It!*** (compiled from recorded lectures) is the philosophical ground for why this book uses a game frame at all. Watts spent forty years pointing at a single insight from every possible angle: the most fundamental game is hide-and-seek — consciousness forgetting itself in order to rediscover itself. The game frame in this book is not a metaphor. It's an ontological claim, and Watts is where that claim is grounded. *Out of Your Mind: Tricksters, Interdependence and the Cosmic Game of Hide-and-Seek* goes deeper on the same territory.

**James Carse's *Finite and Infinite Games*** sharpened the Watts insight into something workable. A finite game is played to win — there are rules, a defined end, a loser and a winner. An infinite game is played to continue the play — the rules change whenever they would end the game. The experience of allyship burnout, of hitting the wall, of losing — that's the experience of finite-game architecture applied to work that has no finish line. Carse's distinction is the reason this book proposes redesigning the game rather than trying harder inside the old one.

**The I Ching** — the Chinese *Book of Changes*, a three-thousand-year-old system for reading the quality of a moment — provides the situational architecture underneath the oracle card system. I use the Richard Wilhelm translation, rendered into English by Cary F. Baynes (Princeton University Press, Bollingen Series). The hexagram Images that open each oracle card are from this tradition. The I Ching's core insight — that situations have qualities, and naming the quality is already a form of guidance — is what makes the cards oracular rather than instructional. The wisdom in those cards belongs to a tradition I didn't create. I applied it to allyship practice.

**A note on research:** The burnout claims in the book draw on Christina Maslach's clinical research (the Maslach Burnout Inventory and associated papers identify three dimensions: exhaustion, depersonalization, and inefficacy) and Paul Gorski's research on activist burnout specifically ("Burnout in Social Justice and Human Rights Activists: Symptoms, Causes and Implications," with Seema Gorski-Chaudhary, 2015). Their work is the backbone of the empirical claim that the problem isn't the practitioner — it's the design of the game.

## What "Structurally Represented" Means

These four sources are not decoration — they are load-bearing. When a reader who knows Egan reads the Practice sections, they should recognize the architecture and find it properly credited. When a reader who knows Elliott reads the 8 Gates, they should see the lineage acknowledged.

More importantly: explicitly naming these sources in the text gives the ideal reader permission to go deeper. She's the kind of reader who, when she trusts a book, goes and reads everything it cited. The attributions are not just courtesy — they're part of the value proposition.

**The test:** A reader who knows all four sources puts down the book and says: "He read these. He used them honestly. And he built something that the sources alone couldn't have built." That's the goal.
