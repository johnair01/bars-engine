# The Allyship Triangle
**The book's alternative to the Drama Triangle**
**Source:** Wendell interview, 2026-05-13

---

## The Problem: The Drama Triangle

Karpman's Drama Triangle is the game most allyship plays without knowing it.

Three roles. Everyone has a part:

- **Victim** — the person with the grievance; gets sympathy by telling stories; needs a Rescuer to feel helped and a Villain to explain the harm
- **Villain** — the person cast as the problem; the target of correction, cancellation, or blame; holds the triangle together by being the enemy
- **Rescuer** — the ally in the conventional sense; needs a Victim to feel useful; takes over rather than supports; substitutes their action for the Victim's agency

The Drama Triangle keeps everyone stuck. The Victim must stay a Victim to keep the Rescuer engaged. The Rescuer must keep finding Victims to feel purposeful. The Villain gets cancelled but the system that produced them doesn't change.

Most allyship discourse operates inside this triangle:
- Allies seek Victims to ally with
- Allies cast themselves as Victims of being bad allies ("I'm such a terrible person for what I said")
- Allies perform the Rescuer role and call it justice
- The actual people being "helped" remain characters in someone else's story

---

## The Alternative: The Allyship Triangle

The transformation maps each Drama Triangle role to its Allyship Triangle counterpart:

| Drama Triangle | → | Allyship Triangle |
|---|---|---|
| **Victim** | → | **Hero** |
| **Villain** | → | **Challenger** |
| **Rescuer** | → | **Ally** |

### Hero
The person who was in the Victim role becomes the protagonist of their own story. Not rescued — authoring. Not waiting for the Rescuer to arrive — taking the next move from where they are. The Hero doesn't stop having been harmed. The Hero stops letting the harm define what's possible.

### Challenger
The person who was cast as the Villain — the one being blamed, called out, cancelled — becomes the Challenger. The Villain role in the Drama Triangle collapses legitimate challenge into enemy status. The Challenger role restores the function: naming what's wrong, drawing lines, refusing to pretend something is okay when it isn't. The Challenger isn't the enemy. The Challenger is the one willing to say the thing the room doesn't want to hear.

### Ally
The person who was Rescuing becomes an Ally. The Rescuer takes over. The Ally holds space. The Rescuer needs the Victim to stay a Victim. The Ally works toward the Victim becoming a Hero — toward their own obsolescence. Real allyship succeeds when the ally is no longer needed in that role.

---

## Why This Is the Book

The six Faces are the capacities required to play each Allyship Triangle role well:

- **The Hero** needs the Shaman (emotional intelligence to act from felt truth), the Architect (systems thinking to build alternatives), and the Player (self-authorship)
- **The Challenger** needs the Challenger Face (clean "no," drawing lines) and the Sage (altitude awareness to see the whole board)
- **The Ally** needs the Diplomat (holding the field), the Regent (stewardship, passing the work on)

The 8 Gates are what keep people stuck in the Drama Triangle:
- The Protector keeps the Rescuer scanning for victims to protect
- The Controller manages the discourse in the Rescuer role
- The Skeptic prevents the Victim from trusting that they can become the Hero
- The Fixer/Healer tries to fix persons (Victim/Villain) instead of circumstances
- The Emotional Body, unprocessed, keeps everyone reactive and in role
- The Victim Gate is the Drama Triangle's Victim role internalized
- The Damaged Self stays stuck in the wound rather than moving toward Hero
- The Vulnerable Child, in exile, cannot author anything

Walking the gates is how you leave the Drama Triangle. The Allyship Triangle is what you're building toward.
