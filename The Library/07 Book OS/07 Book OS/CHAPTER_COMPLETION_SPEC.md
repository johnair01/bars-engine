# MTGOA Chapter Completion Spec
**Type:** Editorial Design Reference
**Created:** 2026-05-11
**Use:** Open this before editing any chapter. It defines what "done" means for each one.

---

## Purpose

Every chapter has the same structural scaffold (Exile → Distortion → Concept → Gates → Practice). That scaffold is working. What's missing is a consistent layer on top of it: the voice moves, reader catches, confessional moments, and Face embodiment that make each chapter feel like Wendell wrote it — not like a capable writer approximating Wendell.

This spec defines what "done" looks like, universally and by chapter, so we can close the gap systematically instead of chapter by chapter with no shared memory.

---

## Universal Requirements — Every Chapter

### 1. Wendell's Confessional Moment
One passage in Wendell's own voice where he names his personal relationship with that Face — not in the third person, not as an anecdote about someone else. His experience, told from inside it.

**What to ask before drafting:** "Tell me about a time you had access to this Face and didn't use it. Or a time you mistook its shadow for the real thing. What happened?"

**The confession lands hardest when the failure is the thing the chapter claims to teach.** See Ch0 model: "I was writing a book about allyship, and it was making me a worse ally."

### 2. Ideal Reader Catch Paragraph
One paragraph that names her specific trap for this Face — precise enough that she feels recognized, not shamed. See chapter-by-chapter catch targets below.

**The test:** A reader who is already doing advanced, therapy-fluent allyship reads this paragraph and thinks: *this is about me specifically, not about beginners.*

### 3. Face Embodiment
The chapter should carry the register of its Face — not just describe it. See the Face Register Guide below.

**The test:** Read the first page aloud. What Face does it sound like? If the answer is "Architect" for every chapter, the embodiment is missing.

### 4. Parentheticals (minimum 3 per chapter)
Each parenthetical must do one of: land a small joke that doesn't undermine the argument, self-correct mid-stride in a way that sharpens the claim, or acknowledge the reader is already ahead.

**Do not add parentheticals that just clarify.** Clarity is for the prose. Parentheticals are for aliveness.

### 5. BAR Capture Prompt (minimum 1 per chapter, ideally 2-3)
At minimum one explicit prompt to capture a BAR. In voice — not as an appendix. The reader has just walked through something real. Name it.

**Model prompt (adapt by chapter):**
> *You just walked through something. Before the insight fades — what happened, what you felt, what's now clearer. Open the app. Two minutes. It becomes yours.*

**Where prompts belong:**
- After the reader recognition moment in the Exile section
- After any "Try It Now" practice
- At chapter end

### 6. Image Ending
The chapter's final line should land on an image, a verdict, or a three-part declarative — not a summary. Cut any paragraph that explains what just happened.

### 7. No Teaching Mode After the Landing
The last real sentence is the last sentence. If there's a paragraph after the real ending that summarizes or packages, cut it.

## Face Register Guide

**⚠️ Superseded for character/voice specifics:** Load `WENDELL_FACE_VOICES.md` instead. The descriptions below are generic archetypes. Wendell's actual versions are more specific (his Challenger is warm before it's confrontational; his Architect is a mad scientist; his Sage is Uncle Iroh in disguise). The generic descriptions below are preserved for structural orientation only.

Each chapter's prose should carry the register of its Face. This is not about chapter topic — it's about how the chapter *feels* to read.

### Shaman — Ch1 and Ch2
**Register:** Somatic, body-first, interior.
**What this feels like:** Short sentences that land in the chest. Weather, texture, temperature. The knowing arrives before the explanation. The reader should feel something in their body before they understand what they've felt.
**Voice moves to use:** Surface/deep structure. The parenthetical that names what the body was doing before the mind caught up. Confession from inside the experience, not from after.
**Avoid:** Anything that sounds like a neuroscience explainer. Any sentence that analyzes the somatic experience rather than demonstrating it.

### Challenger — Ch3
**Register:** Confrontational, direct, clean.
**What this feels like:** The chapter should draw a line somewhere. There should be a moment that feels like a clean "no" — not aggressive, but unambiguous. Short declaratives that don't negotiate.
**Voice moves to use:** The Let Me Be Direct escalation. The anticipatory objection that pre-empts before the reader can form the resistance. The two-word sentence that earns white space.
**Avoid:** Any softening of the chapter's central claim. The Challenger chapter that hedges is a failed Challenger chapter.

### Regent — Ch4
**Register:** Long view, stewardship, weight.
**What this feels like:** Something is being handed down. The sentences should have earned gravity — not heaviness, but the kind of weight that comes from things that have lasted. The reader should feel like they're receiving something, not just learning something.
**Voice moves to use:** The three-part declarative at closings. The image ending that shows something being passed. Reference to what has been lost when this Face goes quiet.
**Avoid:** Sentimentality about tradition. The Regent chapter should honor what lasts without nostalgia.

### Architect — Ch5
**Register:** Systematic, design-forward, pattern-revealing.
**What this feels like:** The reader suddenly sees the structure they've been inside. There's an "oh" moment — not just intellectual, but the feeling of having the pattern named. The chapter should make the invisible visible.
**Voice moves to use:** The numbered setup → subversion. The pop culture pivot (games, D&D, systems). The parenthetical that acknowledges the reader is already designing in their head.
**Avoid:** Jargon that makes systems feel cold. The Architect chapter should make systems feel like liberation, not engineering.

### Diplomat — Ch6
**Register:** Holding tension, bridge-building, multiple altitudes at once.
**What this feels like:** The chapter itself should hold multiple altitudes — the reader should feel the Diplomat at work in the prose. There should be a moment where two things that seem in conflict are both honored without collapsing into each other.
**Voice moves to use:** The anticipatory objection that holds both sides. The surface/deep structure that names the visible position and the actual position underneath.
**Avoid:** False resolution. The Diplomat chapter that makes bridge-building sound easy is lying.

### Sage — Ch7
**Register:** Panoramic, paradoxical, altitude-aware.
**What this feels like:** The reader should feel like they're seeing the whole board for the first time. Not in a comfortable way — in a disorienting way that resolves into clarity. The Sage chapter should be the one that names the pattern of the whole book from inside the book.
**Voice moves to use:** The named game (Trauma Olympics, Captain Save-a-Kid — this is already working in Ch7). The paradox that holds without resolving too fast. The question underneath the question.
**Avoid:** Confirmation. The Sage chapter is disruption, not validation. The reader who expects to be told she's wise needs to be shown where she's been hiding.

### Player — Ch8
**Register:** Generative, self-authoring, integrative.
**What this feels like:** The reader is being handed the design tools. Not more capacity from outside — the recognition that they've been building this capacity all along. The Player chapter should feel like the reader has arrived somewhere, not like they've received a final lesson.
**Voice moves to use:** The image ending that shows the reader designing their own game. The three-part declarative that names what the reader is now capable of. The callback to Ch0 that closes the arc.
**Avoid:** The graduation speech. The Player chapter should feel like a beginning, not a commencement.

## Ideal Reader Catch — By Chapter

These are the specific traps to name for the ideal reader in each chapter. The language should name her experience without naming the Integral framework (no "Green altitude," no "flatland," no developmental hierarchy vocabulary).

### Ch0 — Infinite Arcade
**Her trap:** She graduated from the courtroom version of allyship years ago. She's in the advanced unwinnable game — trying harder, credential-collecting, language-shifting, burning out.
**Catch sentence (already in draft):** *You are not here because you haven't tried. You are here because trying harder has stopped working, and some part of you has started to wonder whether the game itself is the problem.*
**Status:** ✓ DONE (confession paragraph added 2026-05-11)

### Ch1 — Shaman
**Her trap:** She has projected felt-sense wisdom onto marginalized people as if it's innate to them and foreign to her. She thinks "body knowing" belongs to people who are closer to the earth, closer to suffering, closer to their roots. She has learned to distrust her own somatic intelligence — it feels like appropriation, like claiming something that isn't hers.
**Catch:** The Shaman's first move is to help her recognize that what she's been projecting outward is hers. The body that tightened in that meeting wasn't appropriating anything. It was reading the room.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING

### Ch2 — Shaman (continued)
**Her trap:** She is fluent in the language of felt-sense — she's read the somatic practitioners, she does the body scans, she talks about nervous system regulation. What she hasn't done is trust the reading when it conflicts with the Green consensus in the room.
**Catch:** There's a difference between being literate in somatic intelligence and being willing to act on it when the room says otherwise. She's been practicing the literacy. The practice this chapter asks for is the trust.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING (lower priority — Ch2 is above word count target)

### Ch3 — Challenger
**Her trap:** She has named the Challenger capacity "oppressor energy." The clean "no" is what oppressors do. Boundaries are how power protects itself. She has been in relationships where someone used Challenger energy as control — and she can't distinguish that from the real thing.
**Catch:** The shadow Challenger uses "no" to control. The real Challenger uses "no" to protect — including to protect the person they're saying no to. She knows the difference in her body. She's been refusing to trust it.
**The most important sentence the chapter needs:** Something that names what happens when she doesn't draw the line — that the cost of not drawing it is always paid by someone else.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING (Ch3 is at word count target — this is a voice gap, not a length gap)

### Ch4 — Regent
**Her trap:** She reads stewardship as conservatism. The Regent is the one who protects things — and what gets protected, in her experience, is usually the thing that's been harming her community. She has spent years fighting people who invoked "tradition" to justify harm. She can't receive the Regent's gift because she's seen too much of its shadow.
**Catch:** The Regent is not asking her to protect the status quo. The Regent is asking her to protect what's actually worth protecting — and to know the difference. That requires the exact same discernment she's been developing for years. She already has this capacity. She just hasn't named it as the Regent's gift.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING

### Ch5 — Architect
**Her trap:** She reads systems thinking as capitalism. The Architect is the one who optimizes — and optimization, in her experience, is how human beings get turned into metrics. She has watched too many organizations use "systems" as an excuse to stop caring about individuals. She has opted out of systems thinking as an ethical stance.
**Catch:** The Architect she's been refusing is not the one who designed the systems that harm her community. The Architect she's been refusing is the one who could design the alternative. The community has been running on good intentions for years. The Architect can show her why good intentions keep producing mediocre outcomes — and what to build instead.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING

### Ch6 — Diplomat
**Her trap:** She is fluent in the Diplomat. She's been practicing bridge-building for years. What she hasn't noticed is that she only practices it in rooms where everyone already agrees that suffering matters — in Green-coded rooms, with Green-coded people. When the room shifts altitude, she stops being a Diplomat and starts being a Green coalition builder who thinks she's doing cross-difference work.
**Catch:** Diplomacy practiced only with people who already share your values is not diplomacy. It's consensus management. Real Diplomacy holds a room that doesn't want to be held — including rooms where people she disagrees with have real needs.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING (lower priority — Ch6 is at word count target)

### Ch7 — Sage
**Her trap:** She has extensive meta-awareness. She knows what Integral theory says, what spiral dynamics says, what the developmental map looks like. She uses that meta-awareness as a form of altitude — a way to hover above the room without committing to any of it. Her Sage capacity is real. But she is using it to opt out, not to engage.
**Catch:** The Sage chapter is not confirmation of her meta-awareness. It's a question: *are you seeing from altitude, or are you hiding at altitude?* Seeing the whole board is only the Sage's gift when you're willing to play a piece on it.
**This is the disruption chapter.** She expects to be validated here. She needs to be caught.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING (Ch7 is above word count target — this is a voice gap)

### Ch8 — Player
**Her trap:** She has practiced each Face. She is now in danger of making the Player another credential — another thing to be in a way that can be evaluated and found adequate or inadequate. The Player chapter needs to pre-empt this specifically: the Player is not the graduation from the other Faces. It's the recognition that you've been playing all along.
**Catch:** By now she has everything she needs. The only question is whether she'll use it — or whether she'll make "being a Player" the next version of the unwinnable game.
**Status:** ✗ MISSING

## Editorial Sequence (Recommended)

1. **Ch1** — Foundation chapter. Voice + reader catch + somatic register + exercise. Word count gap closes as a result.
2. **Ch3** — Hardest chapter for the reader. Catch paragraph + Challenger register in concept section.
3. **Ch7** — The disruption chapter. Reader catch is the primary job. The prose is already working.
4. **Ch8** — Six Faces integration section (Issue 6). Then catch paragraph.
5. **Ch4** — Regent reader catch + confession + concrete scenario.
6. **Ch5** — Architect reader catch + one parenthetical set.
7. **Voice pass: Ch2, Ch6** — confession moments + parentheticals.
8. **BAR prompts across all chapters** — once the voice is in, add the prompts in Wendell's voice.

## The Pre-Draft Protocol for Each Chapter

Before drafting any confessional moment or reader catch:

1. **Interview Wendell first.** Ask: "What is your personal relationship with this Face? When did you have access to it and not use it? When did you mistake the shadow for the real thing?" Draft from his answer, not from the framework.

2. **Name the exact trap.** Don't approximate the reader catch. Use the specific language from this spec — her specific version, not a general version.

3. **Check the register.** Before writing anything, name what Face you're writing in. If the answer isn't the chapter's Face, reorient.

4. **Read aloud before delivering.** If a sentence wouldn't land in spoken delivery, rewrite it. The rhythm carries the register.

---

## The Test for "Done"

A chapter is done when:

- [ ] There is a confession in Wendell's voice — a specific moment, not a principle
- [ ] There is a reader catch paragraph precise enough to feel like it's about her specifically
- [ ] The first page carries the register of the chapter's Face
- [ ] There are at least 3 parentheticals doing real work
- [ ] There is at least 1 BAR capture prompt in Wendell's voice
- [ ] The chapter ends on image, verdict, or three-part declarative — not summary
- [ ] There are no summary paragraphs after the real last line
- [ ] Reading the chapter aloud: rhythm holds, voice breaks into emphasis, the landing lands
