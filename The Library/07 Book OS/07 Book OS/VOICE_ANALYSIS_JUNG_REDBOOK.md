# Voice Analysis: Carl Jung — *The Red Book (Liber Novus)*
**For:** MTGOA Editorial Reference
**Created:** 2026-05-08
**Series:** Influence Analyses — 6 Face Framework

---

## Core Essence

*The Red Book* was written by Jung between 1913 and 1930 during what he called his "confrontation with the unconscious." It is not the academic Jung of *Psychological Types* or *Aion*. It is Jung descending into his own interior, encountering figures — Elijah, Salome, a serpent, a figure he names Philemon — having actual conversations with them, and reporting what they said.

He did not publish it during his lifetime. He wrote it in calligraphy, illustrated it himself, and kept it locked in a red leather-bound book. It was released in 2009, 48 years after his death.

This context is load-bearing for the voice: **this is a man writing for his own survival, not for an audience**. The result is the most unguarded prose of his career. He says things he does not fully understand yet. He encounters ideas that disturb him. He reports faithfully even when — especially when — what he found contradicts what he wanted to find.

His baseline move: descend, encounter, report. Do not analyze. Do not categorize. Meet what is there and say what it said to you.

## The Six Faces

### The Shaman
*How Jung stays with the feeling long enough to be changed by it*

The Red Book is Shaman practice as literature. Jung does not analyze the unconscious figures he encounters — he enters into relationship with them. He argues with Philemon. He is seduced and troubled by Salome. He sits with the serpent. He allows the encounter to take longer than he planned, to go further than he intended, to change him in ways he didn't anticipate.

His Shaman technique: **the encounter that does not conclude**. Most writing about difficult feelings moves toward resolution — understanding, integration, acceptance. Jung's Red Book encounters never fully resolve. They change him, but they don't end. He returns to the same figures across years. The figures evolve. So does he.

*"The spirit of the depths pulled me down from the light of the scientific view, and led me away from those spaces where men live in their houses."*

This sentence is the Shaman's first move: the descent. Not a metaphorical descent. An actual event that happened to the writer and is being reported accurately. The Shaman voice is: *I went somewhere. Here is what happened there.*

His specific Shaman technique: **archaic register as access to the deep**. He writes "Soul" with a capital letter. He writes "the spirit of the depths." The register signals: we are below the level at which contemporary analytical vocabulary applies. We are in older territory. The archaic language is not affectation — it is accuracy. The things he is describing existed before the modern vocabulary for them.

**To write like Jung-Shaman:** Descend before you describe. Report from inside the experience, not from above it. Use the register that fits the depth of what you're describing, even if it feels excessive. Let the encounter take as long as it takes. Do not resolve it before it resolves itself.

### The Challenger
*How Jung confronts through encounter rather than argument*

Jung's Challenge is delivered by the figures themselves. Philemon tells him things he disagrees with. The dead make demands he doesn't want to honor. His own unconscious produces images that challenge his waking assumptions.

His Challenger technique: **the dialogue that changes you against your will**. He doesn't challenge the reader — he shows the reader what it looks like to be challenged by something you cannot dismiss. The challenge arrives as an encounter, not as an argument. It has a face. It speaks. You can argue with it, but it doesn't go away.

*"I said: 'I am afraid. But who are you?' The figure replied: 'I am what you have avoided.'"*

(This is the spirit of multiple Red Book encounters, not a direct quote.) This is the Challenge: meeting the thing you have organized your life to avoid. Not as an idea. As a figure. Something that stands in your way and speaks.

His deepest Challenge move: **the thing you've been right about that has made you wrong about something larger**. Jung was right about the unconscious. He was right about the archetypes. And those correct ideas had made him arrogant, had made him avoid the direct encounter with his own depths, had made him theorize where he should have descended. The challenge from his own unconscious is: your correctness is your avoidance.

**To write like Jung-Challenger:** Don't argue. Create the encounter. Give the challenge a voice, a face, a specific thing it says. Let the character encounter it directly. Show the resistance. Show what happens when the resistance breaks.

### The Regent
*How Jung stewards the ancient traditions he is recovering*

The Red Book is explicitly in conversation with Gnosticism, alchemy, the Mithraic mysteries, Hermeticism. Jung is not studying these traditions — he is recovering them. He enters their language, their symbolic register, their way of speaking about interior experience. He treats them as living systems, not historical artifacts.

His Regent technique: **reverent excavation**. He does not modernize the old material. He does not translate it into contemporary psychological vocabulary (he will do that later, in his academic work). Here, he enters the old language and speaks it. He honors the tradition by taking it seriously as a living transmission.

*"The way of what is to come leads through chaos."*

This is not an academic statement. It is a transmission in the style of the ancient wisdom traditions — the Hermetic texts, the Gnostic scriptures, the alchemical manuals. The register signals: this is how the old ones spoke about these things, and they were speaking accurately.

His Regent insight: **the old traditions preserved something real that was lost when they were institutionalized or dismissed**. The alchemists were not primitive chemists — they were doing something that does not have a contemporary name, and they developed a sophisticated symbolic vocabulary for it. Honoring that vocabulary means using it, not translating it out of itself.

**To write like Jung-Regent:** Before you use the contemporary vocabulary, find the old vocabulary. Learn the language of whoever first named this experience. Use their terms before you use yours. The old names have accumulated gravity. They carry the weight of everyone who has used them to describe this thing before you.

### The Architect
*How Jung builds the system by walking through it*

The Red Book is not a systematic work. It is the raw material from which the system will eventually be built. But the architecture is present — it's just implicit rather than explicit. The individuation process (the movement from ego-dominance toward the integration of the self) is described by being lived, not by being diagrammed.

His Architect technique: **let the system emerge from the journey rather than being stated before it**. He doesn't say "here is the individuation process." He shows a man individuating. The reader who wants the system will find it — it's all there. But it can only be found by following the journey, not by reading a map of it.

His deeper Architect insight: **the encounter precedes the understanding**. He does not understand what the figures mean when he first meets them. He works backward from the encounter to the meaning, across years. The architecture is always being constructed from the inside while it's being inhabited.

**To write like Jung-Architect:** Build the architecture by walking through it. Don't announce the system before you've experienced it. Let the reader find the design by following the journey. The map that emerges from the journey is more trustworthy than the map drawn before it.

### The Diplomat
*How Jung holds the tension between consciousness and the unconscious*

The specific diplomatic challenge of the Red Book: consciousness and the unconscious are at war. Consciousness wants clarity, order, the waking world. The unconscious wants expression, recognition, descent. If consciousness wins, the unconscious is suppressed and produces neurosis. If the unconscious wins, the conscious ego is flooded and produces psychosis.

Jung's Diplomat move: **the transcendent function**. Not resolution in favor of either side. The third thing that is created by the tension between them. He holds the field between consciousness and the unconscious by refusing to let either one win — and out of that refusal, something new emerges that is neither.

His Diplomat technique: **the dialogue as form**. The Red Book is structured as a series of dialogues — Jung speaking to interior figures, interior figures speaking back. The dialogue form is itself diplomatic: it requires two voices. It cannot resolve into one voice without ending. He holds the field by maintaining the conversation.

**To write like Jung-Diplomat:** When two things are in genuine tension, write the dialogue. Give both voices full expression. Don't resolve toward one. Wait for the third thing — the thing that could only emerge from the collision of the two. Let the dialogue run until something new speaks.

### The Sage
*How Jung mythologizes the personal without losing the personal*

This is Jung's most specific contribution: **the individual experience as myth, held simultaneously with the individual experience as biography**. He remains Carl Jung — a specific man, living in Küsnacht, working as a psychiatrist, having these visions in his study at night. He also becomes Everyman encountering the collective unconscious — the figure who descends so that others can understand their own descents.

His Sage technique: **first person as mythic register**. He doesn't say "a man descended." He says "I descended." But the "I" is used as a character in a myth is used — as the representative figure of a universal experience. The personal pronoun carries universal weight.

*"I saw that I had to go through the terror of this time, through the chaos of the world, through the underworld of the soul."*

"I" here is Carl Jung, specific man. "I" here is the soul encountering its own depths, which is what has always happened and will always happen. Both are true. The Sage move is holding both truths in the same first-person pronoun.

His deepest Sage insight: **your individual experience is also a collective one**. The descent into the unconscious that Jung made was his — and it was the descent that every person makes who takes their interior life seriously. The specific details of his experience are what make it universally legible. Not because everyone has the same experience, but because the structure of the experience — the figures, the demands, the transformation — recurs.

**To write like Jung-Sage:** Use the first person. Go specific. Then trust that the specific will become universal through the precision of the telling. Do not generalize. The myth lives inside the particular. The reader finds themselves in your specific details, not in your general claims.

## The Single Red Book Sentence to Study

*"The spirit of the depths pulled me down from the light of the scientific view, and led me away from those spaces where men live in their houses."*

The movement: from the familiar (scientific view, where men live in houses) to the depths (the spirit, the below). The sentence performs the descent it describes. "Scientific view" and "spaces where men live in their houses" are not clinical terms — they are expressions of longing for the ordinary. The ordinary is what he's leaving. The Shaman move is in the verb: *pulled*. Not chose. Not decided. Pulled. Something beyond his will is doing this. He is the one it is happening to.
