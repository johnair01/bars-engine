# Voice Analysis — Medium Corpus (8 Essays, 2015–2019)
**Sources:** `The Library/08 Source Library/` — 8 Medium essays by Wendell Bernard Britt Jr.
**Purpose:** Extract voice patterns, structural moves, and register markers from pre-MTGOA writing to inform manuscript editing

---

## Why This Corpus Matters

These essays are the earliest recoverable examples of Wendell writing publicly. They predate the MTGOA framework, the Integral map, and the Six Faces — which means what survives here is *structural*, not borrowed from the book's architecture. The moves that appear across all eight pieces are load-bearing. They are what Wendell's voice does naturally, before he had a vocabulary for it.

The contrast with the book voice is also instructive. The Medium essays are **advocacy writing** — arguing from the outside looking in at injustice. The book argues **from inside a framework looking out toward possibility**. The voice matures from combative/defensive to pedagogical/generous, but the underlying moves are identical. Knowing this tells us what to preserve in the manuscript and what the book sometimes suppresses.

---

## 1. Opening Moves

Every piece opens with one of three gambits:

### A. The Bold Claim Hook
> "This morning I entertained the thought that I might catch a bullet for running through my neighborhood."
> *(Black Where We Don't Belong)*

Stakes declared at the sentence level. No warmup. The reader is already inside the problem.

### B. In Medias Res — Fiction or Scene
> "Githre the Bard was having a very bad day."
> *(Roleplaying as Shadow Work)*

> "Assistant Director Moses Baldwin here!"
> *(Welcome to the Real World)*

Drops into story mid-motion. The argument arrives later, earned by the narrative.

### C. The Confessional Disclosure
> "I realize that I've been holding something back in my messaging about Allyship."
> *(I Don't Believe in Justice)*

Opens with what's been suppressed. The piece becomes an act of revealing.

**Voice note:** All three gambits do the same thing — they skip the preamble and create immediate forward motion. The reader has no choice but to follow. The book sometimes loses this by opening chapters with framing rather than pull. The Medium essays never earn the reader; they assume the reader is already running with them.

---

## 2. The Surface/Deep Structure

The most recurring structural move across the corpus — appears in at least 5 of 8 pieces:

> "I wanted what all recently graduated, overeducated millennials wanted... At least this is what I told people on the surface level. Deep down, I just wanted to feel as though life were worth living."
> *(Backstory)*

> "I've been getting a lot of credit for being someone who 'fights the good fight'... But that's not what I did it for."
> *(I Don't Believe in Justice)*

> "I'd love to say the impetus was out of a simple desire to do good but as a black man in America at least 30% of this energy is spent on survival."
> *(Black Where We Don't Belong)*

**The pattern:** State the socially acceptable version → then reveal the real version underneath. This is shadow work as a writing structure. The piece enacts the same move it's teaching. This is the most load-bearing pattern in the corpus and it maps directly onto the MTGOA "disowned self" framework — which makes sense. The essays are the origin point of the framework, not just an example of it.

**In the book:** This move appears, but it tends to be used in vignettes rather than in the author's own voice. The manuscript would be stronger if Wendell applied this move to himself in the text — not just to fictional characters.

---

## 3. Parentheticals as Voice Carrier

The parenthetical is Wendell's most distinctive micro-level voice move. It appears in every single piece and carries at least four distinct functions:

### A. Humor/Wit that doesn't undermine the argument
> "(*laughing forever*)" — on Obama winning without the white male vote
> "(black-lash?)" — on the Boyega backlash
> "White Savior™" — the trademark sign doing the work of an entire critique

### B. Self-correction or self-awareness
> "(and the alcohol from last night)"
> "(I've since changed my tune on that subject, but more on that later)"

### C. The aside that sharpens the claim
> "(see: men)" — on "audiences" who didn't like Rey
> "(actually, they were all portrayed by one actor of Maori descent)" — mid-argument correction that completely reverses the premise

### D. The meta-acknowledge
> "(remind you of anyone?)" — comparing Anakin to Rey mid-argument

**Voice note:** The parenthetical is where Wendell is most alive on the page. It's the register of someone thinking out loud in front of you — not performing, not lecturing, but genuinely working something out and willing to let you see the seams. The book uses parentheticals, but less frequently and less pointedly. When the book loses aliveness, it's often because the parenthetical voice has been replaced by clean prose that's more polished but less present.

---

## 4. ALL CAPS as Spoken-Word Emphasis

Strategic, not decorative. Used at moments of actual spoken emphasis — what would be a raised voice or deliberate pause:

> "YOU DON'T LIKE STAR WARS." — declarative verdict
> "MUST understand" — the word the speaker would stress
> "PROBABLY wouldn't have anything happen to me" — ironic self-awareness of the absurdity
> "SLOOOOW" — extended vowel, comedic emphasis
> "PUNCHY" — single word, self-characterization

**Voice note:** This is the most unambiguous marker that Wendell writes the way he talks. The ALL CAPS marks where his spoken voice would break from conversational into emphatic. It never appears in the manuscript (appropriately — it's a different register), but the spoken quality it represents should still be present in the prose rhythm. When a sentence in the manuscript needs this kind of force, the ALL CAPS equivalent is word order and sentence length — the short declarative after a long one.

---

## 5. The Numbered Setup → Subversion

Appears in at least three pieces as a rhetorical structure:

> "1) She's too powerful at the outset of the movie / 2) She doesn't earn how powerful she becomes"
> [Spends rest of essay dismantling both]
> *(In Defense of Rey)*

> "1) You don't like Star Wars / 2) You don't like women."
> [Uses this as a forcing function — if you disagree with 1, you're stuck with 2]
> *(In Defense of Rey)*

> "1) Emotional Trauma / 2) The New Minority / 3) Ask For Help / 4) Playing The Blame Game"
> [Structures the entire piece as an orientation document]
> *(Welcome to the Real World)*

**Voice note:** The numbered list signals that Wendell is about to be organized and clear — then uses that clarity as a trap. The reader expects balanced treatment of each item; instead they get a rhetorical funnel. This is the Architect face doing the Challenger's work. The book uses numbered lists but tends toward genuine balance (all items treated equally). The Medium essays are willing to use structure as a weapon.

---

## 6. Pop Culture as the Vehicle for Serious Argument

The single most consistent thematic move across the corpus:

- Star Wars (Boyega) → representation in speculative fiction
- D&D (Githre) → shadow work / Jungian psychology
- D&D (general) → human development frameworks
- Star Wars (Rey) → female heroism / gender bias
- *Dope* (film) → Hero's Journey as black narrative form
- Star Wars (franchise) → the game of allyship vs. the war narrative

**Voice note:** This is not decoration. Pop culture is the door Wendell uses to get people inside an argument they would resist if framed as academic or political. "Let's talk about why John Boyega matters" gets more readers into a conversation about representation than "let's talk about structural racism in the media." The MTGOA book does this with game frames — but the Medium essays show the move in its original, pre-framework form. The underlying logic is identical: find what the audience already loves, then use it as the delivery mechanism for the real payload.

## 7. The Anticipatory Objection

Wendell hears the counterargument before it arrives and addresses it in-voice:

> "I can already hear it. 'But if race doesn't matter, why are you making a big deal about the representation of races in these narratives?'"
> *(Whose Fantasy?)*

> "To those people, I would say..."
> *(Whose Fantasy?)*

> "I know all the literary folk out there are sick to death of hearing about the Hero's journey, so you'll have to excuse my excitement..."
> *(Dope)*

**Voice note:** This move acknowledges that the reader exists and has a position — which is itself an act of respect. It also controls the conversation by getting to the objection first, on the writer's terms. The book uses this less frequently; it tends toward declarative statements without pre-empting pushback. Adding this move in revision would make the manuscript feel more like a live conversation.

---

## 8. The "Let Me Be Direct" Escalation

Multiple pieces include a moment where the register shifts from conversational to authoritative — a deliberate gear change:

> "Let me make myself a little more clear on the subject. I repeat, if you are on board with mediocre Luke Skywalker... but aren't down with Rey you are a straight up old school... brand sexist. FULL STOP."
> *(In Defense of Rey)*

> "And the rule of law in America is, 'If you're black, be who we want you to be, and be where we expect you to be.'"
> *(Black Where We Don't Belong)*

> "Fiction is formative. Our reality is shaped by the stories we are told."
> *(Whose Fantasy?)*

**Voice note:** These are the moments where Wendell stops reasoning and declares. Two or three words. Subject-verb. No qualifier. The prose switches from thinking out loud to handing down a verdict. These sentences are the ones readers screenshot. The book has versions of this, but they're sometimes buried in longer paragraphs. These belong at the end of paragraphs, on their own line, earning white space.

---

## 9. The Confession-Credential Move

Wendell regularly establishes his stake in the argument by confessing something unflattering about himself first:

> "I'll be the first to turn my nose at a medieval story with a black protagonist."
> *(Whose Fantasy?)*

> "I've been getting a lot of credit for being someone who 'fights the good fight'... But that's not what I did it for."
> *(I Don't Believe in Justice)*

> "I'd never had any interest in playing a warlock before."
> *(Roleplaying as Shadow Work)*

> "I'd have to be an insane person to take on stakes that high..."
> *(I Don't Believe in Justice)*

**Voice note:** The confession pre-empts the accusation. If Wendell says something unflattering about himself first, the reader can't use it against him — and it also establishes that he's not a true believer performing for an audience. He's working something out. This is what the book's WENDELL_VOICE_AGENT_GUIDE calls "shadow without shame." The Medium essays show where that phrase comes from: it's not a principle, it's a move Wendell has been making since 2015.

---

## 10. Closing Moves

The endings are quiet, image-based, or pointed:

> "I arrived at the stop just in time to watch my bus pull away. I wonder if anyone else missed their bus today."
> *(Black Where We Don't Belong)*

> "There has been an awakening in our collective imaginations, and you can be damn well sure I've felt it."
> *(Whose Fantasy?)*

> "Develop the Gamer, Develop the Game, Develop the world."
> *(Backstory)*

> "By the end of his adventures, it was Githre's ability to master the darkness within himself that allowed him to help save the world."
> *(Roleplaying as Shadow Work)*

**Voice note:** Almost never a summary. The endings either return to the opening image (the bus), land on a declarative (the three-part Develop line), or close the fictional frame. The book chapters sometimes end with summaries that explain what just happened. These endings trust the reader to carry the weight. The image or the declaration — the reader supplies the meaning.

## What This Corpus Is vs. What the Book Is

| Medium Essays | MTGOA Manuscript |
|---------------|------------------|
| Advocacy — arguing from outside | Pedagogy — teaching from inside |
| Combative register | Generous register |
| Pop culture as door into argument | Game frame as door into framework |
| Confessional, personal staking | Vignettes, third-person examples |
| Parentheticals frequent, sharp | Parentheticals present but controlled |
| ALL CAPS spoken-word emphasis | Sentence structure carries emphasis |
| Ending in image or verdict | Sometimes ending in summary |
| The reader as opponent to convince | The reader as student to equip |

**The key insight:** The voice matures but doesn't change. The Medium essays are the book's voice under pressure. When the book loses aliveness, it's because the adversarial pressure has been removed — and with it, the parenthetical wit, the confessional staking, and the "FULL STOP" moments. The book doesn't need to be combative. But it needs to feel like the person who wrote these essays is still in the room.

---

## Recommendations for Manuscript Editing

1. **Add confessional moments in Wendell's own voice** — not just in vignettes. At least once per chapter, Wendell should say something unflattering about himself before making his point.

2. **Restore the parentheticals** — the book's parentheticals should be doing sharper work. If a parenthetical is just clarifying, cut it. If it can be witty, let it be witty.

3. **Add anticipatory objections** — especially in Ch0 and Ch1, where the ideal reader is most resistant. Hear her objection before she raises it.

4. **Let the closing sentences breathe** — the ending of each chapter should be an image or a two-word verdict, not a summary paragraph. Cut everything after the last real sentence.

5. **Restore the pop culture pivot** — the book has it, but it's more controlled. Let Star Wars into the room more often. The reader Wendell is writing for grew up on the same movies.
