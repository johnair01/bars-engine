# Chapter Goals, Capacity Milestones, and BAR Evidence
**Core insight:** BARs are not just capture moments — they are structured evidence that a specific capacity has increased. The gate BARs are the data trail. The milestone is the destination. When a player has enough gate BARs pointing toward the milestone, they know they've built the capacity.

---

## The Architecture

Each chapter has three layers:

1. **Orienting Goal** — the capacity the chapter is building toward. Orients every gate encounter and every hexagram card in that chapter.

2. **Capacity Milestone** — the verifiable marker that tells a player the capacity is present. Not a feeling of completion — a specific behavioral event that could only happen if the capacity is real. Observable. Capturable as a BAR.

3. **Milestone BAR Template** — what the milestone looks like in BAR format. This is the "final boss" BAR for the chapter. When a player captures this, they have evidence.

**The gate BARs build toward the milestone.** Eight gate encounters, eight data points. Accumulate them and the milestone becomes possible. The milestone BAR is the moment the capacity fires in a real situation — outside the book, in the world.

## Chapter 0 — Infinite Arcade

**Orienting Goal:**
Recognize that the game of allyship as currently designed is unwinnable — and that the problem is the game, not the player.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You can name the specific rules you were following that were keeping you stuck — not as a general critique of "allyship culture," but your particular version of the unwinnable game. What you were trying to win. What winning would have looked like. Why it was never going to be enough.

*You know you're there when:* Someone describes the allyship game they're exhausted by and you can say — specifically, not generically — what game they're playing and why it can't be won from inside its own rules.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Named your version of the unwinnable game out loud — to yourself or to someone else
- **A:** Felt the specific quality of relief that comes from naming the game rather than trying harder at it
- **R:** What becomes possible when the problem is the game and not your effort

## Chapter 1 — The Forest

**Orienting Goal:**
Recognize that the inner forest walk is the prerequisite for all outward allyship work — you cannot show up for someone else's center if you are still terrified of your own.

**Chapter polarity (integral design 2026-05-25):** **Forest ↔ Village** — see `POLARITY-TEACHING-GRAMMAR.md`, `GOVERNING-POLARITIES-REGISTER.md` GP-A-Ch1.

**Chapter action item** *(not a capacity milestone — Ch1 orients; it does not prove gate mastery):*

You can **acknowledge the difference** between allyship in the **village** and allyship in the **forest** — in your own words, with at least one example from your life where you were doing village allyship (performance, optics, public moves) when the work actually required forest allyship (inner map, parts, pause).

*You know you're there when:* You can name both places without collapsing them — forest is not "good" and village "bad"; you see **healthy and shadow** forms of each, and you know which kind of allyship you were practicing in a specific moment.

**Action item BAR** *(Ch1 — capture optional, not milestone-unlock tier):*
- **B:** Named the forest/village distinction out loud or in writing — one real example of village allyship vs what forest allyship would have asked for
- **A:** What shifted when you saw them as two different kinds of allyship, not one ladder
- **R:** What you will watch for next time before defaulting to village moves

## Chapter 2 — Shaman

**Orienting Goal:**
Develop the capacity to read emotional pattern — in yourself, in others, in the room — before any allyship move is made.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You can feel the room before you speak. You've caught yourself starting to act from strategy and paused to read the emotional field first. You know which emotional channel is alive in a situation — not from analysis but from somatic sensing — before you make your move.

*You know you're there when:* You walk into a hard conversation and your first move is to feel rather than to plan. And the feeling changes what you do.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Felt the emotional field in a real situation before speaking — named the channel (fear, anger, grief, joy, or neutrality) from the body, not from analysis — and let that reading change your move
- **A:** The felt difference between reading-then-moving versus planning-then-executing
- **R:** What the emotional read made possible that the strategy wouldn't have

## Chapter 3 — Challenger

**Orienting Goal:**
Develop the capacity to hold a line from feeling rather than strategy — the clean no that protects without cutting.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You have said no to something that cost you something, and it came from a felt sense of what mattered rather than a rehearsed script. The no held. You did not apologize it away, soften it into a maybe, or abandon it when it made someone uncomfortable.

*You know you're there when:* You can feel the difference between a no that comes from fear (the Protector's no, the Fixer's no-as-intervention) and a no that comes from what you actually stand for. The clean no has a different texture. You've felt both.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Said no to something that cost you something — not as strategy, not as script — from the felt sense of what mattered
- **A:** What the clean no felt like in the body — distinct from the managed no, the apologetic no, the no-that-was-really-a-maybe
- **R:** What held because of the no that would have dissolved without it

## Chapter 4 — Regent

**Orienting Goal:**
Develop the capacity to carry something worth belonging to across time — to steward without owning, to hold without hoarding.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You have made a decision or held a space that prioritized the institution, community, or relationship over your own position in it. You have passed something forward. The thing continued without needing you to remain in control.

*You know you're there when:* You can feel the difference between holding something (Regent) and owning it (the shadow Regent who can't let go). You've felt the exhale of releasing something you'd been gripping — and the thing didn't collapse.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Made a decision or took an action that served the continuity of something at the cost of your own position, comfort, or recognition within it
- **A:** What it felt like to release the grip — not resignation, but active passing-forward
- **R:** What continued, or what became possible, because you didn't hold on

## Chapter 5 — Architect

**Orienting Goal:**
Develop the capacity to design structures that make the right thing easy for whoever comes next — with or without you in the room.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You have designed or redesigned something — a process, a meeting, a practice, a system — so that it runs without your presence. Someone else can operate it. The design makes the right thing the easy thing.

*You know you're there when:* You can catch yourself designing for your own indispensability (the Architect's shadow) versus designing for your own obsolescence. You've felt the difference. You've built something you could leave.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Designed or handed off something that now runs without you — intentionally, not through abandonment
- **A:** What structural generosity feels like — the specific quality of building something for the next person rather than for your own legacy
- **R:** What the next person can do because the structure exists

## Chapter 6 — Diplomat

**Orienting Goal:**
Develop the capacity to hold multiple valid perspectives without collapsing the tension — to create conditions where real things can happen.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You have stayed in a room where two valid but opposed perspectives were in conflict, held space for both without forcing resolution, and the tension moved toward something real rather than being managed into false agreement.

*You know you're there when:* You can feel the difference between holding a field (Diplomat) and managing a field (the shadow Diplomat who needs everyone to be okay). You've held a conversation that was genuinely uncomfortable and not rescued it from the discomfort.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Held space for a genuine conflict without collapsing it into resolution — let the tension do its work
- **A:** What field-holding feels like versus field-managing — the specific somatic difference
- **R:** What became possible in that space because you didn't rush to resolve it

## Chapter 7 — Sage

**Orienting Goal:**
Develop the capacity to see the whole game without leaving it — altitude awareness in service of showing up more precisely.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You have named an altitude mismatch in a real situation — identified that a group was treating a Green problem as a Red problem, or a Teal problem as a Green problem — and the naming changed something. Not from above the game, but from inside it.

*You know you're there when:* You can see which altitude a conversation is stuck at, name it without lecturing, and offer a move that meets people where they are rather than where you think they should be.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Named an altitude mismatch in a real situation — what the group was treating the problem as versus what it actually was — and the naming opened a new move
- **A:** What committed seeing feels like — seeing clearly without detaching, maintaining altitude awareness while staying in the game
- **R:** What became possible because the mismatch was named

## Chapter 8 — Player

**Orienting Goal:**
Compose your own allyship practice from all six Faces — design a game that outlives your direct involvement.

**Capacity Milestone:**
You have run more than one Face in a single allyship situation, choosing from the toolkit rather than defaulting to your home Face. You have named your practice — the specific combination of Faces that is yours — and you have begun to hand it on.

*You know you're there when:* You catch yourself reaching for the wrong Face (the one you always use) and consciously choose the Face the moment actually requires. You feel the difference.

**Milestone BAR:**
- **B:** Chose a Face deliberately — not the one you default to, but the one the moment required — and made the move from that Face
- **A:** What it feels like to play from choice rather than from default — the specific quality of self-authorship in a real situation
- **R:** What the situation could hold because you brought the right Face rather than the familiar one

## How Gate BARs Build Toward the Milestone

Each chapter's 8 gate BARs are evidence accumulation. The gate BAR captures a specific internal encounter. The milestone BAR captures an external event — the capacity firing in the world.

**The logic:**
- Gate BARs = internal evidence (this gate did something, this is what it cost or opened)
- Milestone BAR = external evidence (the capacity is present — here is where it showed up in a real situation)

A player who has captured all 8 gate BARs in a chapter has met every interior voice that guards the capacity. The milestone BAR is the moment those encounters became usable in the world.

**The app can track this:** When a player has X gate BARs from a chapter, the app can surface the milestone prompt — "You've walked this forest 8 times. Here's what you're now ready to name in the world."

## Design Decisions (resolved 2026-05-19)

**1. Milestone BAR placement:** Appears in BOTH the book (at the end of the chapter, after Section 7) AND the app (triggered after 8 unique gate BARs are captured from that chapter).

**1b. Ch1 action item vs capacity milestone (resolved 2026-05-25 — integral design):** Ch1 uses a **chapter action item** only (forest/village distinction acknowledged). Gate-catch-in-real-moment milestone is **book-level**, not Ch1. See Ch1 section.

**2. Gate BAR prompt placement:** End of each gate section — after the gate's closing prose, before the section divider.

**3. Repeat gate BARs:** Repeat captures do NOT count toward milestone unlock. Each gate slot fills once (first capture). However, in a future skill-tracking system, repeat gate BARs would award 1 XP toward the relevant skills — the infrastructure should be built with this in mind even if the XP system isn't live yet.

**4. Ch0 — BAR Introduction:** Ch0 does not use the standard gate walk. Its role in the BAR system is to introduce BARs as a technology — the reader's first explanation of what a BAR is, how to capture one, and what the app does with it. No gate BARs in Ch0; one introductory BAR prompt that initiates the practice.

**5. Ch8 — CTA BARs (needs own spec):** Ch8 does not use the standard gate walk format. Its BARs are **CTA BARs** — calls to action that route players to their next step in their allyship journey. Not gate captures; not milestone evidence. Each CTA BAR should route the player toward a specific next action (a quest, a campaign, a practice, a community) based on which self-authorship mode (Cartographer, Designer, Founder, Elder, Outlaw) is most alive for them. Ch8 BAR spec to be written separately.
