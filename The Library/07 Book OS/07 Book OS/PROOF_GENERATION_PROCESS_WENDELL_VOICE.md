# Proof Generation Process — Wendell Voice

## Why this exists

The book now has five proof burdens:

- narrative proof
- poetic/metaphoric proof
- practice proof
- source proof
- allyship application proof

What it does **not** yet have, unless we make it explicit, is a **Wendell-native process** for generating those proofs.

Without a process, "proof" drifts into:

- teaching mode
- framework explanation
- citation theater
- examples that sound correct but do not feel lived

This document exists to prevent that.

The job is not to sound more academic.
The job is to make the reader say:

- "yes, that's true"
- "yes, I've done that"
- "yes, I can see where this lives in me"
- "yes, I can see what this changes in allyship"

That is proof in this book.

---

## Core rule

In MTGOA, proof is not:

- "I explained it clearly"

Proof is:

- the reader recognized it
- the reader felt the cost of not seeing it
- the reader was given a move to test it
- the reader could see what changed in relationship

If the reader only understands the concept, the chapter is still underproven.

---

## Wendell proof sequence

Every polarity chapter should try to generate proof in this order:

1. **Recognition**
2. **Pressure**
3. **Image**
4. **Move**
5. **Allyship consequence**
6. **Source support**

That order matters.

Do not begin with the source.
Do not begin with the framework.
Do not begin with the definition.

Begin with the place where the reader has already lived the problem.

---

## Step 1 — Name the exact thing the reader resists

Before generating any proof, answer:

- what is the chapter asking the reader to believe?
- what will the ideal reader resist about that?
- what will they misname?
- what shadow are they likely to confuse for the healthy pole?

Examples:

- Ch2: "probably my stuff" mistaken for discernment
- Ch3: politeness mistaken for care, charge mistaken for integrity

If this step is skipped, the proof will be generic.

---

## Step 2 — Generate narrative proof

### What narrative proof does

Narrative proof makes the tension happen to someone.

Not in theory.
In a room.
In a call.
In a body.
In a relationship.

The reader needs to watch the problem occur before they are asked to understand it.

### Wendell rule for narrative proof

Use a moment where:

- something real was at stake
- the wrong move looked reasonable
- the cost of the wrong move was visible
- the better move was not obvious at first

That is what makes the story proof instead of decoration.

### Narrative proof prompt

Ask:

- where did this tension actually happen?
- what did I do first that didn't work?
- what was I trying to protect?
- what did it cost me, them, or the room?
- what became visible only after the failed move?

### Narrative proof failure modes

Do not write:

- a story that only flatters the framework
- a story where the lesson was obvious from the start
- a story that turns the narrator into the hero too early

If the story makes you look right from the first line, it is probably not doing proof work.

---

## Step 3 — Generate poetic/metaphoric proof

### What poetic/metaphoric proof does

Poetic/metaphoric proof bridges the gap between prose and lived recognition.

It does **not** claim direct access to the reader's body.
It gives the reader an image they can recognize themselves inside.

This is one of the primary ways the book proves things.

### Wendell rule for poetic/metaphoric proof

Find the image that tells the truth faster than the explanation.

Examples of the kind of move this book already knows how to make:

- a hand on a lid
- a room getting colder
- the line disappearing
- a wound taking up the whole room
- a voice standing at the gate

These are not decorations.
They are proof bridges.

### Poetic/metaphoric proof prompt

Ask:

- what image carries the tension cleanly?
- what object, terrain, weather, motion, or room dynamic tells the truth here?
- what phrase would make the reader say "yes, that's exactly what that is"?

### Poetic/metaphoric proof failure modes

Do not write:

- vague therapeutic atmosphere
- ornate language with no diagnostic value
- body-certainty language that pretends to verify the reader's somatics from the page

The image has to clarify the tension, not perfume it.

---

## Step 4 — Generate practice proof

### What practice proof does

Practice proof answers:

- can the reader test this?

The chapter has not proven a distinction if the reader cannot try it.

### Wendell rule for practice proof

A practice should:

- be usable in a real moment
- help the reader distinguish one pole from its shadow
- produce a different move, not just a better description

### Practice proof prompt

Ask:

- what is the smallest repeatable move that lets the reader test this distinction?
- what would someone do differently in the next thirty seconds?
- what would someone do differently in a slower, reflective setting?
- what mistake does the practice prevent?

### Practice proof failure modes

Do not write:

- practices that only rehearse the idea
- practices that sound wise but do not change behavior
- practices that require total transformation before first use

If the practice does not alter the next move, it is not proof yet.

---

## Step 5 — Generate allyship application proof

### What allyship application proof does

This is one of the book's non-negotiables.

The chapter has to show what the distinction changes in:

- a meeting
- a conflict
- a repair attempt
- a moment of harm
- a room where power is alive

Without this, the material remains self-development content wearing allyship clothes.

### Wendell rule for allyship proof

Do not save allyship for the end.
The chapter should keep showing:

- how this distortion appears in allyship
- how this healthy capacity appears in allyship
- what goes wrong when the chapter's lesson is missing
- what becomes possible when the lesson is present

### Allyship proof prompt

Ask:

- where does this show up when someone is trying to be a good ally?
- what does the shadow look like in an allyship room?
- what does the healthy pole look like in an allyship room?
- what sentence would the reader say differently?
- what would they interrupt, hold, ask, name, refuse, or stay with differently?

### Allyship proof failure modes

Do not write:

- generic interpersonal examples with no power differential
- examples where the reader is only managing themselves
- examples where allyship is added as a moral label after the real example is over

The allyship relevance must be structural, not decorative.

---

## Step 6 — Generate source proof

### What source proof does

Source proof is not there to impress.
It is there to steady the reader at the exact point where they might otherwise dismiss the distinction.

### Wendell rule for source proof

Source proof should come last.

Only add it when:

- the chapter has already made the tension legible
- the reader can already recognize the pattern
- an external source would help them trust the distinction enough to test it

### Source proof prompt

Ask:

- where is the reader likely to say "this just sounds like your preference"?
- what work, lineage, or adjacent framework would reassure without flattening?
- does the source deepen trust, or does it replace living voice with borrowed authority?

### Source proof failure modes

Do not write:

- citation dump
- bibliography as costume
- scholarly thoroughness that kills reading rhythm

In this book, source proof is a support beam, not a wallpaper pattern.

## The five proof types, in Wendell voice

## 1. Narrative proof

**Question:** Where did this happen for real?

**Voice test:** Does the scene feel reported, not explained?

**Good sign:** The reader can feel the cost before the interpretation arrives.

## 2. Poetic/metaphoric proof

**Question:** What image tells the truth faster than the explanation?

**Voice test:** Does the image carry diagnostic value?

**Good sign:** The reader recognizes themselves without the book claiming certainty about their interior.

## 3. Practice proof

**Question:** What can the reader do to test this now?

**Voice test:** Does the move sound like a real move, not curriculum language?

**Good sign:** The reader's next action changes.

## 4. Allyship application proof

**Question:** What does this change in a room with other people, power, and risk?

**Voice test:** Does the example feel like actual allyship, not generic ethics?

**Good sign:** The reader can picture the sentence they would say differently.

## 5. Source proof

**Question:** Where does the reader need backup to trust the move?

**Voice test:** Does the source strengthen the chapter without hijacking its voice?

**Good sign:** The source confirms a living distinction instead of replacing it.

---

## The proof ladder for a chapter

When building or revising a chapter, use this ladder:

1. **Make the reader recognize the pattern**
2. **Show the cost of missing it**
3. **Give the pattern an image**
4. **Give the reader a move**
5. **Show what changes in allyship**
6. **Add source support only where needed**

If a chapter jumps from 1 to 6, it will feel overexplained and underlived.

---

## Proof-map questions

When writing a proof map, ask these for each section:

- what is already proven here?
- what is only asserted here?
- what image is carrying the section?
- what move does the reader get here?
- what allyship consequence is visible here?
- does this section need outside support, or just better internal architecture?

---

## Voice lint for proof generation

If the prose starts sounding like any of these, stop:

- "this demonstrates"
- "what this means is"
- "the key takeaway is"
- "research shows" too early
- examples that exist only to illustrate a framework
- moral conclusions that arrive before the scene has earned them

Better questions:

- what happened?
- what was the cost?
- what was the move?
- what changed in the room?

---

## Proof generation workflow for each chapter

Use this sequence:

1. Name the claim the chapter is making
2. Name what the reader will resist
3. Find the real scene
4. Find the image that carries it
5. Write the live move
6. Show the allyship consequence
7. Only then add source support

That is the generation algorithm.

Not:

- define
- explain
- cite
- summarize

This book earns trust the other way around.

---

## Bottom line

In Wendell's voice, proof is not:

- argument first

It is:

- encounter
- recognition
- move
- consequence
- then support

If the chapter can do that, the polarity does not need to be sold.
The reader will already know it is true.
