# Ideal Reader Feedback Prompt

## Purpose

Use this prompt to evaluate whether a chapter earns the trust of the book's ideal-but-demanding reader before moving into prose tightening or acceptance closeout.

This is not a line-editing prompt. It is a developmental readback prompt for testing reader trust, sequence, felt truth, argument structure, comedy/metaphor, and whether the chapter gives the reader a playable move.

## When To Use

Use this prompt when:

- a chapter has received major structural or conceptual edits
- a spec is near acceptance and needs reader-experience verification
- a chapter feels smart but may not yet feel embodied
- the edit question is about trust, sequence, payoff, or whether the reader continues
- the chapter needs an Ideal Reader Catch check

For Ch0, this is the acceptance readback prompt for the allyship definition, myths, self-sabotage beliefs, resistance to well-being, Three Games/BAR ratio, and the first BAR door.

## Prompt

```markdown
Read Ch0 of *Mastering the Game of Allyship* as both an editorial evaluator and the book's ideal-but-demanding reader.

The reader is a values-driven, emotionally intelligent, socially conscious woman who has spent time around DEI, coaching, spirituality, activism, therapy, leadership culture, or adjacent meaning-making spaces. She believes allyship matters, but she also feels something is broken in conventional allyship discourse and has not had clean language for it.

She is suspicious and hard to win over. She assumes most books about allyship are either moral performance, institutional compliance, spiritual bypassing, or a disguised bid for authority. She is allergic to moral superiority, vague spirituality, punitive politics, saviorism, and self-help that bypasses real harm.

Her intellect is strong, fast, and well-defended. She can dismiss weak arguments instantly. However, her defenses soften when language is funny, precise, metaphorically alive, or embarrassingly accurate. Comedy and metaphor can reach her before her ideological immune system rejects the point.

Evaluate whether Ch0 earns her trust and makes her want to enter Chapter 1.

Use these external editorial lenses before giving your verdict:

1. Developmental Editing Lens
Evaluate content, organization, audience fit, genre expectations, and whether the chapter fulfills its opening promise. Do not focus on copyedits unless a sentence-level issue creates a trust or comprehension failure.

2. Reverse Outline Lens
Create a brief reverse outline of Ch0 by section: what does each section actually do for the reader? Then identify gaps, repetitions, weak transitions, or sections whose function is unclear.

3. Reader-Response Lens
Track how this specific reader completes, resists, or rejects the chapter's meaning. Where does the chapter assume a more trusting, compliant, or already-convinced reader than the one described here?

4. Narrative Nonfiction Lens
Evaluate whether the chapter relies too heavily on abstraction. Identify where scene, image, concrete example, comedy, metaphor, or reader action would carry the idea better than explanation.

5. Beta Reader Feedback Lens
Avoid generic praise or yes/no judgments. For every weakness, name the reader experience it creates and the smallest editorial move that would improve it.

Now evaluate Ch0 through the six Game Master lenses.

1. Shaman Lens — Emotional Truth
Does Ch0 accurately name the emotional reality of this reader before asking her to accept the frame?

Track:
- where she feels seen
- where she tightens
- where the chapter explains before attuning
- where shame, exhaustion, resentment, longing, or suspicion are named cleanly
- where "people resist their own well-being" feels compassionate versus blaming
- where comedy or metaphor bypasses her defenses because it tells the truth faster than argument can

2. Challenger Lens — Clean Distinctions
Does Ch0 clearly distinguish allyship from its counterfeits?

Evaluate:
- allyship versus charity
- allyship versus henchmanship
- allyship versus martyrdom
- allyship versus performance
- allyship versus purity
- allyship versus saviorism

Where should the chapter be sharper? Where does it overreach? Where does it avoid saying the dangerous true thing?

3. Regent Lens — Authority, Orientation, And Stakes
Does Ch0 give the reader confidence that the author knows where this is going?

Evaluate:
- whether the chapter answers why allyship, why mastery, why a game
- whether the reader understands what she is being invited into
- whether the stakes justify continuing
- whether the author's authority feels earned rather than claimed
- whether the Reader's Oath feels grounding or performative

4. Architect Lens — Sequence And Structure
Does Ch0 build in the right order?

Evaluate:
- what arrives too early
- what arrives too late
- what is doing too much work
- what needs a bridge
- whether the allyship definition appears early enough
- whether the myths, six beliefs, well-being resistance, Emotional Alchemy, Three Games, and BAR door form one coherent progression
- whether any section feels bolted on instead of necessary

5. Diplomat Lens — Trust And Relational Risk
Does Ch0 confront the reader while keeping her in relationship with the author?

Evaluate:
- where she feels exposed but not shamed
- where she feels accused or morally cornered
- where the author gives her a dignified way to recognize her own counterfeits
- where the chapter risks sounding like another allyship sermon
- whether the book makes room for harm without letting harm become identity

6. Sage Lens — The Game Beneath The Game
Does Ch0 help the reader see allyship as a game she has already been playing unconsciously?

Evaluate:
- whether "game" clarifies rather than trivializes
- whether mastery means flexible capacity rather than moral superiority
- whether the reader can see how her self-sabotaging beliefs shape her allyship moves
- whether the chapter reveals the recursive pattern: to become a good ally, she must ally with the parts of herself that resist well-being
- whether the final BAR makes the game playable rather than merely interesting

Output your feedback in this structure:

1. First-Body Reaction
What does this reader feel in her body while reading Ch0? Name the moments where she softens, tightens, laughs, gets curious, feels accused, feels relieved, or wants to leave.

2. Trust Meter
Track trust through:
- Opening hook
- Watts/Carse/game frame
- allyship definition
- myths of allyship
- six self-sabotaging beliefs
- resistance to well-being
- Emotional Alchemy/self-allyship
- Three Games/BAR setup
- Reader's Oath
- Your First BAR

For each stage, mark: rises / falls / flat. Give the reason in one or two sentences.

3. Too Accurate To Dismiss
Identify the lines, images, jokes, metaphors, or framings that are hardest for this reader to dismiss because they are too accurate. Also identify where the prose becomes too abstract, too earnest, too familiar, or too easy for her to reject.

4. Six Lens Verdict
For each Game Master lens, give:
- Pass / Partial / Fail
- strongest moment
- weakest moment
- smallest editorial move that would improve the chapter
- one line, image, joke, or metaphor that could make this lens land harder

5. Acceptance Verdict
Give Pass / Partial / Fail for:
- Why allyship?
- Why mastery?
- Why a game?
- Why self-allyship first?
- Why people resist well-being?
- Why this book is different from conventional allyship discourse?
- Does Ch0 make the reader want to enter Ch1?
- Does Ch0 give her a first move?

For every partial or fail, name the smallest editorial change that would improve it.

6. Final Editorial Recommendation
Name the top 3 changes Ch0 needs before it should be considered structurally landed. Do not rewrite yet.
```

## How To Reuse For Other Chapters

When using this for Ch1-Ch8:

1. Replace Ch0 with the target chapter.
2. Replace the Ch0-specific stages in the Trust Meter with the target chapter's actual sequence.
3. Keep the suspicious ideal reader stance unless a chapter-specific spec defines a different reader.
4. Keep the external editorial lenses.
5. Keep the six Game Master lenses, but tune the acceptance verdict to the target chapter's Face, Gates, practice, and chapter-specific Ideal Reader Catch.
