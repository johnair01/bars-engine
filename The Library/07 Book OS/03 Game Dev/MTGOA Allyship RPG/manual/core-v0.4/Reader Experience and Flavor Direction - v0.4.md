# Reader Experience and Flavor Direction - v0.4

Status: product and manual design direction

Purpose: define what the book must feel like so a reader believes they bought something rich, playable, beautiful, and worth returning to.

## Core Promise

The reader should feel like they bought a manual for a hidden academy that might actually be real.

Not a workbook.

Not a corporate training tool wearing a cape.

Not a thin metaphor.

The book should feel like a field guide, school handbook, campaign engine, secret curriculum, and fantasy RPG manual braided together.

The emotional promise:

**You are not reading about allyship. You are being invited aboard a ship that trains people to practice it under pressure.**

## What The Buyer Needs To Feel

A buyer should feel five things within the first ten minutes:

1. **This is gorgeous.**
   - The book has visual gravity.
   - The school-ship, Houses, Schools, teachers, badges, maps, and artifacts feel specific.
   - The art makes the world feel larger than the text.

2. **This is playable.**
   - They can understand the core loop quickly.
   - Character creation feels concrete.
   - The moves look usable at a table.
   - The book does not require a trained facilitator to begin imagining play.

3. **This is secretly useful.**
   - The mechanics are fantasy-coded, but the reader can feel the real allyship curriculum underneath.
   - They learn without feeling lectured.
   - The game gives them language they can use outside the game.

4. **This has depth.**
   - The six Schools feel like real traditions.
   - The four Houses feel socially alive.
   - Advancement feels like a path, not a checklist.
   - Campaign packets feel expandable.

5. **This is mine now.**
   - The book invites annotation, character ideas, house loyalty, favorite teachers, ship rumors, and campaign dreams.
   - The reader starts imagining their student before they finish the rules.

## The Manual Should Feel Like

### A School Handbook

Use this for:

- House descriptions
- School pages
- teachers and lieutenants
- rules of the ship
- student creation
- advancement
- school trials

Texture:

- orientation language
- marginal notes from prior students
- house mottos
- exam warnings
- little institutional contradictions
- official language interrupted by lived experience

Example flavor:

"Students are reminded that the School of the Line is not authorized to conduct midnight boundary drills in the mess hall after last term's incident."

### A Field Guide

Use this for:

- Basic Moves
- stats
- tokens
- pressure clocks
- mission procedures
- consent and safety

Texture:

- clear tables
- quick references
- diagrams
- "when this happens, do this"
- examples from actual mission reports

Example flavor:

"If the room gets quieter after the truth is named, do not assume the move failed. Silence is sometimes the sound of the field recalibrating."

### A Secret Curriculum

Use this for:

- Schools
- ranks
- shadow exams
- self-sabotaging beliefs
- Growth
- Clarity
- Tension

Texture:

- poetic but precise
- initiatory language
- names for inner capacities
- warnings about shadow forms
- short teaching stories

Example flavor:

"The first mistake of Horizon students is believing distance is wisdom. The second is believing return is failure."

### A Campaign Engine

Use this for:

- campaign packets
- world generation
- mission setup
- factions
- clocks
- school spotlights
- downtime

Texture:

- modular procedures
- repeatable campaign templates
- maps and prompts
- card-like episode seeds
- GM tools that feel like play, not prep burden

Example flavor:

"Every world the ship visits has already tried to solve its own crisis. Your first job is to find out why the old solution became part of the problem."

## What To Avoid

Avoid making the book feel like:

- a DEI training binder
- a therapy workbook
- a generic PbtA hack
- a parody of Harry Potter
- a lore encyclopedia without procedures
- a rules document with fantasy nouns pasted on top
- an art book with no table utility
- a moral performance simulator

The game can be sincere, educational, and transformative, but it should never feel like it is asking the reader to prove they are good.

## Flavor Principles

## Specific Over Abstract

Bad:

"Students learn to create systemic change through awareness and accountability."

Better:

"In the Pattern rooms, students build model cities from glass, thread, and ash. If their solution saves the city but strands one person outside the gate, Head Irix breaks the model and makes them begin again."

## Magical, But Operational

The ship should feel wondrous, but the wonder should teach procedure.

Examples:

- The mess hall changes seating arrangements when a conversation is being avoided.
- The Line training rooms only open after a student names what they are protecting.
- The Bridge tea rooms pour bitter tea when someone is confusing peace with silence.
- The Pattern map tables show the person erased by a plan.
- The Horizon observatory refuses to focus unless the student names the next grounded act.
- The Body weather decks change pressure when the field is lying.

## Mechanics As Lore

Every major mechanic should have an in-world meaning.

| Mechanic | Flavor |
|---|---|
| Adversity | what failure taught you that success could not |
| Clarity | a clean line of sight through pressure |
| Tension | unresolved pressure asking to be named |
| Growth | impact metabolized into capacity |
| School Rank | deeper fluency in a discipline |
| House | practice lineage and social belonging |
| Basic Move | the kind of allyship moment you are in |
| Stat | the School discipline you are using |

## The Book Should Teach By Making The Reader Want To Play

The fantasy should carry the curriculum.

Instead of explaining allyship as a concept first, the manual should often present:

1. a charged fictional situation
2. a student instinct
3. a move trigger
4. a consequence
5. a short reflection

Example:

"You notice the village council keeps thanking the ship but never looking at the dockworkers. That is Wake Up. If you ask what the dockworkers know, roll +Sense. If you interrupt the ceremony to name the omission, roll +Speak or +Act, depending on whether you are revealing truth or stopping harm."

## Art Direction Jobs

The art assets should not merely decorate. They should make the game easier to understand and more desirable to own.

Needed art categories:

- full-page school-ship establishing image
- House crests
- School sigils
- Headmaster portrait
- six Heads of School portraits
- eighteen lieutenant portraits or spot illustrations
- student ensemble illustration
- ship map
- classroom / training room spreads
- mission world splash pages
- token icons: Adversity, Clarity, Tension, Growth
- rank badges for each School
- example character sheet with illustrated annotations
- episode card frame
- campaign packet cover template

Art should answer:

- Where am I?
- Who trains me?
- What do I belong to?
- What kind of danger is this game about?
- What does mastery look like?
- What does the table touch?

## Book Interface Features

To make the manual feel premium and usable:

- full-page chapter openers written as school documents
- margin notes from students, teachers, and the Headmaster
- sidebars titled "At The Table"
- sidebars titled "In The Fiction"
- quick-reference boxes after every major mechanic
- sample scenes after each Basic Move
- teacher quotes before School sections
- "Common Shadow" warnings for every stat and School
- printable handout pages
- campaign packet template pages
- icons for Houses, Schools, stats, and tokens

## The First 20 Pages Should Do This

The opening experience matters.

Recommended opening sequence:

1. Full-page image: the school-ship hidden above a world in crisis.
2. One-page invitation from the Headmaster.
3. What This Game Is.
4. What You Do In Play.
5. The Ship.
6. The Four Houses.
7. The Six Schools and their owned stats.
8. A short mission excerpt showing a roll.
9. What You Need To Play.
10. Safety and consent as ship protocol.

Do not open with long theory.

Open with the fantasy, then show the engine.

## Language Palette

Use words like:

- signal
- field
- line
- oath
- pattern
- bridge
- horizon
- pressure
- consent
- repair
- leverage
- witness
- handoff
- burden
- continuity
- return
- clarity
- consequence
- practice

Use carefully:

- allyship
- privilege
- oppression
- accountability
- trauma
- systems
- safety

These words matter, but if they dominate the surface, the manual starts to feel like training material instead of a game artifact.

## Reader Delight Objects

The book should include small things people want to point at.

Examples:

- "Which House would you be in?" spread
- "Which School keeps calling you?" spread
- Headmaster marginalia that is funny, evasive, or too honest
- teacher quotes that feel collectible
- ship rumor table
- student contraband table
- school trial generator
- failed mission report
- "do not do this at the table" comic strip
- map of a training deck
- rank badge sheet
- sample student record
- mission debrief form

These are not filler. They are how the artifact becomes lovable.

## Product Stack Implication

The core manual should make someone want more without feeling incomplete.

Core manual gives:

- the ship
- Houses
- Schools
- character creation
- core moves
- advancement
- pressure systems
- campaign construction tools
- one starter mission

Paid campaign packets give:

- new worlds
- mission arcs
- NPCs
- factions
- school spotlight paths
- episode cards
- custom moves
- new art
- printable handouts

The book sells the engine and the fantasy.

Campaigns sell fresh worlds to visit.

## Current Design North Star

When someone closes the book, they should think:

**I understand the game. I want to make a student. I know which House I would join. I am curious which School would challenge me. I can imagine paying for a campaign because I want to see where this ship goes next.**
