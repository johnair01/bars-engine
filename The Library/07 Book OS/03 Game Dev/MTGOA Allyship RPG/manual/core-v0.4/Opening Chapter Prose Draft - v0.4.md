Status: book prose draft

Purpose: begin translating the v0.4 engine into the actual voice of the manual.

## Page 1: Invitation From The Headmaster

You were not chosen because you were ready.

No one arrives ready.

The ship has taken in prodigies who could bend weather and still could not apologize. It has trained fugitives who could cross a battlefield under fire but could not stay in a room where someone told them the truth. It has welcomed heirs, dropouts, organizers, cowards, mystics, loudmouths, caretakers, liars, saints, and children who had already learned too much.

Readiness is not the price of admission.

Willingness is.

If this book has found you, then somewhere, in some world, help is about to become complicated. A community has already named what hurts. A council has already formed a committee. Someone has already written a speech. Someone has already been thanked instead of supported. Someone has already been asked to wait.

You will be tempted to arrive as the answer.

Do not.

The first lesson of this school is that help is not pure because you meant well. Help enters a field already crowded with memory, power, fear, debt, pride, need, and hope. A clean intention can still bruise. A brilliant plan can still strand the person it was meant to protect. A courageous speech can still turn someone else's pain into your mirror.

This is not a reason to do nothing.

It is a reason to train.

Welcome aboard.

The ship is moving.

The lesson will begin before you feel prepared.

## Page 2: What This Game Is

**Mastering the Game of Allyship** is a tabletop roleplaying game about students learning to become useful under pressure.

You play members of a hidden traveling academy, a school-ship that moves from world to world answering crises ordinary institutions cannot or will not resolve. Each mission asks the same impossible question in a new costume:

**What does help require here?**

The answer is never only courage.

Sometimes help means noticing what everyone has learned not to say. Sometimes it means repairing the harm your own team caused while trying to be useful. Sometimes it means staying present while someone is disappointed in you. Sometimes it means moving a resource, interrupting harm, telling the truth, building a structure, or stepping back so the right person can lead.

This game is not about proving your character is good.

It is about what happens when your help meets reality.

You will make mistakes. The rules expect this. The ship expects this. Your teachers expect this so thoroughly that several of them will be suspicious if you do not make mistakes in the first week.

The question is not whether you can remain clean.

The question is whether you can remain in contact.

## Page 3: What You Do In Play

In play, the Guide presents a world in crisis.

You and the other players create students aboard the school-ship. Each student belongs to a House, trains through Schools, carries a private belief that gets in their way, and has people or promises that make the work matter.

During a mission, you enter a charged situation and make allyship moves.

You Wake Up when you try to understand what is really happening.

You Clean Up when you repair harm, own impact, or restore integrity.

You Grow Up when you stay with discomfort, feedback, complexity, or limits.

You Show Up when you act, intervene, resource, organize, witness, or follow through.

The dice do not tell you whether you are a good person.

The dice tell you what your move does to the field.

On a strong hit, your move lands. On a mixed hit, it lands with a cost. On a miss, the move reveals a truth you did not yet know how to carry.

Failure does not remove you from the story.

Failure gives you Adversity: the kind of leverage only earned by finding the edge of your current capacity.

If you can name your impact, failure can also become Growth.

## Page 4: The Ship

No two crews describe the ship the same way.

Some swear it is a skyship, all brass rails, stormglass windows, and training decks open to impossible weather. Others remember it as a fugitive temple hidden between stars, its corridors folding away from empire patrols. Some call it a monastery. Some call it a school. Some call it the last kind thing left after the old order fell.

The ship does not mind.

It has changed its face before.

What matters is what it carries.

It carries the four Houses of practice: the social lineages that teach students how work moves through a crew.

It carries the six Schools of mastery: the disciplines that teach students how to become useful without becoming dangerous in the old familiar ways.

It carries maps of worlds that never asked to become lessons.

It carries memorials to missions that went badly.

It carries a Headmaster with a long memory, a blind spot, and at least one grief no one is allowed to touch directly.

Most of all, it carries students.

Students who are not ready.

Students who are needed anyway.

## Page 5: The Four Houses

The Houses are where students learn how work moves.

They are not personality tests, though first-year students keep treating them that way. They are practice lineages, ship departments, social worlds, and arguments that have been going on longer than anyone currently aboard.

Every House believes it knows what the others forget.

They are all correct.

### Provisioners

Provisioners ask what is actually depleted.

They know every crisis has a material body. Someone is out of food, time, transport, shelter, medication, translation, sleep, childcare, money, or backup. Provisioners do not trust beautiful plans that leave exhausted people carrying the same load.

Their shadow is turning care into control.

### Weavers

Weavers ask what structure lets the work continue.

They build meetings, rotations, agreements, maps, messages, coalitions, rituals, and handoffs. They know that a good impulse without a container becomes another burden for the same three people.

Their shadow is mistaking process for contact.

### Linekeepers

Linekeepers ask what line must be drawn and what it will cost.

They train for the moment when waiting becomes complicity. They interrupt harm, refuse false peace, protect the vulnerable, and remind the ship that consensus is not always available before action is required.

Their shadow is confusing force with clarity.

### Lanternbearers

Lanternbearers ask what truth must become visible, and with whose consent.

They carry witness, story, signal, memory, warning, and public meaning. They know silence protects power, but they also know exposure can become another harm when truth is taken from the people who paid for it.

Their shadow is turning pain into spectacle.

## Page 6: The Six Schools

The Schools are where students learn how mastery changes shape.

They are not a ladder where one School outranks another. Each School teaches a discipline. Each discipline can be practiced shallowly, bravely, rigidly, beautifully, destructively, or with hard-won grace.

The School owns the curriculum.

You own your relationship to it.

### School of the Body

Owned Stat: **Sense**

The Body teaches students to feel the field before explaining it. Its classrooms are weather decks, breath halls, herb rooms, dream cabins, and quiet floors where students learn that the body often notices the truth before the mind has negotiated permission.

The Body asks: **What is happening beneath the stated story?**

### School of the Line

Owned Stat: **Act**

The Line teaches students to intervene without becoming cruel. Its rooms are sparring circles, boundary courts, emergency drills, and red-lit halls where students practice the awful clean moment when someone must move before consensus arrives.

The Line asks: **What needs to happen now?**

### School of the Oath

Owned Stat: **Steady**

The Oath teaches students to preserve what matters without becoming the wall. Its halls hold records, vows, memorials, protocols, and ceremonies that remember why the ship exists.

The Oath asks: **What can you remain with without collapsing or performing?**

### School of the Pattern

Owned Stat: **Shape**

The Pattern teaches students to design leverage without losing contact. Its rooms are full of map tables, model cities, thread diagrams, simulated councils, and systems that break if one face disappears from the design.

The Pattern asks: **What structure would make right action easier?**

### School of the Bridge

Owned Stat: **Tend**

The Bridge teaches students to hold relationship without vanishing. Its rooms are tea chambers, translation decks, conflict circles, guest quarters, and doorways where staying and leaving both have consequences.

The Bridge asks: **What does care require materially or relationally?**

### School of the Horizon

Owned Stat: **Speak**

The Horizon teaches students to see the game and still stay in it. Its observatories and long-windowed rooms help students rise above the crisis, then require them to return with one grounded truth they are willing to serve.

The Horizon asks: **What needs to be said, and how cleanly can it be said?**

## Page 7: A Mission Begins

The ship arrives above a city built around a dry river.

From the observation deck, the riverbed looks like a pale scar. Bridges cross nothing. Fishing boats sit tilted in dust. The city council has hung banners thanking the school before anyone from the ship has set foot on the ground.

The Headmaster reads the banners and sighs.

"They are grateful too early," she says.

That is all the warning you get.

By noon, your crew is standing in a public square while the council performs welcome. The Provisioners notice the water barrels are locked behind the stage. The Lanternbearers notice every speech praises resilience but no one says the word theft. The Linekeepers notice guards at the edge of the square, hands resting near their weapons. The Weavers notice three local organizers standing apart from the official delegation.

Then a child steps forward with an empty cup.

No one on the stage looks at her.

The Guide turns to the table.

"What do you do?"

## Page 8: A Move At The Table

One player says, "I want to understand why nobody is looking at the child."

That is **Wake Up**.

The Guide asks, "How are you doing it?"

The player thinks for a moment. "I stop watching the stage and read the crowd. Who tenses? Who looks away? Who looks angry that she stepped forward?"

That is **Sense**, the discipline of the Body.

The player rolls 2d6 + Sense.

On a 10+, they might ask three questions. On a 7-9, they ask one. On a 6-, they still ask one, but the answer will implicate them, cost them, or reveal what they missed.

The dice come up 7.

The player has +1 Sense, for a total of 8.

A mixed hit.

They ask, "Who is carrying the cost?"

The Guide answers: "The local organizers. They arranged emergency water distribution last month, but the council took control of the barrels this morning so the school would see an orderly welcome instead of a crisis."

The player gets the truth.

The Guide adds a cost.

"As you notice this, one of the organizers notices you noticing. She gives you a look that says: do not turn us into your lesson."

Now the move has landed.

The field has changed.

The table knows more than it did.

The next move matters.

## Page 9: Why The Dice Matter

The dice are not here to punish you for trying.

They are here because allyship is action under uncertainty.

You will rarely have the whole story. You will almost never have perfect timing. You may have power you have not accounted for, needs you have not noticed, urgency that is real, and fear that dresses itself as wisdom.

The dice make room for the thing you did not control.

A 10+ does not mean you are good.

It means this move landed cleanly enough to create more possibility.

A 7-9 does not mean you failed.

It means the world answered with complexity.

A 6- does not mean you should have stayed quiet.

It means the move revealed the edge of your training.

The ship can work with that.

The question is whether you can.

## Page 10: The First Rule Of The Ship

The first rule is not be brave.

The first rule is not be right.

The first rule is not speak up.

The first rule is not step back.

The first rule is:

**Stay in contact with what your move does.**

If you speak and someone becomes exposed, stay in contact.

If you act and someone else pays the cost, stay in contact.

If you repair and the repair is not accepted, stay in contact.

If you freeze, perform, rescue, overexplain, disappear, or make the beautiful plan that helps everyone except the person standing outside the gate, stay in contact.

Contact is not comfort.

Contact is the beginning of usefulness.

Everything else in this book is training.

## Page 11: What You Need To Play

To play, you need:

- two six-sided dice
- character sheets
- pencils or a shared document
- tokens or marks for Adversity, Clarity, Tension, and Growth
- a mission or campaign packet
- people willing to play honestly and treat each other with care

You do not need to be an expert in allyship.

You do not need to know the perfect language.

You do not need to be fearless, flawless, or impressive.

You need a table that can agree to pause when needed, repair when possible, and remember that the point of play is not to win the moral argument.

The point is to practice becoming useful before the moment costs someone real.

## Page 12: Safety As Ship Protocol

The ship has survived because it learned the difference between discomfort and harm.

Discomfort is part of training.

Harm is not proof that training is working.

Before play, the table establishes boundaries. Some subjects may be welcome. Some may be veiled, softened, moved offscreen, or removed entirely. Anyone may ask for a pause. Anyone may name that something has become too much, too close, too fast, or simply not what they came to play.

This is not weakness.

It is ship protocol.

No mission is improved by forcing a player past consent.

No scene is so important that the table must become unsafe to finish it.

In the fiction, your students are learning consent, repair, pressure, truth, and care.

At the table, so are you.

The rules begin here.

## Page 13: The Promise Of Play

This game will not certify you.

It will not absolve you.

It will not make allyship simple.

It will give you a ship, a crew, a crisis, a handful of moves, and enough distance from the real world to practice things the real world asks for too late.

You will learn which House feels like home.

You will learn which School irritates you because it is probably correct.

You will learn what your student offers too quickly, what they spend too freely, what line they fear holding, and what bond makes the work matter.

You will watch help become complicated.

Then you will decide what to do next.

That is the game.

The ship is moving.

Take your place.
