# School Sheet - School of the Horizon

Face: **Sage**

Owned Stat: **Speak**

Milestone question:

**Can you see the game and still stay in it?**

Core lesson:

Perspective is only mastery when it returns as service.

## Vibe

The School of the Horizon sits in observatories, quiet decks, archives, void gardens, long-windowed rooms, and places on the ship where students can see multiple worlds at once.

It feels like airbending detachment meeting Jedi exile, cosmic strategy, old grief, and the lonely clarity of knowing the game is larger than the current scene.

## Head of School

Name: **Head Thalen Orr**

Title: Keeper of the Far Game

What they embody:

Panoramic vision that still chooses commitment.

Teaching style:

Thalen lets students rise above the problem, then requires them to name the next grounded act.

Blind spot:

They can mistake spaciousness for presence.

Signature line:

"If seeing more makes you less useful, look again."

## Lieutenants

### Veyra Sol

Title: Altitude Tutor

Subskill: Level-shifting

What they teach:

Students learn to identify what level of the game a conversation is happening on.

How they test students:

Veyra gives the same conflict at personal, relational, institutional, and mythic scales.

Shadow version:

Using altitude to escape accountability.

Move or training prompt:

**Name the Level:** When a scene is stuck, name whether the conflict is personal, relational, structural, or mythic. If the table agrees, gain 1 Clarity.

### Omi Farwake

Title: Descent Guide

Subskill: Returning from insight

What they teach:

Students learn to translate vision into the next grounded move.

How they test students:

Omi asks students to turn an elegant insight into one sentence someone in pain can use.

Shadow version:

Beautiful uselessness.

Move or training prompt:

**Descend:** After naming a big pattern, choose one concrete action available in the next scene. Take +1 forward to Show Up if it serves someone other than your self-image.

### Kestrel Noon

Title: Gamekeeper

Subskill: Multiple-game awareness

What they teach:

Students learn to notice when different people are playing different games in the same room.

How they test students:

Kestrel stages scenes where one faction seeks repair, one seeks status, one seeks delay, and one seeks exit.

Shadow version:

Cynicism; seeing everyone's game and trusting no one.

Move or training prompt:

**Spot the Game:** When you identify two different games being played in the same scene, ask: What does each player think winning means?

## Trial

The student must see the larger pattern and then make a grounded commitment inside it.

They pass if their perspective increases their usefulness.

## Shadow Exam

The student is given a scene they can explain perfectly from above.

They fail if explanation replaces participation.

They pass if they descend.

## Mark / Badge

The **Horizon Mark**, a thin arc worn above the brow, collar, or spell focus.

## Move Unlock: See and Stay

When you name the larger game without leaving the people inside it, roll + Wake Up or + Grow Up.

On a 10+, choose 2.

On a 7-9, choose 1.

On a 6-, your perspective is accurate but alienating. Say what would bring it back into service.

Options:

- The table sees the level mismatch.
- Someone gets a more useful frame.
- You identify the next grounded act.
- You resist using perspective as escape.

## Downtime Training

During downtime, train with the School of the Horizon to:

- convert Clarity into a grounded action
- understand a recurring campaign pattern
- prepare for a descent scene
- ask what game you are actually playing
- reduce cynicism by making a commitment

## Campaign Use

Use this School when:

- players see the problem but are not acting
- a mission has multiple levels of conflict
- a character is hiding in insight
- the campaign needs integration and long-arc meaning
