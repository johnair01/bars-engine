# Inspiration Texts - Reader Onboarding Analysis

Status: formal comparative analysis

Purpose: study how the inspiration texts draw readers into their systems, then extract lessons for the MTGOA manual's prose, structure, and first-reader experience.

## Core Question

How does each book move a reader from curiosity to play?

This analysis focuses on four kinds of onboarding:

1. **Emotional onboarding** - why the reader wants to enter.
2. **Procedural onboarding** - how the reader learns what to do.
3. **Identity onboarding** - how the reader imagines who they are in the game.
4. **Artifact onboarding** - how the book itself feels like a usable object.

## Summary Matrix

| Text | First Doorway | Main Reader Promise | Teaching Style | Best Lesson For MTGOA |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Kids on Bikes** | sensory scene and small-town mystery | ordinary people facing strange forces together | friendly, safety-forward, example-heavy | open with a vivid situation, then make collaboration feel easy |
| **Dungeon World** | heroic address and class fantasy | you are the adventurers who go where others cannot | bold, direct, fiction-first, rules-confident | make the reader feel addressed as a capable player immediately |
| **Hearts Blazing** | collaborative kit and genre emulation | build a spec-fic ensemble drama together | procedural, card-facing, improvisational | give players handles: roles, episodes, caretakers, spotlight |
| **Foundations** | clean tool promise and first act of creation | create a world by drawing cards and changing a map | minimal, orderly, prompt-driven | make procedures feel like creation, not explanation |

## Kids on Bikes

## How It Draws The Reader In

Kids on Bikes opens with a sensory micro-scene: an old house, a creaking door, cheap flashlights, danger, and the question of who goes first.

This is extremely efficient.

Before it explains the system, it gives the reader a bodily memory of the genre. The reader does not begin by learning a mechanic. They begin by standing at a threshold.

Then the game names its premise in plain language: everyday people face strange and powerful forces they cannot fully understand or control. The promise is not "you are heroes." The promise is "you are ordinary, outmatched, and together."

That emotional hook does a lot of work.

## Its Onboarding Sequence

Kids on Bikes moves in this order:

1. sensory genre scene
2. plain-language game premise
3. safety and boundaries
4. collaborative town creation
5. rumors
6. character creation
7. relationship questions
8. stat dice and checks

This sequence says something important about the game's priorities.

The first real procedure after the premise is not combat, stats, or archetypes. It is boundary setting. Then worldbuilding. Then character creation.

The book quietly teaches that the table is the container before the story is the content.

## How It Uses Examples

Kids on Bikes relies heavily on examples that show a table making decisions together. The examples are not only rules examples; they model tone, permission, and collaboration.

The examples often do three jobs at once:

- show what the rule means
- demonstrate acceptable table behavior
- make the fictional town feel more real

This is especially strong in the town creation and rumor sections. The reader learns the procedure while watching a place become playable.

## How It Handles Safety

Safety is introduced early and practically. It is not framed as an optional appendix or a moral lecture. It is part of starting the game.

The important design move:

Kids on Bikes makes safety feel like normal table logistics.

For MTGOA, this is crucial. A game about allyship cannot bury safety later in the book, but it also cannot make safety feel like paperwork. It should feel like ship protocol: ordinary, expected, protective, and playable.

## What MTGOA Should Borrow

Borrow:

- sensory threshold opening
- practical safety before heavy play
- collaborative world/mission setup
- rumors as a way to make a place alive
- examples that model table behavior, not only mechanics
- "no wrong answers" permission during creation
- relationship prompts before action begins

Avoid:

- overexplaining before the reader has a strong fantasy anchor
- letting the ordinary-town tone flatten MTGOA's academy wonder

## MTGOA Translation

MTGOA should open with a threshold scene equivalent to the old house door.

Possible MTGOA equivalent:

- the school-ship arriving over a world in crisis
- the Headmaster noticing something wrong before anyone else does
- students standing at the edge of their first mission
- a small visible injustice that reveals a larger pattern

The reader should feel the genre before they learn the rules.

## Dungeon World

## How It Draws The Reader In

Dungeon World opens with a confident genre declaration: this is a world of magic, monsters, treasure, danger, gods, demons, heroes, and glory.

Then it addresses the reader directly:

You are those heroes.

This is the core onboarding move. Dungeon World does not ask the reader to admire the setting from outside. It recruits them.

The text then moves quickly into class fantasy. Each class receives a strong voice passage that tells the reader who they are, what they want, and why the world needs them.

The class sections are not merely informational. They are seductive identity containers.

## Its Onboarding Sequence

Dungeon World moves in this order:

1. broad genre promise
2. direct address to the reader
3. class fantasy
4. what roleplaying is
5. how moves trigger
6. how dice work
7. character creation and moves

The book teaches through confidence.

It does not apologize for the genre. It does not spend long defending why fantasy adventure matters. It assumes the reader wants danger, treasure, and heroism, then hands them a role.

## How It Teaches System

Dungeon World's key teaching achievement is the move trigger.

It explains that when a character does something in the fiction that triggers a move, the move happens. The player cannot bypass the move by describing the fictional action, and cannot use the move without doing the fictional action.

This is a clean conceptual bridge between prose and mechanics.

The rules are not abstract widgets.

They are consequences of fictional behavior.

## How It Uses Voice

Dungeon World's class prose is opinionated, punchy, and character-facing. It makes each class feel like a dare.

The prose often implies:

- you are necessary
- you are dangerous
- your companions misunderstand you
- the world is waiting for what only you can do

This creates identity heat.

## What MTGOA Should Borrow

Borrow:

- direct address
- strong identity invitations
- confident explanation of move triggers
- class/role sections that feel like recruitment letters
- rules language that ties action to fiction
- a clean explanation of 10+ / 7-9 / 6-

Avoid:

- importing heroic dominance as the main fantasy
- making players feel like saviors
- framing danger as mainly external monsters instead of relational/systemic pressure

## MTGOA Translation

MTGOA should give each House and School the same identity heat Dungeon World gives classes.

The reader should feel:

- "I might be a Provisioner."
- "The School of the Line scares me, which means I want to train there."
- "This teacher would see through me."

MTGOA should address the reader directly, but the address should recruit them into usefulness rather than glory.

Dungeon World says, in effect: you are the heroes.

MTGOA should say: you are not ready, and you are needed anyway.

## Hearts Blazing

## How It Draws The Reader In

Hearts Blazing opens more like a play kit than a polished lore chapter.

It tells the reader what the game does:

- generate speculative fiction together
- use recognizable archetypes
- give characters hidden motives
- draw episodes from a deck
- bid cards to shape outcomes and spotlight
- create ensemble drama

The appeal is not primarily setting immersion. The appeal is "this machine will help us make a show together."

That is a different kind of seduction.

It draws the reader in by making the table feel active immediately.

## Its Onboarding Sequence

Hearts Blazing moves in this order:

1. premise of collaborative spec-fic drama
2. setup materials
3. assign genre/world caretakers
4. safety and tone boundaries
5. choose archetypes
6. explain character decks, frailty, motives
7. build plotline deck
8. reveal episode
9. bid and resolve

The major onboarding move is distributing authority.

Players are not only characters. They are caretakers of parts of the story universe.

## How It Teaches Play

Hearts Blazing teaches through components:

- character decks
- episode cards
- motives
- keywords
- commercial break card
- bid values
- frailty cards

Each component gives the reader a handle.

The system says, "If you do not know what to contribute, touch this object and it will give you a direction."

This is very important for MTGOA because allyship play could otherwise become intimidating. Players need handles.

## Its Main Risk

The setup can feel dense.

Hearts Blazing has a lot of conceptual objects before the first scene fully breathes: caretakers, archetypes, decks, motives, keywords, episode thresholds, bidding, launch/bridge/wrap, commercial breaks.

That can be exciting for invested players but rough for one-shot onboarding.

## What MTGOA Should Borrow

Borrow:

- ensemble role clarity
- shared world authority through caretakers
- episode structure
- commercial break as pause/safety/pacing
- hidden or private motives as drama engine
- cards as inspiration and pacing tools
- table-facing components that reduce blank-page anxiety

Avoid:

- requiring too many components before first play
- making setup feel like homework
- letting hidden motives encourage betrayal instead of productive tension

## MTGOA Translation

MTGOA can use Hearts Blazing at the mission and campaign layer.

Potential translations:

- mission cards instead of episode cards
- House caretaker roles during world creation
- School spotlight cards
- commercial break renamed as "Ship Bell," "Deck Pause," or "Breath Signal"
- private Belief prompts instead of purely competitive hidden motives
- Launch / Bridge / Wrap as Briefing / Field / Debrief

The big lesson:

Make the table touch the story.

The book should include objects, cards, prompts, records, badges, maps, and handouts that make the game feel actionable.

## Foundations

## How It Draws The Reader In

Foundations opens with exceptional clarity.

It says what it is: a solo worldbuilding game for fantasy settings.

It says what it uses: a standard deck of cards, paper, and something to write with.

It says what it produces: unpredictable, grounded worlds shaped by history, geography, and anthropology.

The seduction is not a lush fictional intro. The seduction is trust.

The reader feels, "I can do this."

## Its Onboarding Sequence

Foundations moves in this order:

1. content warning
2. table of contents
3. concise premise
4. materials
5. setup
6. optional magic module
7. time scale
8. world map
9. chronicle
10. how to play
11. how to finish
12. terminology
13. prompts

This is procedural onboarding at its cleanest.

It teaches just enough to begin, then the prompts do the rest.

## How It Uses Prompts

Foundations prompts combine:

- evocative world text
- precise physical instruction
- permission to interpret
- reflective questions
- durable change to the artifact

For example, an early prompt does not merely say "create geography." It asks the player to draw, rename, split, move, destroy, or reinterpret the map.

The world is not explained into being.

It is altered into being.

This is the strongest craft lesson in Foundations.

## How It Makes The Artifact Matter

Foundations centers two artifacts:

- the map
- the chronicle

Everything the player does modifies one or both.

This means the player can see progress. The world accumulates memory.

The book itself becomes a machine for producing an object the player cares about.

## What MTGOA Should Borrow

Borrow:

- clean "what you need" onboarding
- prompt-driven world creation
- living map / mission field
- chronicle as memory
- instructions that create visible changes
- optional modules clearly marked as optional
- minimal first-play overhead

Avoid:

- being so procedural that the fiction feels dry
- making the worldbuilding solo when MTGOA needs ensemble relationship

## MTGOA Translation

MTGOA should have:

- ship map
- mission field map
- mission chronicle
- debrief record
- faction clocks
- visible token tracks
- campaign packet prompts that modify the map and social field

The manual should give readers procedures that produce artifacts they want to keep.

## Cross-Text Patterns

## 1. Each Text Opens A Door Before Teaching A System

Kids on Bikes opens a haunted door.

Dungeon World opens the door to heroic danger.

Hearts Blazing opens a box of collaborative genre tools.

Foundations opens a deck and a blank map.

MTGOA needs its own door:

**the school-ship arrives above a world in crisis, and the reader is invited aboard before they feel ready.**

## 2. Each Text Tells The Reader Who They Get To Be

Kids on Bikes: ordinary people in extraordinary danger.

Dungeon World: adventurers who go where others cannot.

Hearts Blazing: ensemble archetypes in speculative drama.

Foundations: creator and historian of a world.

MTGOA:

students of a hidden academy learning to become useful under pressure.

This identity must be repeated early and often.

## 3. Each Text Gives The Reader A Handle

Kids on Bikes gives town questions, rumors, tropes, and relationship prompts.

Dungeon World gives classes, moves, and 2d6 outcomes.

Hearts Blazing gives archetype decks, episode cards, motives, and caretakers.

Foundations gives cards, map, chronicle, and prompts.

MTGOA should give:

- House
- School
- owned stat
- Offer
- Cost
- Line
- Bond
- Basic Moves
- tokens
- mission map
- debrief form

These handles keep allyship from becoming abstract.

## 4. Each Text Makes Safety Match The Genre

Kids on Bikes makes safety feel like common table care.

Hearts Blazing frames safety as Cut and Brake for emotionally charged collaborative drama.

Foundations uses a content warning and lets players determine parameters.

Dungeon World is older and less safety-forward in its opening, but its fiction-first rules still create table clarity.

MTGOA should make safety feel like ship protocol.

It should be:

- early
- practical
- in-world flavored
- non-negotiable
- not moralizing

## 5. Each Text Balances Permission And Constraint

Kids on Bikes says there are no wrong answers, then gives concrete town questions.

Dungeon World lets fiction lead, but move triggers are strict.

Hearts Blazing invites improvisation, but cards and bids structure spotlight.

Foundations gives prompts, but the map interpretation is open.

MTGOA should do the same:

- strong fantasy permission
- clear procedures
- flexible interpretation
- visible consequences

## 6. Each Text Uses Examples To Reduce Fear

Kids on Bikes shows example players building a town.

Dungeon World gives simple roll examples.

Hearts Blazing explains episode bidding in procedural detail.

Foundations gives prompts that function as examples by being playable immediately.

MTGOA needs more examples than a normal RPG because the subject can feel intimidating.

Every major rule should have:

- what someone says at the table
- which move triggers
- which stat applies
- what a hit looks like
- what a cost looks like
- what changes afterward

## Implications For The MTGOA Opening

The current opening prose draft is directionally right because it uses:

- threshold invitation
- direct address
- school-ship fantasy
- mission scene
- sample roll
- safety as protocol

But it should be strengthened by borrowing more deliberately from the source texts.

## Recommended Opening Architecture

1. **Threshold scene**
   - Borrow from Kids on Bikes.
   - Begin with a sensory mission moment or invitation aboard.

2. **Direct recruitment**
   - Borrow from Dungeon World.
   - Tell the reader who they are in this game.

3. **Concrete table promise**
   - Borrow from Foundations.
   - Tell them what they need and what they will make.

4. **Collaborative authority**
   - Borrow from Hearts Blazing.
   - Make it clear players help define the ship, crew, mission field, and stakes.

5. **Safety as protocol**
   - Borrow from Kids on Bikes and Hearts Blazing.
   - Put it early and make it feel native.

6. **Example of play**
   - Borrow from all four.
   - Show a concrete move inside a mission scene before deep rules.

7. **Identity choice**
   - Borrow from Dungeon World and Kids on Bikes.
   - Give Houses and Schools as desire objects early.

## Recommended MTGOA Reader Arc

The reader should move through these feelings:

1. "I am curious."
2. "I can see the world."
3. "I know who I might be here."
4. "I understand what happens at the table."
5. "This is safe enough to try."
6. "This has enough depth to return to."
7. "I want to make a student."

## What The MTGOA Manual Should Do Better Than The Sources

## More Beautiful Than Foundations

Foundations is procedurally elegant, but spare.

MTGOA needs more romance, more texture, more artifact-feel.

## Easier To Start Than Hearts Blazing

Hearts Blazing has great ensemble machinery, but setup can feel busy.

MTGOA should offer a one-shot path that gets to a mission quickly.

## Less Savior-Coded Than Dungeon World

Dungeon World's confidence is useful, but MTGOA must avoid "you are the heroes who fix the world."

The fantasy is training, not domination.

## More Mechanically Integrated Than Kids on Bikes

Kids on Bikes has beautiful town and relationship onboarding, but its core mechanics are more general-purpose.

MTGOA should make its mechanics teach the exact curriculum: contact, impact, repair, capacity, action, and consequence.

## Concrete Revisions To Make Next

1. Add a stronger "What You Will Make" page early:
   - a student
   - a crew
   - a school-ship
   - a mission field
   - a chronicle of impact

2. Add a "Choose Your First Handle" spread:
   - House
   - School
   - Offer
   - Cost
   - Line
   - Bond

3. Add more artifact language:
   - student record
   - mission briefing
   - debrief form
   - ship map
   - field map
   - School marks

4. Add more example-table dialogue:
   - player says what they do
   - Guide asks how
   - table identifies move and stat
   - dice land
   - field changes

5. Add the collaborative authority promise:
   - players define truths about the ship
   - players create rumors about the mission world
   - players help decide what changed after missions

6. Keep the Headmaster invitation, but consider placing a sensory mission threshold before or immediately after it.

## Final Takeaway

The best onboarding strategy for MTGOA is a braid:

- **Kids on Bikes** gives the sensory threshold and collaborative safety.
- **Dungeon World** gives direct identity invitation and fiction-first rules confidence.
- **Hearts Blazing** gives ensemble structure and table-facing components.
- **Foundations** gives clean procedures that create durable artifacts.

MTGOA should open like a secret academy, teach like a field guide, equip like a play kit, and accumulate memory like a chronicle.
