# Chapter 5 Prose Draft - The Four Houses v0.1

Status: book prose draft

Purpose: introduce the four Houses as vivid practice factions through Deputy Headmistress framing, senior student leader voices, House Trust and Heat, House moves, features, duties, clocks, rivalries, and advancement prompts.

## Page 1: A Note From The Deputy Headmistress

The Headmaster wished this chapter to be written by the student leaders.

His exact instruction, delivered while attempting to convince a window to become an ethical telescope, was that they should "have a say."

This is the sort of sentence that begins as democratic wisdom and ends with four seniors submitting ninety-seven pages of House gossip, three competing maps of the laundry tunnels, and one formal complaint about the Weavers' misuse of the phrase "temporary committee."

Nevertheless, the Headmaster is correct.

The Houses are not best understood from the faculty gallery. They are best understood from inside the rooms where students burn toast at midnight, argue over mission reports, hide when their first apology goes badly, and learn which kind of courage they confuse with being right.

The student leaders whose words appear in this chapter are senior students in their final term aboard the ship. They are rookie veterans: old enough to have run field missions, young enough to remember their first week, trusted enough to leave the ship soon as Journeymen, and still reckless enough to believe that a House common room can be improved by moving furniture without permission.

Read them as guides.

Not authorities.

This distinction will save you several awkward conversations and one chair.

## Page 2: Houses Are Arguments That Keep The Ship Honest

The ship has four Houses because allyship has four recurring labors:

The ship has four Houses because allyship has four recurring labors:

- someone must move resources
- someone must build structure
- someone must draw lines
- someone must make truth visible

These labors are not interchangeable. They are also not sufficient alone.

A Provisioner can feed a crisis without changing the pattern that created it.

A Weaver can build a perfect container for work that no longer needs to exist.

A Linekeeper can stop harm and leave everyone too scorched to continue.

A Lanternbearer can make the truth famous while the people inside it become exhausted.

This is why the Houses argue.

Not because the ship enjoys drama, though it should be admitted that several corridors seem suspiciously optimized for overhearing. The Houses argue because each House protects one necessary truth from being swallowed by the others.

Your House is the practice tradition that claimed you first.

It is not your personality.

It is not your moral ranking.

It is not a legally binding prophecy, however much the old portraits in the dining hall enjoy implying otherwise.

Your House tells the table what you are trained to notice, what kind of help you are likely to offer, what mistakes you are most vulnerable to making, and which faction aboard the ship will feel personally invested in whether you grow up well.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> A House is not "what kind of ally you are." It is what kind of allyship trouble you are most likely to cause while trying to help.

## Page 3: The Four Houses At A Glance

| House | Domain | Core Question | Motto |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Provisioners** | Gather Resources | What is actually depleted, and how does capacity move? | Need first. Theater later. |
| **Weavers** | Skillful Organizing | What structure lets the work continue? | Care needs a container. |

| **Lanternbearers** | Raise Awareness | What truth must become visible, and with whose consent? | Make it visible. Do not make them a symbol. |

In play, a House gives you four things:

1. A question to ask when the scene becomes tangled.
2. A starting move that enters the Basic Moves through your House discipline.
3. A once-per-episode feature.
4. A shadow the table may lovingly, firmly, and with appropriate snacks, hold you accountable to.

Houses slot into the Basic Moves from Chapter 4.

They do not replace **Wake Up**, **Open Up**, **Clean Up**, **Grow Up**, or **Show Up**. A House move tells the table how your training changes one of those moments.

For example:

- A Provisioner may Wake Up by noticing depletion.
- A Weaver may Clean Up by naming the missing role.
- A Linekeeper may Show Up by drawing a boundary.
- A Lanternbearer may Wake Up by making a hidden pattern visible.

The Basic Move remains the spine. The House gives the move a body, an accent, a hallway rumor, and occasionally a clipboard with upsettingly accurate tabs.

## Page 4: House Trust And House Heat

Houses remember.

This is not always flattering, but it is useful.

Each student may track **Trust** and **Heat** with Houses aboard the ship.

**House Trust** means a House believes your work honors its domain.

**House Heat** means a House believes your work is misusing, threatening, or distorting its domain.

Trust and Heat are not opposites. A House can trust your courage and still have Heat on your methods. A House can be irritated by you and still sponsor your growth. This is known as education.

### Trust

Track Trust from 0 to 3.

| Trust | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0 | Unknown or neutral |
| 1 | Recognized |
| 2 | Trusted |
| 3 | Sponsored |

Mark Trust with a House when you:

- honor that House's core question under pressure
- accept correction from that House
- use that House's method without performing superiority
- protect that House's domain from being ignored
- repair harm caused by misusing that House's domain

At **3 Trust**, the House may offer one major support during an episode:

- a teacher's counsel
- a specialized resource
- a protected meeting
- a mission lead
- a trusted contact
- access to cross-House training
- a temporary move card

Trust is not spent like pocket change. It is a relationship. Use it, and then show whether the relationship deepened or thinned because of what happened next.

### Heat

Track Heat from 0 to 3.

| Heat | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0 | No active concern |
| 1 | Watched |
| 2 | Challenged |
| 3 | Summoned |

Mark Heat with a House when you:

- ignore that House's core question and create consequences
- use that House's method as a costume
- create a problem that House members must clean up
- dismiss a House warning that later proves relevant
- turn the House gift into its shadow

At **3 Heat**, the House demands a scene.

This might be a hearing, confrontation, mentorship session, repair request, public correction, field evaluation, or ominously polite invitation to tea.

After the scene, reduce Heat to 1 if the student makes a sincere repair, concession, learning move, or change in field practice.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Heat is not "the game punishes me for doing allyship wrong." Heat is "people noticed your method has consequences." This is terrible news if your growth plan was never being perceived.

## Page 5: How A House Enters A Scene

When you want your House training to matter, do not announce a doctrine.

Ask the House question.

Then check the gift.

Then check the shadow.

Then choose the Basic Move.

The procedure is small because the situation will not be.

### The House Pressure Test

Ask:

1. What does my House notice first?
2. What help does my House want to offer?
3. What would my House get wrong if I acted too quickly?
4. Which Basic Move is actually happening?

If the answer changes the scene, bring in the House move.

If the answer merely decorates your choice, let it be color and keep playing.

### Example: The Same Crisis, Four Readings

The crew arrives on Pelagos Station, where public reservoirs are locked behind a governor's emergency order. The official story is that rationing is necessary after a sabotage attempt. The local organizers say the wells were already being diverted before the explosion.

The Provisioner asks, "Who has no water tonight?"

The Weaver asks, "Who controls distribution, and who is doing the unpaid coordination?"

The Linekeeper asks, "What line has already been crossed, and who is still pretending it is policy?"

The Lanternbearer asks, "What proof can become visible without making the residents pay first?"

No House is seeing the whole truth.

The crew needs all four.

## Page 6: Provisioners

Senior student leader: **Nia Calder**, outgoing Keeper of the Warm Room, Provisioner field lead for the Juniper Evacuation, three-time defendant in the Great Blanket Redistribution Hearing.

> NIA CALDER, PROVISIONERS
>
> People think we are the House of soup.
>
> We are not offended by this. Soup is ancient technology. Soup has ended fights, exposed lies, saved meetings, and made several very proud people admit they had bodies.
>
> But if you join us because you enjoy being needed, we will notice. If you join us because you think care means anticipating every need before anyone risks asking, we will notice that too. Provisioners are not here to become the ship's favorite exhausted person.
>
> We ask where depletion lives. Then we move capacity without making a shrine of ourselves.

### First Impression

The Provisioner quarter smells like bread, metal polish, rainwater, old canvas, and whatever fruit the hydroponics deck is pretending was intentional this week.

Their common room is called **the Warm Room**, though it is less a room than a sequence of kitchens, supply lockers, sleep alcoves, repair benches, and tables that have survived more strategy meetings than several governments.

Nothing matches. Everything has a label. Half the labels are warnings. The other half are love letters to future exhausted people.

There is always a kettle on.

There is always a list on the wall titled **WHO IS CARRYING TOO MUCH?**

No one is allowed to erase their own name from it.

### House Culture

Provisioners greet each other by checking hands: empty, shaking, full, burned, ink-stained, clenched.

Their initiation meal is not cooked by the new student. It is cooked for them. The lesson is simple and humiliating to exactly the right people: before you learn to feed others, learn what it feels like to receive without auditioning for worth.

House slang:

- **warm work**: care that reaches the body
- **theater bread**: highly visible help nobody asked for
- **ghost load**: labor everyone benefits from and nobody counts
- **second kettle**: the backup plan for the backup plan

Other Houses say Provisioners can smuggle a bedroll, a battery, and a labor dispute into any room aboard the ship.

This is largely true.

Provisioners move resources.

They are quartermasters, hearth-keepers, mutual aid captains, field medics, cooks, logistics witches, supply runners, and the people who know a revolution without snacks becomes a meeting about snacks.

Provisioners are the House of practical care.

They notice hunger, exhaustion, missing tools, silent shortages, blocked access, and the person carrying too much while everyone else praises their resilience.

**Domain:** Gather Resources

**Core Question:** What is actually depleted, and how does capacity move?

**Motto:** Need first. Theater later.

**Symbol:** A cupped bowl with a star inside.

### Gift

Provisioners can tell the difference between visible help and needed help.

They know support is not real until it reaches the place where depletion lives.

### Shadow

Provisioners can turn care into control.

They may hoard resources, over-function, rescue people who did not ask to be rescued, or turn material support into a performance of goodness.

Listen for:

- "I know what they need."
- "If I do not hold this, no one will."
- "Look how much I am helping."
- "They should be grateful."

### Starting Move: Move The Right Resource

When you stop before helping and ask what is actually depleted, roll + Wake Up.

On a 10+, choose 2.

On a 7-9, choose 1.

On a 6-, you identify a need, but your first offer would make something worse unless you change course.

Options:

- Name the real missing resource.
- Identify who is carrying the current burden.
- Learn what support is not wanted.
- Find one resource already nearby.
- Gain 1 Clarity.

### House Feature: Supply Cache

Once per episode, you may declare that you brought one ordinary useful item, contact, or preparation that fits the mission.

If it solves the problem too neatly, the Guide names a cost:

- it is limited
- someone else needed it
- it creates an obligation
- using it exposes the ship
- it helps now but complicates later

### House Clock: Burnout Spiral

1. One person quietly carries too much.
2. The group praises their resilience.
3. The real need gets renamed as attitude.
4. Help arrives in the wrong form.
5. The overburdened person withdraws or breaks.
6. Trust in support collapses.

### Mara In Play

Mara is a Provisioner because depletion is the first language she learned to read.

On Pelagos Station, she wants to break the lock on the reservoir warehouse. The Guide asks what she is trying to understand before acting.

Mara pauses and asks, "Who is going without water tonight, and who has been moving it quietly anyway?"

That is **Move the Right Resource**.

On a 10+, Mara learns that the public warehouse is mostly symbolic. The real water is being moved through maintenance pipes by station engineers who are already risking arrest. She also identifies who is carrying the burden: an exhausted night-shift mechanic named Seli.

Mara does not get to solve the mission.

She gets to stop solving the wrong mission.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Provisioners are at their strongest when they ask first. They are at their most terrifying when they decide "asking first" is a luxury for people who have not seen real need. This is how soup becomes colonialism with carrots.

### Advancement Prompt

At the end of a mission, ask:

**Who needed less admiration and more support?**

If your answer changes how the group allocates resources, mark Growth.

## Page 7: Weavers

Senior student leader: **Sol Anik**, outgoing Chair of Necessary Meetings, Weaver field lead for the Glass Orchard Coalition, author of the famous eight-word protocol: "Name the role or become the role."

> SOL ANIK, WEAVERS
>
> Everyone mocks the meeting until the meeting is the only reason anyone knows who brought the medicine, who promised transport, who speaks for the absent, and who has been quietly doing the work of four people while being thanked for their "energy."
>
> Weavers do not worship process. We worship continuity.
>
> A structure is good when it lets living people keep choosing the work without being eaten by it. A structure is bad when it survives by feeding on the people it claims to protect.
>
> Learn the difference quickly. The ship has enough haunted committees.

### First Impression

The Weaver quarter is built around **the Loom**, a suspended map-room where colored cords run from ceiling hooks to tables, doorframes, railings, and occasionally students who stood still too long.

The cords mark agreements, roles, debts, promises, conflicts, and questions that have not yet found their owners.

It smells like ink, tea, dust, warmed paper, and the charged silence before someone says, "Can we clarify the decision rule?"

Their common room has no fixed furniture. This is either profound or unbearable, depending on your day. Tables fold from walls. Chairs hang from ceiling tracks. Whiteboards appear in places where whiteboards should not be able to appear.

Somewhere in the quarter is a bell rung only when a handoff is completed cleanly.

First-years assume this is ceremonial.

Seniors weep when they hear it.

### House Culture

Weavers greet each other by asking, "What are you holding?"

The correct answer may be a task, a grief, a grudge, a schedule, a secret, or "nothing, and I mean it."

Their initiation is called **the Empty Chair**. New students sit beside an empty chair and must name who is not in the room, what authority they have, and what harm will happen if the group proceeds without them.

House slang:

- **load-bearing person**: someone the system is pretending is a structure
- **thread rot**: an agreement that looks intact but no longer carries trust
- **meeting-shaped fog**: a gathering designed to postpone a decision
- **clean knot**: a handoff that can be understood by someone who was not there

Other Houses say Weavers can turn breakfast into a governance model.

Weavers reply that breakfast already has governance. People simply prefer not to notice who washes the pans.

Weavers build structure.

They are coalition architects, ritual designers, schedulers, governance nerds, bridge builders, minutes-takers with knives behind the smile, and people who understand that "we should all just communicate" is not a plan.

Weavers are the House of containers.

They build roles, handoffs, agreements, rhythms, decision rules, and repeatable practices.

**Domain:** Skillful Organizing

**Core Question:** What structure lets the work continue?

**Motto:** Care needs a container.

**Symbol:** Four threads knotted around an open center.

### Gift

Weavers can turn fragile good intentions into durable coordination.

They see where work is falling through the gaps and create the minimum structure that lets people keep going.

### Shadow

Weavers can worship process.

They may create meetings instead of movement, control through structure, or protect the system after it stops serving life.

Listen for:

- "We need a process for that."
- "If people followed the structure, this would not happen."
- "The container matters more than the people complaining about it."
- "Let's circle back."

### Starting Move: Name The Missing Role

When a group keeps failing because no one owns a needed function, roll + Wake Up or + Clean Up.

On a 10+, choose 2.

On a 7-9, choose 1.

On a 6-, you name the missing role, but someone hears it as blame.

Options:

- Identify the missing role.
- Clarify who currently carries it unofficially.
- Create a clean handoff.
- Reduce a group's confusion.
- Restore 1 Trust with someone who needed structure.

### House Feature: The Smallest Container

Once per episode, you may propose a small structure:

- a role
- a timebox
- a handoff
- a decision rule
- a check-in
- a shared note

If the table accepts it and uses it, gain 1 Legacy if it still matters by the end of the episode.

### House Clock: Process Collapse

1. Everyone assumes someone else owns the work.
2. One person informally becomes load-bearing.
3. Decisions happen in side channels.
4. Trust erodes around unclear authority.
5. The group creates process too late.
6. The container survives but the people leave.

### Jun In Play

Jun is a Builder with Weaver training. He loves a clean agreement and has been known to become emotionally attached to a shared document.

On Pelagos Station, the crew discovers that three local groups are separately trying to negotiate water access. Each group believes the others abandoned them. None of them knows that the governor's office has been scheduling them in different rooms at different times.

Jun asks, "Who thinks they own coordination, and who is actually doing it?"

That is **Name the Missing Role**.

On a 7-9, Jun identifies that Seli, the night-shift mechanic, has become the unofficial coordinator while also keeping the pipes running. He chooses to create a clean handoff: the crew will host one meeting where each group names a point person and a limit.

The cost is that someone hears "missing role" as "you failed."

Jun now has a repair scene before his structure can become trusted.

### Advancement Prompt

At the end of a mission, ask:

**What worked only because someone overheld it?**

If your answer creates a cleaner handoff, mark Growth.

## Page 8: Linekeepers

Senior student leader: **Sera Voss**, outgoing Captain of the Bright Line, Linekeeper field lead for the Ash Market Walkout, permanently banned from three ceremonial debates and quietly invited back to all of them.

> SERA VOSS, LINEKEEPERS
>
> You will be told we are the House of confrontation.
>
> Fine. Sometimes a locked door is not opened by naming its history. Sometimes a hand must be removed from someone's shoulder. Sometimes the room asks for more time because time has always served the person causing harm.
>
> But if you came here to feel pure, leave.
>
> A line is not a weapon you swing to prove you are brave. A line is a place where you accept cost so someone else can have choice.

### First Impression

The Linekeeper quarter is called **the Threshold**.

It sits along the ship's outer training ring, where windows show stars, weather, docking lights, and the occasional alarming reminder that the ship is moving through a universe with consequences.

The walls are marked with lines: chalk, paint, scars, light, thread, tape, carved oaths, repaired cracks. Every line has a story. Some were drawn in training. Some were drawn after a mission when everyone agreed they should have been drawn sooner.

The air smells like stormglass, leather, old smoke, metal, and the sharp herbal oil used on bruises.

There are practice mats. There are quiet rooms. There are apology benches. The benches are used more often than first-years expect.

Above the entrance is written:

**A clean no protects the yes.**

Below it, in smaller letters:

**Stay for the cleanup.**

### House Culture

Linekeepers greet each other by asking, "Where is the line?"

This is not rhetorical. If you answer with posture instead of thought, a senior will make you start over.

Their initiation is called **the Doorway**. New students stand between two people in a staged conflict and must decide when to interrupt, when to listen, and when their own desire to be decisive has become part of the danger.

House slang:

- **bright line**: a boundary that protects life or consent
- **hot line**: a boundary drawn from unprocessed fear or rage
- **false peace**: quiet purchased by someone else's silence
- **cost named**: the moment after action when consequences become honest

Other Houses say Linekeepers enjoy making everyone uncomfortable.

Linekeepers say discomfort is not the same as harm, and if you confuse them, someone else usually pays.

Linekeepers interrupt harm.

They are boundary guardians, strike teams, interrupters, oathbreakers when the oath has become rotten, and people who know the room will call it rude when harm finally gets named.

Linekeepers are the House of decisive interruption.

They act when delay becomes complicity.

**Domain:** Direct Action

**Core Question:** What line must be drawn, and what will it cost?

**Motto:** A clean no protects the yes.

**Symbol:** A bright line across an open hand.

### Gift

Linekeepers can move when everyone else is waiting to feel ready.

They understand that some moments close if no one acts.

### Shadow

Linekeepers can become addicted to escalation.

They may confuse intensity with courage, purity with clarity, or confrontation with change.

Listen for:

- "If you are not with me, you are against me."
- "The cost proves it was real."
- "Burn it down."
- "They only understand force."

### Starting Move: Draw The Line

When you name a boundary that protects the work or interrupts harm, roll + Grow Up or + Show Up.

On a 10+, choose 2.

On a 7-9, choose 1 and name the cost.

On a 6-, the line is real, but your delivery creates a new repair need.

Options:

- Harm is interrupted now.
- Someone vulnerable gains room to choose.
- The real conflict becomes visible.
- You avoid unnecessary cruelty.
- Gain 1 Trust with someone who needed the boundary.

### House Feature: Stand In The Doorway

Once per episode, when another character would take a consequence from someone crossing a clear boundary, you may take that consequence instead.

If you do, ask the protected character:

**What do you need next, now that there is room?**

If they answer and you honor it, mark Growth.

### House Clock: Escalation Spiral

1. A boundary is softened to keep peace.
2. Harm repeats.
3. Anger gathers offscreen.
4. The first clear no arrives too hot.
5. Potential allies scatter.
6. The line holds, but repair becomes harder.

### Sera In Play

Sera is a Linekeeper because she can hear the click in a room when delay becomes permission.

On Pelagos Station, a governor's guard moves to arrest Seli during the coalition meeting. The room freezes. Everyone knows this is meant to break the meeting before it becomes public.

Sera steps between the guard and Seli and says, "No one is removed from this room without a named charge and a witness from each delegation."

That is **Draw the Line**.

On a 7-9, she chooses that harm is interrupted now. The cost is that the governor's office now treats the crew as agitators rather than observers.

That cost matters.

It does not mean Sera was wrong.

It means direct action has consequences even when it is clean.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> The Linekeeper fantasy is "I said the brave thing." The Linekeeper discipline is "I stayed afterward to help with what my brave thing changed."

### Advancement Prompt

At the end of a mission, ask:

**Where did I confuse force with clarity?**

If your answer leads to a repair or cleaner boundary, mark Growth.

## Page 9: Lanternbearers

Senior student leader: **Tavi Ren**, outgoing Keeper of the Low Flame, Lanternbearer field lead for the Mirror Choir Inquiry, once corrected the ship's official history of a mission while standing inside the mural commemorating it.

> TAVI REN, LANTERNBEARERS
>
> People think truth wants to be shouted.
>
> Sometimes it does.
>
> Sometimes truth wants a witness, a locked drawer, a name restored, a pattern made legible, a camera turned away, a public record corrected, or five more minutes before someone turns a living person into evidence.
>
> We do not make things visible because visibility is holy.
>
> We make things visible because hidden patterns govern the world, and because exposure without consent is another kind of theft.

### First Impression

The Lanternbearer quarter is called **the Low Flame**.

It is dimmer than the rest of the ship, not because Lanternbearers love mystery, though several absolutely do, but because not every truth survives floodlight.

The walls hold archives, listening booths, memory cabinets, signal mirrors, sealed testimonies, unfinished murals, banned posters, corrected captions, and recordings that can only be opened with the consent of the people inside them.

It smells like dust, citrus oil, old film, lamp-wax, and rain on glass.

There are windows everywhere. None of them show the same sky twice.

At the center is a lantern that is never allowed to become bright enough to hurt the eyes.

First-years are told this is symbolic.

Seniors know it is also practical.

### House Culture

Lanternbearers greet each other by asking, "Who owns the story?"

If the answer is "everyone," they ask again.

Their initiation is called **the Veil and the Window**. New students are given a true story and must decide what should be public, what should be witnessed privately, what should be archived, and who must be asked before any of it moves.

House slang:

- **hot signal**: a truth spreading faster than consent can travel
- **borrowed pain**: someone else's suffering used to make your point
- **clean witness**: attention that does not steal the center
- **pattern light**: visibility that reveals structure, not spectacle

Other Houses say Lanternbearers can turn a hallway rumor into a public reckoning.

Lanternbearers say yes, and that is exactly why they are so careful about hallway rumors.

Lanternbearers make truth visible.

They are witnesses, storytellers, signal keepers, archivists, journalists, mirror-workers, and people who know a hidden pattern can run a whole society until someone makes it legible.

Lanternbearers are the House of careful visibility.

They reveal what has been trained into silence.

**Domain:** Raise Awareness

**Core Question:** What truth must become visible, and with whose consent?

**Motto:** Make it visible. Do not make them a symbol.

**Symbol:** A lantern with an eye-shaped flame.

### Gift

Lanternbearers can name what everyone feels but no one has language for.

They understand the difference between witness and exposure.

### Shadow

Lanternbearers can turn truth into spectacle.

They may chase the dramatic reveal, overexpose people, confuse discourse with change, or use visibility as a substitute for support.

Listen for:

- "People need to know."
- "If we name it, we fixed it."
- "This story will move people."
- "Awareness is the work."

### Starting Move: Light The Pattern

When you make a hidden pattern visible, roll + Wake Up or + Show Up.

On a 10+, choose 2.

On a 7-9, choose 1 and ask who becomes more exposed.

On a 6-, the truth becomes visible, but the wrong person pays first.

Options:

- The pattern becomes undeniable.
- You protect the dignity of the affected.
- You identify who must consent before the story spreads.
- A false public story loses power.
- Gain 1 Clarity.

### House Feature: Consent Before Signal

Once per episode, before information becomes public, you may pause the table and ask:

**Who becomes more vulnerable if this becomes known?**

If the table changes course because of the answer, restore 1 Trust with an affected person or faction.

### House Clock: Spectacle Spiral

1. A hidden truth is sensed.
2. The most dramatic version spreads first.
3. Affected people lose control of the story.
4. Helpers gather around the visibility.
5. Material support lags behind attention.
6. The story becomes famous and the people become exhausted.

### Leor In Play

Leor is a Lanternbearer because he notices when a lie has become architecture.

On Pelagos Station, he finds records showing that the governor's office used the sabotage story to hide a pre-existing water diversion contract.

Leor wants to broadcast the records immediately.

The Guide asks, "Who becomes more vulnerable if this becomes known?"

Leor pauses. The records include Seli's maintenance credentials and the routes the engineers used to keep water flowing.

He uses **Consent Before Signal** and asks Seli what can be made public without exposing the engineers. Then he rolls **Light the Pattern** to make the governor's false story lose power.

The truth still matters.

So does who pays for it.

### Advancement Prompt

At the end of a mission, ask:

**Who became more visible, and did they choose it?**

If your answer protects consent in the next scene, mark Growth.

## Page 10: Rivalries And Alliances

The Houses disagree by design.

Provisioners and Weavers have argued for generations over whether the pantry inventory system is a sacred trust, a living document, or a cry for help.

Linekeepers and Lanternbearers have never fully settled whether naming harm before acting on it is courage, delay, or theater. The correct answer is "it depends," which has never satisfied either House.

Weavers respect Linekeepers when agreements are violated. They fear them when the bridge is burned before anyone checks whether people are still crossing it.

Lanternbearers need Provisioners so awareness leads to support. Provisioners need Lanternbearers so care does not become quiet disappearance.

Rivalry is playable tension.

It is not permission to make another player's character smaller.

Use rivalry when it helps the table ask:

- What necessary truth is my House protecting?
- What necessary truth am I avoiding?
- Who should I ask before acting?
- What would a rival House notice that I am missing?

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> If you use House rivalry to be tedious at the table, the Council of Joannes recommends every other player mark one imaginary Heat with you personally. This has no mechanical effect, but it will be spiritually accurate.

## Page 11: Cross-House Training

You may train with another House without changing your House.

This is encouraged.

The ship is old enough to know that purity makes students brittle.

Cross-House training becomes available when:

- you reach 2 Trust with another House
- a mission exposes a repeated blind spot
- a teacher from another House offers a challenge
- a campaign packet grants a House training opportunity
- a player wants their student to grow beyond their default method

To begin cross-House training, answer:

1. Which House has something you need to learn?
2. Which teacher, rival, or peer will not let you learn it cheaply?
3. What habit from your own House will get in the way?
4. What Basic Move will this training most often change?

Cross-House training may grant:

- a temporary move card
- an extra option on a House move
- a once-per-episode question
- permission to reduce Heat with that House through field practice
- access to a House contact

Cross-House training should never erase the original House.

It should make the student more complicated and more useful.

## Page 12: House Duties During Missions

Before a mission begins, the table may assign House duties.

This is especially useful for campaign packets, convention play, and tables that enjoy a little machinery before the emotional weather arrives.

Each duty is a question someone must keep alive.

Provisioner duty:

**What resource or capacity must be tracked?**

Use when the mission involves scarcity, exhaustion, access, logistics, care work, money, food, shelter, medicine, transportation, language, or time.

Weaver duty:

**What structure or handoff must exist?**

Use when the mission involves coalitions, meetings, divided responsibility, fragile agreements, governance, plans, or invisible labor.

Linekeeper duty:

**What line must not be crossed?**

Use when the mission involves coercion, unsafe compromise, intimidation, exploitation, boundary violation, or a pattern of delay.

Lanternbearer duty:

**What truth must be handled with consent?**

Use when the mission involves secrecy, propaganda, public narrative, hidden patterns, witness, archives, testimony, or exposure.

No one has to hold the duty alone.

If a duty becomes too heavy, Open Up.

Ask for help. Name the pressure. Let the crew carry the question with you.

## Page 13: House Advancement

At the end of an episode, review the Houses.

Do not make this a performance review. The ship already has enough forms that ask students to convert spiritual growth into boxes, and several of them have begun reproducing in the aft archive.

Ask:

1. Which House question mattered most this episode?
2. Which House shadow caused trouble?
3. Which House gained Trust with a student, crew, NPC, or faction?
4. Which House gained Heat?
5. Did any House clock advance?
6. Did anyone earn Growth through a House advancement prompt?

Then update:

- Student Records
- House Trust / Heat trackers
- active House clocks
- campaign Chronicle
- open House NPC threads

### House Advancement Prompts

Provisioners:

**Who needed less admiration and more support?**

Weavers:

**What worked only because someone overheld it?**

Linekeepers:

**Where did I confuse force with clarity?**

Lanternbearers:

**Who became more visible, and did they choose it?**

If your answer changes future action, marks repair, creates a cleaner practice, or protects someone in the next scene, mark Growth.

## Page 14: Using Houses In Campaign Packets

Campaign packets should make the Houses matter.

A campaign does not need to feature all four Houses equally in every episode. It should, however, give each House a way to become relevant over time.

For each campaign packet, define:

- featured House
- pressure House
- House NPCs
- House Trust opportunities
- House Heat risks
- House clock triggers
- House-specific mission rewards
- cross-House training opportunities

Example:

In a water scarcity campaign, the featured House might be Provisioners because material access is central. The pressure House might be Lanternbearers because making the theft visible could expose the people secretly keeping the neighborhood alive.

This gives the campaign a spine:

- resources must move
- truth must be handled carefully
- structure must eventually hold the work
- direct action must decide where delay becomes complicity

That is enough engine to sell campaign content.

It is also enough engine to make the table feel that the book is not merely explaining allyship.

It is letting them encounter it.
