## Page 1: Your Admission Record

The Headmaster claims the ship admits students when "the work recognizes its unfinished instrument."

This is one of those statements that sounds profound until someone has to process housing assignments, meal access, deck permissions, field liability, and whether the student in question is likely to set fire to the Bridge tea rooms while attempting to prove they are emotionally available.

In practical terms: the ship noticed you before you noticed it.

It opened a door.

You came through.

That is enough for admission.

The ship does not accept the version of you that is already useful. It accepts the version that can still be trained.

Your first task is not to become impressive. Your first task is to make a record honest enough that the crew can play with you.

Take a Student Record. If your table is using a shared document, open your page. If your table is using paper, write clearly enough that someone can read your name after the first mission bell. This is not always as easy as it sounds.

Answer the following before choosing House, School, Role, or stats.

### Admission Prompts

Answer at least three.

You may choose prompts that already pull at you, or you may roll.

Choosing is introspection.

Rolling is provocation.

Both are valid. The ship is not sentimental about how a true answer arrives.

- What did the ship notice before you did?
- Who argued that you were ready enough?
- Who worried the school was making a mistake?
- What rumor followed you aboard?
- What did you bring that you have not unpacked?
- What part of you relaxed when the doors closed?
- Which room aboard the ship did you find before anyone gave you a map?

### Rollable Admission Tables

For each question you answer, you may write freely or roll 1d6 on that question's table.

If the result is close but not exact, alter it. The admission office is not a court of law. It is, at best, a corridor with filing cabinets and opinions.

## Page 2: What A Student Is

A student is not a hero class.

A student is a person under training, which is less glamorous and far more dangerous to the ego.

Each student has nine parts the manual can responsibly name:

- **Gift:** what arrived before training
- **House:** what kind of work claimed you
- **School:** how the ship trains your mastery
- **Role:** how you function in the crew
- **Belief:** the false story pressure activates
- **Offer:** the help you reflexively bring
- **Cost:** what your help spends
- **Line:** what your help must not become
- **Bond:** why the work has stakes

These are not separate boxes in your soul. They are handles.

The field will not ask whether your sheet is elegant. It will ask what your student does when a council thanks the wrong people, a room goes quiet after the truth is named, a teammate mistakes urgency for permission, or someone offers you care you did not earn by suffering first.

The purpose of character creation is to give the table enough handles to answer.

## Page 3: Quick Build Or Full Admission

There are two approved routes through admission.

The first is **Quick Build**.

Use Quick Build for one-shots, demos, public tables, late arrivals, tired evenings, or groups that wisely suspect they will learn more by playing than by composing beautiful biographies under fluorescent pressure.

The second is **Full Admission**.

Use Full Admission for campaigns, long arcs, school drama, crew entanglement, and tables that enjoy discovering which teacher has already formed a strong and probably unfair opinion about them.

### Quick Build

To create a student quickly:

1. Choose name, pronouns, age band, look, and personal object.
2. Choose one Allyship Gift.
3. Choose one House.
4. Choose Home School only.
5. Assign stats.
6. Choose one Ensemble Role.
7. Choose one Self-Sabotaging Belief.
8. Define either Offer, Cost, Line, or Bond.
9. Ask one relationship question.

You can fill in the rest during play.

### Full Admission

To complete Full Admission, work through every page in this chapter in order.

Answer what is useful. Skip what does not yet have a true answer. Return later when the ship, the crew, or your own poor decisions have clarified the matter.

Write each answer on your Student Record as instructed.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> If someone says "we should all do Full Admission" and then stares at you while you invent a traumatic backstory, call Pause. Depth is not a tax.

## Page 4: Name, Vibe, And Arrival

Begin with the person.

Choose:

- name
- pronouns
- age band
- look
- personal object
- what people notice first

Age bands:

- younger student
- older student
- transfer
- returning dropout
- reluctant prodigy
- late-blooming adult student

The school accepts people when the work calls them, not when a neat age bracket says the paperwork would be less irritating.

Choose one arrival detail:

- You arrived during a storm drill.
- You arrived through the wrong door and were officially "misplaced" for three hours.
- You arrived with someone who did not stay.
- You arrived carrying evidence from a crisis nobody believed.
- You arrived because the ship owed your community a debt.
- You arrived laughing and stopped when you saw the memorial hall.
- You arrived without knowing you had been admitted.

Then answer:

- Who noticed you first?
- What rumor followed you aboard?
- What have you not unpacked?
- What do people get wrong about you after one conversation?

Write your name, pronouns, age band, look, object, first impression, and arrival rumor on your Student Record.

## Page 5: Allyship Gift

Your Allyship Gift is what came before training.

It may be a talent. It may be a survival adaptation. It may be something you learned because nobody else in the room was doing it. It may be useful. It may also be overused, mistrusted, or pointed at the wrong problem because it once kept you safe.

Your Gift is not your destiny.

Your School is how the ship trains it, contradicts it, disciplines it, or teaches you not to confuse instinct with wisdom.

Choose one Gift.

### Signal Gift

You notice emotional weather before other people do.

Choose this if your student reads rooms, silences, tension, consent, fear, or the feeling under the official story.

Starting edge:

Once per episode, ask: "What is the room trying not to feel?"

Risk:

You may confuse noticing with helping.

Ask yourself:

What signal did you learn to read too early?

Write **Signal Gift** and its starting edge on your Student Record.

### Spark Gift

You make stuck things move.

Choose this if your student creates openings, starts conversations, interrupts stasis, or gives the crew momentum when everyone is waiting for permission.

Starting edge:

Once per episode, when the group hesitates, you may create an opening for action.

Risk:

You may move before the field has consented to be moved.

Ask yourself:

Who taught you that waiting could become its own harm?

Write **Spark Gift** and its starting edge on your Student Record.

### Shelter Gift

People steady themselves around you.

Choose this if your student grounds, comforts, feeds, protects, tends, or makes a room feel survivable.

Starting edge:

Once per episode, help another character recover 1 Capacity through care, grounding, or practical support.

Risk:

You may become the place everyone rests while no one asks what you need.

Ask yourself:

What do people assume you can carry?

Write **Shelter Gift** and its starting edge on your Student Record.

### Pattern Gift

You see the structure behind the visible problem.

Choose this if your student notices systems, incentives, missing handoffs, repeated failures, and the machinery behind the mess.

Starting edge:

Once per episode, ask: "What keeps making this happen?"

Risk:

You may fall in love with the diagram and forget the people inside it.

Ask yourself:

What pattern do you keep seeing because it once hurt you?

Write **Pattern Gift** and its starting edge on your Student Record.

### Bridge Gift

You can stay connected across difference.

Choose this if your student translates, mediates, hosts, listens, repairs, or helps people remain in contact without pretending conflict has vanished.

Starting edge:

Once per episode, ask: "What does each side need protected?"

Risk:

You may confuse staying connected with staying too long.

Ask yourself:

When did connection cost you more than anyone noticed?

Write **Bridge Gift** and its starting edge on your Student Record.

### Horizon Gift

You see the larger game.

Choose this if your student names context, reads power, tracks long arcs, sees strategy, or asks what story the field thinks it is in.

Starting edge:

Once per episode, ask: "What game are we actually playing?"

Risk:

You may use altitude as escape.

Ask yourself:

What do you understand from far away that you avoid up close?

Write **Horizon Gift** and its starting edge on your Student Record.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Gift is not destiny. It is the thing you will try first even when the situation is begging you to try third.

### Running Example: Mara Venn

Mara chooses **Shelter Gift**.

Her starting edge lets her help another character recover 1 Capacity through care, grounding, or practical support once per episode.

Her risk is becoming the place everyone rests while no one asks what she needs.

## Page 6: Choose A House

Your House is your practice faction aboard the ship.

It is not a personality test, despite first-years producing personality-test behavior with the reliability of mold in the south archives.

Your House asks what kind of allyship work claims your attention first.

Choose one.

### Provisioners

Provisioners move resources.

Choose the Provisioners if your eyes go first to the empty hands, the locked cabinet, the missing translator, the exhausted organizer, the meal nobody planned, the transport nobody funded, or the cost everyone keeps calling logistics.

Provisioners know every crisis has a material body.

You gain:

- the Provisioners' starting move
- one friend or rival in the House
- +1 Trust or +1 Heat with another House

House shadow:

Turning care into possession.

Ask yourself:

What do you keep offering because you are afraid to ask what is actually needed?

Write **Provisioners** on your Student Record.

### Weavers

Weavers build structure.

Choose the Weavers if you notice the missing agenda, the invisible labor, the brittle coalition, the room with no handoff, the plan everyone praises but nobody can follow, or the recurring meeting that has become a shrine to avoidance.

Weavers know a good impulse without a container becomes another burden.

You gain:

- the Weavers' starting move
- one friend or rival in the House
- +1 Trust or +1 Heat with another House

House shadow:

Mistaking structure for contact.

Ask yourself:

What kind of mess makes you reach for a system before you reach for a person?

Write **Weavers** on your Student Record.

### Linekeepers

Linekeepers interrupt harm.

Choose the Linekeepers if you feel the moment when waiting becomes complicity, when a clean no is kinder than a delayed consensus, or when the room needs someone to draw the line and accept the cost of being visible.

Linekeepers know false peace is not peace.

You gain:

- the Linekeepers' starting move
- one friend or rival in the House
- +1 Trust or +1 Heat with another House

House shadow:

Confusing force with clarity.

Ask yourself:

What are you protecting when you say no?

Write **Linekeepers** on your Student Record.

### Lanternbearers

Lanternbearers make truth visible.

Choose the Lanternbearers if you notice what has been hidden, misnamed, sanitized, buried, over-managed, or thanked into silence.

Lanternbearers know attention changes the field.

You gain:

- the Lanternbearers' starting move
- one friend or rival in the House
- +1 Trust or +1 Heat with another House

House shadow:

Turning pain into spectacle.

Ask yourself:

Whose consent matters before a truth becomes public?

Write **Lanternbearers** on your Student Record.

After choosing your House, answer:

- Why did this House claim you?
- Which House trusts you for the wrong reason?
- Which House worries about what you might become?

### Running Example: Mara Venn

Mara chooses **Provisioners** because she notices depletion before speeches, principles, or chain of command.

Her House contact is a rival in the Weavers who thinks Mara confuses urgency with logistics.

The Weavers trust her practical competence for the wrong reason. The Lanternbearers worry she will protect people so thoroughly that nobody hears what happened to them.

## Page 7: Choose Your School Triangle

The Schools are horizontal paths of mastery.

No School outranks another. Each teaches a discipline the ship believes students will need before they are old enough, wise enough, or calm enough to use it cleanly.

Choose three School relationships:

- **Home School:** the School training you now
- **Curiosity School:** the School calling to some part of you
- **Avoided School:** the School you distrust, misunderstand, or would prefer not to need

Your Home School starts at **Initiate**.

Your Curiosity School gives you one friendly lieutenant or open invitation.

Your Avoided School gives the Guide permission to challenge your assumptions.

### The Schools

| School | Owned Stat | Core Question |
|---|---|---|
| School of the Body | Sense | What is happening beneath the stated story? |
| School of the Line | Act | What needs to happen now? |
| School of the Oath | Steady | What can you remain with without collapsing or performing? |
| School of the Pattern | Shape | What structure would make right action easier? |
| School of the Bridge | Tend | What does care require materially or relationally? |
| School of the Horizon | Speak | What needs to be said, and how cleanly can it be said? |

Answer:

- What did your Home School see in you?
- Why does your Curiosity School fascinate you?
- What do you think your Avoided School gets wrong?
- Which lieutenant from your Curiosity School has left their door open to you?

Write your Home School, Curiosity School, Avoided School, and lieutenant contact on your Student Record.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Your Avoided School is not "the one you are bad at." It is the one you are currently telling stories about. The ship loves correcting stories. It is rude that way.

### Running Example: Mara Venn

Mara chooses:

- Home School: School of the Bridge
- Curiosity School: School of the Pattern
- Avoided School: School of the Horizon

The Bridge saw that Mara can make a room survivable. The Pattern fascinates her because she wants support systems that do not depend on one exhausted person noticing everything. She thinks the Horizon gets too much credit for naming truths after other people have already paid for them.

## Page 8: Assign Stats

Stats are the six ways the Schools teach students to meet pressure.

They are not virtues.

A high stat does not mean you are good. A low stat does not mean you are doomed. Stats tell the table what your student trusts, what they reach for, and where training still has work to do.

Assign:

- +2 to one stat
- +1 to two stats
- 0 to two stats
- -1 to one stat

| Stat | Owning School | Use It When You... |
|---|---|---|
| **Sense** | School of the Body | read the room, notice emotion, track consent, feel the field |
| **Act** | School of the Line | intervene, interrupt, follow through, take visible action |
| **Steady** | School of the Oath | stay present, regulate, keep commitments, hold a line |
| **Shape** | School of the Pattern | organize, design, map systems, build containers |
| **Tend** | School of the Bridge | repair, support, resource, care, reduce burden |
| **Speak** | School of the Horizon | name truth, ask hard questions, translate perspective |

Your Home School suggests a strong stat, but does not force it.

Your House may suggest likely strengths:

- Provisioners often favor Sense, Tend, or Act.
- Weavers often favor Shape, Tend, or Sense.
- Linekeepers often favor Act, Steady, or Speak.
- Lanternbearers often favor Speak, Sense, or Act.

Your Belief often marks the stat you distrust.

Ask:

- What do you trust under pressure?
- What do you avoid under pressure?
- Which stat does your School train that you still resist?

Examples:

- High Sense / low Speak: you feel the room before you can name it cleanly.
- High Act / low Tend: you can move under pressure, but may miss what support requires.
- High Shape / low Steady: you can build the plan, but struggle to remain when the plan disappoints someone.

Write all six stats on your Student Record.

### Running Example: Mara Venn

Mara assigns:

- Sense +1
- Act 0
- Steady +1
- Shape 0
- Tend +2
- Speak -1

Mara trusts care when pressure rises. She can read depletion and move support, but public truth-telling makes her feel like she is turning someone else's need into a spectacle.

## Page 9: Choose Ensemble Role

Your Ensemble Role names how the crew tends to use you when pressure rises.

It is not your class.

Roles do not need to be unique. This is fortunate, because no known crew has ever distributed itself according to administrative preference.

Two Sparks mean the crew may move quickly and argue over who created the opening. Two Anchors mean the crew may stabilize beautifully and then spend twenty minutes mistaking hesitation for care. Notice overlaps. Notice gaps. Do not fix them too quickly. The mess is playable.

If your table likes TV ensemble language, think of these as Ace, Leader, Engineer, Rookie, and Veteran translated into school-ship practice.

Choose one Role.

### Spark: The One Who Catalyzes Action

Function:

You do the impossible-looking thing.

Team gift:

When the mission needs bold competence, you step forward.

Risk:

You make the work about proving you can.

Starting move:

**Flash of Brilliance:** Once per episode, when you take a risky action using your strongest approach, add +1. On a miss, your brilliance draws attention or creates a new cost.

Ask the table:

Who has seen you turn courage into performance?

Write **Spark** and its starting move on your Student Record.

### Anchor: The One Who Orients The Crew

Function:

You orient the team.

Team gift:

When people scatter, you name the direction.

Risk:

You confuse responsibility with control.

Starting move:

**Name the Direction:** Once per episode, when the group is divided, name the shared objective. If everyone accepts it, each player takes +1 forward when acting toward it.

Ask the table:

Who trusts you to steady the room, and who worries you will decide for them?

Write **Anchor** and its starting move on your Student Record.

### Builder: The One Who Makes Insight Usable

Function:

You solve with tools, systems, and applied knowledge.

Team gift:

You turn insight into something usable.

Risk:

You design instead of feeling.

Starting move:

**Useful Apparatus:** Once per episode, describe a tool, plan, method, or structure you prepared. It works, but the Guide names one limitation.

Ask the table:

Who has benefited from your structure, and who has felt hidden inside it?

Write **Builder** and its starting move on your Student Record.

### Scout: The One Who Notices First

Function:

You ask the obvious question no one else will ask.

Team gift:

You bring freshness, heart, and disruptive honesty.

Risk:

You rush toward meaning before understanding cost.

Starting move:

**Beginner's Question:** Once per episode, ask a plain question that cuts through expert fog. If the table answers honestly, gain 1 Clarity.

Ask the table:

Who is relieved when you ask the obvious question, and who gets nervous?

Write **Scout** and its starting move on your Student Record.

### Witness: The One Who Carries Memory

Function:

You have been through something like this before.

Team gift:

You steady the team when pressure rises.

Risk:

You mistake past survival for present wisdom.

Starting move:

**I've Seen This Before:** Once per episode, say what this reminds you of. The Guide tells you one way this is similar and one way it is dangerously different.

Ask the table:

Who wants your perspective, and who worries you have stopped being surprised?

Write **Witness** and its starting move on your Student Record.

After everyone chooses a Role, answer two:

- Who usually moves first?
- Who notices the cost?
- Who turns insight into a plan?
- Who says the thing everyone is avoiding?
- Who can make you stop performing?

> "Crew function" is the polite term. The impolite term is "what everyone starts expecting from you until you make them stop."

Mara chooses **Anchor**.

Her starting move is **Name the Direction**.

She can steady a crew that is scattering, but she risks confusing responsibility with control. Jun trusts her to steady the room and worries she will decide what support should look like before anyone has named it.

## Page 10: Choose Your Belief

Your Self-Sabotaging Belief is the false story pressure activates.

It is not a diagnosis. It is not a moral flaw. It is not an invitation for the table to punish you with your own psychology, which has been attempted before and is now forbidden under three separate protocols.

Your Belief probably helped you survive something.

It also distorts allyship when the field gets loud.

Choose one.

### I'm Not Good Enough

What it sounds like:

"If I cannot do this well, I should not do it where anyone can see."

How it helped:

It may have kept you practicing, improving, and noticing standards other people ignored.

How it distorts allyship:

You perform competence or disappear instead of making the useful imperfect move.

Trigger:

Someone evaluates your contribution.

Temptation:

Perform competence or disappear.

Wound move:

When you compare yourself and act from shame, take 1 Tension.

Growth move:

When you make a useful imperfect move anyway, mark Growth.

Relationship prompt:

Who has seen you try to vanish after almost helping?

Write the Belief, trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move on your Student Record.

### I'm Not Ready

What it sounds like:

"I need one more plan before I can act."

How it helped:

It may have kept you from confusing adrenaline with wisdom.

How it distorts allyship:

You delay a needed move until delay becomes part of the harm.

Trigger:

The moment asks for action before you feel prepared.

Temptation:

Delay, study, plan, or ask for one more sign.

Wound move:

When you postpone a needed move for more preparation, advance the Mission Clock.

Growth move:

When you act with partial readiness and name what support you need, mark Growth.

Relationship prompt:

Who can tell when your planning has become hiding?

Write the Belief, trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move on your Student Record.

### I Don't Belong

What it sounds like:

"If they knew the real shape of me, the room would close."

How it helped:

It may have taught you to read norms, survive exclusion, and notice the cost of entry.

How it distorts allyship:

You over-adapt, self-exile, or test whether people will keep you.

Trigger:

The room's norms make you feel outside the circle.

Temptation:

Over-adapt, self-exile, or test whether people will keep you.

Wound move:

When you hide your real position to stay included, take 1 Tension.

Growth move:

When you stay present without erasing yourself, mark Growth.

Relationship prompt:

Who notices when you start becoming acceptable instead of present?

Write the Belief, trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move on your Student Record.

### I'm Insignificant

What it sounds like:

"Nothing I do will matter unless it is dramatic enough to count."

How it helped:

It may have protected you from false hope.

How it distorts allyship:

You dismiss small useful moves or chase the gesture that finally proves you matter.

Trigger:

The problem feels too large for your action to matter.

Temptation:

Collapse into cynicism or chase a dramatic gesture.

Wound move:

When you dismiss a small useful move because it is not grand enough, lose 1 Clarity.

Growth move:

When you make the small move that changes the next thing, mark Growth.

Relationship prompt:

Who has seen one small thing you did change the field?

Write the Belief, trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move on your Student Record.

### I'm Not Worthy

What it sounds like:

"If I receive this, I must repay it until nobody can regret giving it."

How it helped:

It may have kept you humble, careful, or aware that trust is precious.

How it distorts allyship:

You turn care into debt and responsibility into a trial.

Trigger:

You receive care, trust, praise, or responsibility.

Temptation:

Deflect, overpay, sabotage, or prove you deserve it.

Wound move:

When you turn receiving into debt, take 1 Tension.

Growth move:

When you receive support without making it a trial, mark Growth.

Relationship prompt:

Who keeps offering you help you do not know how to receive cleanly?

Write the Belief, trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move on your Student Record.

### I'm Not Capable

What it sounds like:

"The task has revealed the truth: someone else should do this."

How it helped:

It may have kept you from pretending mastery you did not have.

How it distorts allyship:

You abandon your agency before asking for support.

Trigger:

The task reveals a skill gap.

Temptation:

Freeze, outsource, joke, or let someone else take over.

Wound move:

When you abandon your agency before asking for help, lose 1 Capacity.

Growth move:

When you ask for help and still keep one piece of the work, mark Growth.

Relationship prompt:

Who can help without taking the whole task from you?

Write the Belief, trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move on your Student Record.

When she receives care, trust, praise, or responsibility, she wants to deflect, overpay, sabotage, or prove she deserves it.

Her wound move: when she turns receiving into debt, take 1 Tension.

Her growth move: when she receives support without making it a trial, mark Growth.

## Page 11: Offer, Cost, Line, Bond

These are the four sentences every student carries into the field.

They are character features with moves attached.

### Offer

Your Offer is the help you reflexively bring.

Examples:

- advice
- protection
- structure
- humor
- research
- money
- confrontation
- emotional presence
- public witness

Offer Move:

When your Offer matches a named need, take +1 forward.

When you offer it before asking what is needed, take 1 Tension.

Ask:

What do you give before you know whether it is wanted?

Write your Offer and Offer Move on your Student Record.

### Cost

Your Cost is what you spend too freely.

Examples:

- energy
- status
- certainty
- money
- sleep
- belonging
- attention
- safety
- patience
- dignity

Cost Move:

When you name your Cost before paying it, gain 1 Adversity.

When you ignore your Cost, the Guide may make it a complication.

Ask:

What does your help spend before you admit it is spending you?

Write your Cost and Cost Move on your Student Record.

### Line

Your Line is what you will not do, even for a good cause.

Examples:

- lie for the institution
- turn someone into a symbol
- abandon a teammate
- make promises for someone else
- use private pain as public leverage
- keep peace by hiding harm

Line Move:

When you hold your Line under pressure, take +1 forward to Grow Up or Show Up.

When you cross your Line, mark Growth only if you name the impact and seek repair.

Ask:

What must your help never become?

Write your Line and Line Move on your Student Record.

### Bond

Your Bond is who or what gives your training stakes.

Examples:

- another student
- a teacher
- a sibling
- a lost home
- a community
- a promise
- a former rival
- someone harmed by your old pattern

Bond Move:

When your Bond is directly at stake, you may reroll one die. If the reroll misses, the Bond changes.

Ask:

Who or what makes this lesson stop being theoretical?

Write your Bond and Bond Move on your Student Record.

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Put Offer in ink. Especially if your Offer is advice. Actually, underline it if your Offer is advice.

## Page 12: Crew Relationships

Before the first mission, make the crew real enough to disappoint each other interestingly.

Each player asks:

- one trust question
- one concern question
- one need question

You may ask the same player more than one question, but try to spread the web. The ship can function with isolated geniuses only in Headmaster speeches, and even there it is pushing credibility.

### Trust Questions

- What did I do that made you trust me?
- What do you think I am better at than I believe?
- What promise did we make together?

### Concern Questions

- What did I do that made you hesitate?
- What do you think I avoid?
- What would you ask me not to do again?

### Need Questions

- What do you need from me when pressure rises?
- What should I never assume about you?
- When I am acting from my Belief, what sign might you notice first?

Answers become table truth unless both players revise them.

Write relationship answers on your Student Record and the Crew Sheet.

### Running Example: Mara Venn

Tavi trusts Mara because Mara once noticed they had stopped eating before Tavi noticed it themselves.

Jun asks Mara not to decide what support people need before they name it.

Mara needs someone to tell her when care has become control.

>

>

>

After each introduction, one other player asks a relationship question.

### Mara In Play

During a mission, Mara sees three local organizers managing a water distribution line while a councilor thanks the ship from a stage. Her first instinct is to bring the organizers food, shade, and another set of hands.

That is a good Offer.

It is not automatically the right Offer.

If Mara asks what they need and they name relief, she can use her Offer cleanly. If she starts organizing their table before asking, she takes 1 Tension because she has turned care into control.

Later, when a student thanks Mara for catching a mistake in the supply plan, her Belief triggers. She wants to deflect, overwork, and prove the trust was not misplaced.

If she receives the thanks and lets someone else take the next shift, she marks Growth.

This is not because she has become pure.

It is because she stayed in contact with what the moment required.
