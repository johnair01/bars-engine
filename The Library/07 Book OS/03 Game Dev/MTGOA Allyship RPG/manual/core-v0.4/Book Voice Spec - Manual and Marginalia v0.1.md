# Book Voice Spec - Manual and Marginalia v0.1

Status: voice and style spec

Purpose: define the two competing voices of the MTGOA RPG manual so future chapter drafts can be generated consistently.

Core premise:

The book is not written in one voice.

It is an official school-ship manual written by a Regent on behalf of a Headmaster who could not be bothered to translate his Sage-level desires for the school into actual procedure.

The manual has then been annotated by a student representative of the Council of Joannes: snide, observant, allergic to institutional bullshit, and unexpectedly committed to making sure the next student survives the curriculum.

The main text teaches the game. The marginalia cuts through the main text when it becomes too polished, too evasive, too self-important, or too pleased with its own wisdom.

## The Two Voices

## Voice 1: The Regent's Official Manual

The official manual is written by a Regent.

They are writing on behalf of the Headmaster, who has a grand vision for the school, a troubling habit of speaking in altitude metaphors, and little interest in producing the actual manual students need.

The Regent has therefore done the work.

They believe in the school. They are also fully aware that the ship is an ecologically sound, pedagogically ambitious, structurally impossible, and quite madcap place that should probably not function as well as it does.

It is:

- ceremonial
- clear
- field-tested
- direct
- humane
- quietly magical
- operational
- morally serious without being moralizing
- wry
- genre-savvy
- administratively overburdened
- fond of the institution in the way one is fond of a machine that should have exploded years ago

It should feel like a school handbook, field guide, secret curriculum, ship operations manual, and tabletop RPG manual braided together by someone with excellent judgment and a permanent headache.

It knows the work matters.

It does not beg the reader to agree.

It also does not pretend the ship is normal.

### What The Official Manual Believes

The Regent's manual believes:

- readiness is not the price of admission
- help has consequences
- intention does not erase impact
- repair matters more than reputation
- pressure reveals training
- the table needs handles, not lectures
- fantasy can carry real practice safely
- a student is not good because they meant well
- a student becomes useful by staying in contact with what their moves do
- the Headmaster's vision is probably correct and absolutely unusable until translated into steps
- the ship's madness is survivable when properly labeled
- a procedure is a kindness when everyone is frightened

### Official Manual Tone

Use:

- short declarative lines for principles
- sensory detail before abstraction
- direct address when recruiting the reader
- procedure when teaching the table
- poetic compression for Schools, Houses, Beliefs, and milestones
- concrete examples for mechanics
- dry institutional asides
- operational warnings about impossible ship spaces
- restrained jokes that reveal the world's absurdity
- wry genre awareness without collapsing into parody

Avoid:

- therapy workbook language
- corporate DEI phrasing
- excessive lore exposition
- smug wisdom
- parody academia
- generic fantasy grandeur
- telling the reader they are morally good
- making allyship sound clean
- making every sentence solemn
- sanding off the ship's strangeness
- sounding like the Headmaster wrote it personally

### Official Manual Sentence Shape

The official voice often uses:

- one strong claim
- one concrete image
- one consequence
- one procedural instruction

Example:

> The Line training rooms only open after a student names what they are protecting. If you cannot name the person, promise, or boundary behind your no, the room keeps its doors shut.

Another example:

> Do not roll to discover whether your student is good. Roll when their move could change trust, safety, resources, consent, pressure, belonging, leverage, or the mission.

Regent-flavored example:

> The Headmaster originally described the first semester as "a spiral of necessary unmasking." This was not helpful. For scheduling purposes, we have divided it into six ranks, three field assessments, and one supervised apology.

Another Regent-flavored example:

> The ship produces no waste, which is admirable. It does, however, produce rumors, procedural exceptions, and at least four kinds of tea that appear only when someone is lying to themselves.

## Voice 2: Student Marginalia

The marginalia is the voice of a student who has annotated the manual.

Specifically, the annotator may be a representative of the **Council of Joannes**, a semi-formal student body whose exact authority depends on whom you ask, what deck you are standing on, and whether the Regent has had breakfast.

It is not random jokes in the margin.

It is a second narrative layer.

The marginalia should make the reader feel:

- someone has used this book before
- the school is real enough to complain about
- the official text is useful but incomplete
- students gossip, doubt, resist, misunderstand, grow, and leave traces
- the institution has contradictions
- the curriculum is survivable, but not cute

### Marginalia Core Identity

Default annotator:

**A Council of Joannes representative writing for the next student who finds the book.**

They are older than a first-year but not fully at peace. They have survived missions, failed publicly, loved the ship, resented the ship, served on enough committees to identify bullshit by smell, and learned when the official manual is technically correct but emotionally unhelpful.

Working name placeholder:

- "J."

Do not lock the name until character voice is tested.

### Marginalia Tone

The marginalia is:

- candid
- funny, but not meme-y
- affectionate toward the ship
- suspicious of institutional certainty
- emotionally specific
- occasionally embarrassed
- practical in a lived-in way
- willing to contradict the official text
- snide when the manual is evasive
- protective when a student is likely to blame themselves
- politically alert
- allergic to noble-sounding vagueness

It should sound like a student writing in the margins for someone who might need the truth sooner than the curriculum provides it.

It should not sound like:

- a modern internet comment section
- pure snark
- a joke machine
- a second GM voice
- an omniscient critic
- a lore dump
- a villain
- a nihilist
- a pure cynic

### What The Marginalia Believes

The annotator believes:

- the manual is useful
- the manual leaves things out
- teachers are people with patterns
- Houses are social worlds, not sorting hats
- Schools teach what they also fail at
- nobody uses safety tools perfectly the first time
- every student thinks their Belief is special
- the ship saves people and also asks too much
- the official version of a lesson is often cleaner than the lived version
- bullshit is not the same as mystery
- some official vagueness protects power
- some official vagueness protects students
- telling the difference is part of the curriculum

### Marginalia Jobs

Marginalia should do one of seven jobs:

1. **Clarify**
   - explain a rule in plainer student language
2. **Complicate**
   - reveal the official rule has social consequences
3. **Warn**
   - name a common mistake
4. **Humanize**
   - make the school feel inhabited
5. **Foreshadow**
   - hint at deeper curriculum, future chapters, or hidden rooms
6. **Gossip**
   - add rumors about teachers, Houses, prior missions, or ship spaces
7. **Confess**
   - admit how the annotator misunderstood the lesson

Every marginal note should have a job.

Do not add marginalia only for flavor.

## Relationship Between The Voices

The two voices should create productive tension.

The official manual says what the school means to teach.

The marginalia says how the lesson felt when someone actually had to live it.

The Regent writes with procedure, restraint, and wry institutional competence.

The Council annotator writes with suspicion, real-talk, and the intimacy of someone who knows which corridors flood when the ship is embarrassed.

The official manual says:

> The table may call Pause whenever a scene becomes too much, too close, too fast, or simply not what the group came to play.

The marginalia might add:

> I waited three sessions before using this because I thought needing it meant I was bad at the game. It did not. It meant the scene had teeth.

The official manual says:

> Your Offer is useful. Your Offer is not always what is needed.

The marginalia might add:

> Put this on your sheet in ink. Especially if your Offer is advice. Actually, underline it if your Offer is advice.

The official manual says:

> The School of the Horizon teaches students to see the game and still stay in it.

The marginalia might add:

> Horizon students love the phrase "larger pattern" until someone asks them to wash cups after the meeting.

## Marginalia Frequency

Use marginalia sparingly enough that it feels desirable.

Recommended density:

- chapter opener: 1 marginal note
- major rule section: 1 note
- each House / School / Role / Belief spread: 1 note maximum
- examples of play: 1 clarifying or confessional note
- never more than 2 marginal notes on a page unless the page is designed as an annotated artifact

If every paragraph has a joke, the book loses gravity.

If no margins speak, the book loses intimacy.

## Marginalia Formatting

Use a consistent marker in draft text.

Suggested markdown format:

```md
> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Put this in ink. Especially if your Offer is advice.
```

When the annotator crosses something out or corrects the manual, use it rarely:

```md
> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> "No one arrives ready" is true. "No one arrives dramatic about it" is false.
```

Do not overuse fake handwriting effects in source drafts. Save visual treatment for layout.

## Official Voice Examples By Section

## Chapter Openers

Official voice should be cinematic, initiatory, and Regent-filtered.

Example:

> The admission office has no fixed door.
>
> Some students find it at the end of a corridor they were sure ended yesterday. Some wake to a bell they have never heard and follow it through rain. Some are brought by teachers, rivals, siblings, debts, accidents, or the kind of need that makes maps honest.
>
> The ship does not accept the version of you that is already useful.
>
> It accepts the version that can still be trained.

Regent variant:

> The Headmaster claims the ship admits students when "the work recognizes its unfinished instrument."
>
> This is one of those statements that sounds profound until someone has to process housing assignments.
>
> In practical terms: the ship noticed you before you noticed it. It opened a door. You came through.
>
> That is enough for admission.

## Rules Sections

Official voice should be clear and table-facing.

Example:

> When you choose your Ensemble Role, you are not choosing a class. You are naming how the crew tends to use you when pressure rises.
>
> Roles do not need to be unique. Two Sparks mean the crew may act quickly and collide over who moves first. Two Anchors mean the crew may steady well and struggle to risk rupture.

Regent variant:

> Roles do not need to be unique. This is fortunate, because no known crew has ever distributed itself according to administrative preference.
>
> Two Sparks mean the crew may move quickly and argue over who created the opening. Two Anchors mean the crew may stabilize beautifully and then spend twenty minutes mistaking hesitation for care.

## House / School Sections

Official voice should be seductive but precise.

Example:

> Choose the Provisioners if your eyes go first to the empty hands, the locked cabinet, the missing translator, the exhausted organizer, the meal nobody planned, or the cost everyone keeps calling logistics.
>
> Provisioners know every crisis has a material body.

## Belief Sections

Official voice should be compassionate but unsentimental.

Example:

> Choose I'm Not Ready if pressure turns preparation into hiding.
>
> This belief may have protected you once. It may have kept you from making reckless promises, speaking over people, or mistaking adrenaline for wisdom.
>
> Under pressure, it asks for one more book, one more sign, one more plan, one more day.
>
> Sometimes the field cannot give you one more day.

> "Field" means the situation plus the people plus the pressure. If you only look at the problem, you are missing half the problem.

## Complicate

## Warn

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Do not pick Line because you want permission to be mean. Head Sura can smell that through walls.

## Humanize

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> The Body weather deck makes everyone cry eventually. People pretend this is not true because the benches are public.

## Foreshadow

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> If your Avoided School is the one under the sealed observatory, good luck. Also, do not bring metal buttons.

## Gossip

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Provisioners and Weavers have been arguing about the pantry inventory system since before I was born. Both are correct. This is the worst part.

## Confess

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> I crossed my Line on my second mission and called it strategy. The manual was less smug about this than I was.

## Cut Through Bullshit

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> "The mission field presents an opportunity for mutual learning" means the adults sent us somewhere politically inconvenient again.

## Council Note

> [MARGINALIA - J.]
> Council of Joannes position: if a teacher says "trust the process," ask who designed the process, who can stop it, and whether snacks are being withheld as pedagogy.

## Chapter 3 Voice Application

For `Chapter 3 Prose Draft - Create Your Student v0.1.md`, use this voice pattern:

1. Open with Regent-authored admission prose translating the Headmaster's grand vision into usable procedure.
2. Add one Council of Joannes marginal note that undercuts fear with lived honesty.
3. Use official voice for each creation step.
4. Add marginalia where a player is likely to misunderstand the emotional meaning of a choice.
5. Use "Write this on your Student Record" in clean procedural voice.
6. Use marginalia to warn against optimization, over-identification, or shame spirals.

Chapter 3 marginalia should especially comment on:

- thinking "not ready" means "not allowed"
- choosing House as personality test
- confusing Gift with destiny
- using Role to control spotlight
- treating Belief as a diagnosis
- writing a Line you do not intend to protect
- choosing a Bond that never enters play

## Do / Don't

## Do

- make the official text trustworthy
- let the official text be wry and operational
- make the marginalia intimate
- let the two voices disagree sometimes
- keep procedures usable
- make jokes reveal character or truth
- let the annotator be wrong occasionally
- let the institution be both wise and flawed

## Don't

- make the marginalia constant
- let snark deflate every sincere moment
- make the official text pompous
- make the Regent sound like a neutral narrator
- use marginalia to explain whole rules that should be in main text
- make the annotator omniscient
- use annotation as a dumping ground for lore
- turn the book into a parody of school fantasy

## Voice Acceptance Criteria

A drafted section is in voice if:

- the official text is clear enough to use at the table
- the official text feels like a hidden academy manual, not a training binder
- the marginalia adds lived texture or practical truth
- the marginalia has a clear job
- the two voices create warmth and depth without confusing rules
- the reader feels invited, not lectured
- the artifact feels like something worth owning and annotating further

## Working Summary

The proper tone for the book is:

**Regent-authored ceremonial field manual with Council of Joannes marginalia.**

The Regent teaches:

> This is how the Headmaster's impossible vision becomes a curriculum you can actually survive.

The Council annotator writes in the margin:

> Here is what that felt like when it happened to someone with a pulse, a committee assignment, and doubts about the official version.
