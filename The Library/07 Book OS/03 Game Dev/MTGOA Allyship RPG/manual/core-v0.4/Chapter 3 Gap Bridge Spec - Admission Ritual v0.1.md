# Chapter 3 Gap Bridge Spec - Admission Ritual v0.1

Status: implementation spec

Purpose: define the work required to bridge the gap identified in `Chapter 3 Source Comparison - Character Creation v0.1.md` and turn `Character Creation - v0.4.md` into a book-ready Chapter 3 prose draft.

Target output:

- `Chapter 3 Prose Draft - Create Your Student v0.1.md`

## Core Goal

Chapter 3 must stop feeling like a character creation worksheet and start feeling like an **admission ritual**.

The reader should finish the chapter feeling:

1. The ship noticed me.
2. I know what kind of help I bring.
3. I know what training is shaping me.
4. I know what pressure does to me.
5. I know what I cost, protect, and care about.
6. I know how I function in the crew.
7. I know what to write on my Student Record.
8. I am not ready.
9. I am here anyway.

## Source-Informed Design Requirements

## Kids on Bikes Requirement: Relational Warmth First

Chapter 3 must begin with a playable human before it becomes a mechanical build.

Implement:

- opening admission scene or letter
- arrival prompts before system categories
- rumor / first impression prompts
- relationship questions earlier in the process
- final relationship exchange before play begins

Must include prompts:

- What did the ship notice before you did?
- Who argued that you were ready enough?
- Who worried the school was making a mistake?
- What rumor followed you aboard?
- What did you bring that you have not unpacked?

Acceptance criteria:

- A reader can imagine their student as a person before choosing House, School, or stats.
- At least three prompts create relationships with other students, teachers, or the ship.
- Character creation produces table truth, not only private backstory.

## Dungeon World Requirement: Identity Heat

Each major choice must feel like an invitation, not a menu item.

Implement the following template for major choices:

1. **Identity invitation**
   - short, direct-address prose that makes the option feel desirable and dangerous
2. **Choose this if**
   - taste guidance for the player
3. **You gain**
   - mechanical effect
4. **Ask yourself**
   - private identity prompt
5. **Ask the table**
   - relationship, rumor, or world prompt
6. **Write on your Student Record**
   - artifact instruction

Apply this template to:

- Allyship Gift
- House
- Home School / Curiosity School / Avoided School
- Ensemble Role
- Self-Sabotaging Belief
- Offer, Cost, Line, Bond

Acceptance criteria:

- Each major identity choice has at least one sentence that could appear as inspirational book prose.
- Each choice has a mechanical consequence or clear play function.
- Each choice tells the player exactly what to record.

## Hearts Blazing Requirement: Ensemble Function

Ensemble Role must be selected as a crew-facing choice.

Use MTGOA-native Role names:

- Spark
- Anchor
- Builder
- Scout
- Witness

Keep Hearts Blazing-style labels only as a translation sidebar:

- Spark = Ace
- Anchor = Leader
- Builder = Engineer
- Scout = Rookie
- Witness = Veteran

Implement:

- group role-selection moment
- note that roles do not need to be unique
- overlap guidance: two Sparks mean the crew may move fast and compete for the first move; two Anchors mean the crew may stabilize well but hesitate to risk conflict
- role relationship questions
- role starting move box for each Role

Required crew questions:

- Who usually moves first?
- Who notices the cost?
- Who turns insight into a plan?
- Who says the thing everyone is avoiding?
- Who can make you stop performing?

Acceptance criteria:

- Ensemble Role clearly means "crew function," not class.
- Role choice creates at least one relationship or expectation at the table.
- Native role names are primary everywhere in new prose.

Every step must visibly fill in the Student Record.

Implement:

Acceptance criteria:

- A player can create a student by moving through the chapter in order.
- Each step updates the Student Record.

## Proposed Chapter Structure

## Page 1: Your Admission Record

Function:

- emotional doorway
- school admission frame
- direct address

Key claim:

> The ship did not accept the version of you that was already useful. It accepted the version that could still be trained.

Content:

- short admission prose
- "the ship noticed something before you did"
- first five admission prompts
- instruction to write answers on Student Record

## Page 2: What A Student Is

Function:

- explain identity stack without jargon

Content:

- Gift = what arrived before training
- House = what kind of work claims you
- School = how the ship trains your mastery
- Role = how you function in the crew
- Belief = the false story pressure activates
- Offer = help you reflexively bring
- Cost = what your help spends
- Line = what help must not become
- Bond = why the work has stakes

## Page 3: Quick Build Or Full Admission

Function:

- reduce one-shot friction
- preserve deeper campaign richness

Content:

- Quick Build: choose Gift, House, School, Role, Belief, assign stats, define one of Offer/Cost/Line/Bond, ask one relationship question
- Full Admission: answer all prompts
- note that campaign packets may provide quick-build student frames

## Page 4: Name, Vibe, And Arrival

Function:

- humanize before mechanics

Keep from current draft:

- name
- pronouns
- age band
- look
- personal object
- what people notice first

Add:

- arrival rumor
- who noticed you first
- what you have not unpacked
- what part of you relaxed when the doors closed

## Page 5: Allyship Gift

Function:

- distinguish natural gift from trained mastery

Current Gifts:

- Signal
- Spark
- Shelter
- Pattern
- Bridge
- Horizon

Spec note:

There is conceptual overlap with Schools. The prose must explicitly separate them:

- Gift = instinct, talent, survival adaptation, natural offer
- School = curriculum, discipline, training, mastery path

Each Gift needs:

- identity invitation
- starting edge
- risk / shadow
- prompt
- Student Record instruction

## Page 6: Choose A House

Function:

- make House feel like faction, lineage, and social home

Use current Houses:

- Provisioners
- Weavers
- Linekeepers
- Lanternbearers

Each House needs:

- direct-address prose adapted from Chapter 1
- choose this if
- you gain
- House shadow
- friend or rival prompt
- Trust / Heat prompt
- Student Record instruction

## Page 7: Choose Your School Triangle

Function:

- make Schools horizontal and personal

Selections:

- Home School
- Curiosity School
- Avoided School

Explain:

- Home School trains you
- Curiosity School calls you
- Avoided School challenges you

Required prompts:

- What did your Home School see in you?
- Why does your Curiosity School fascinate you?
- What do you think your Avoided School gets wrong?
- Which lieutenant from your Curiosity School has left their door open to you?

## Page 8: Assign Stats

Function:

- turn stats into trained approaches, not optimization knobs

Use current stat spread:

- +2 to one stat
- +1 to two stats
- 0 to two stats
- -1 to one stat

Stats:

- Sense
- Act
- Steady
- Shape
- Tend
- Speak

Add reflection prompts:

- What do you trust under pressure?
- What do you avoid under pressure?
- Which stat does your School train that you still resist?

Include examples:

- high Sense / low Speak
- high Act / low Tend
- high Shape / low Steady

## Page 9: Choose Ensemble Role

Function:

- establish crew ecology

Roles:

- Spark
- Anchor
- Builder
- Scout
- Witness

Each Role needs:

- identity invitation
- function
- team gift
- risk
- starting move
- role relationship prompt
- Student Record instruction

Add group procedure:

1. Everyone chooses a Role.
2. Read Roles aloud.
3. Notice overlaps and gaps.
4. Answer two crew questions.

## Page 10: Choose Your Belief

Function:

- establish emotional subclass / pressure engine

Beliefs:

- I'm not Good Enough
- I'm not Ready
- I Don't Belong
- I'm Insignificant
- I'm Not Worthy
- I'm Not Capable

Each Belief needs:

- what it sounds like in your head
- how it helped you survive
- how it distorts allyship
- trigger
- temptation
- wound move
- growth move
- relationship prompt
- Student Record instruction

Important:

Beliefs create tension, not betrayal. The prose must say this clearly.

## Page 11: Offer, Cost, Line, Bond

Function:

- create four actionable identity levers

Frame:

> These are the four sentences every student carries into the field.

Keep current moves:

- Offer Move
- Cost Move
- Line Move
- Bond Move

Add:

- examples
- prompt for each
- "what this looks like in play"
- Student Record instruction

## Page 12: Crew Relationships

Function:

- make the team playable before first mission

Procedure:

1. Ask one trust question.
2. Ask one concern question.
3. Ask one need question.

Questions:

- What did I do that made you trust me?
- What did I do that made you hesitate?
- What do you think I am better at than I believe?
- What do you think I avoid?
- What promise did we make together?
- What would you ask me not to do again?
- What do you need from me when pressure rises?
- What should I never assume about you?

## Page 13: Starting Resources And Final Check

Function:

- complete sheet
- verify play readiness

Starting values:

- Capacity 3
- Clarity 0
- Adversity 0
- Tension 0
- Growth 0

Final check:

- Does every student have a Gift?
- Does every student have a House?
- Does every student have School triangle?
- Are stats assigned?
- Does every student have Role and Belief?
- Did everyone define Offer, Cost, Line, Bond?
- Did each student answer at least one relationship question?

## Page 14: Introduce Yourself

Function:

- ceremonial close
- table onboarding

Use script:

> I am ___, a ___ of the ___ House.
> I usually help by ___.
> I am training with the School of ___.
> Under pressure I start believing ___.
> My Line is ___.
> My Bond is ___.
> I am not ready because ___.
> I am here anyway.

Each player reads this aloud.

After each introduction, one other player asks a relationship question.

## Page 15: Sample Completed Student

Function:

- reduce fear
- show all parts braided together

Required sample:

- complete Student Record
- short prose summary
- one example of their Gift in play
- one example of their Belief in play
- one relationship answer
- one stat choice explanation

Sample should avoid being too perfect.

The student should feel playable, not optimized.

## Design Risks

## Risk 1: Too Many Choices

Mitigation:

- add Quick Build route
- mark optional depth prompts clearly
- put "write this" instructions after each step

## Risk 2: Beliefs Feel Too Therapeutic

Mitigation:

- keep Beliefs tied to field consequences
- emphasize action, pressure, repair, and growth
- avoid diagnostic language
- make each Belief produce play, not analysis alone

## Risk 3: Allyship Becomes Abstract

Mitigation:

- every identity feature gets a move, prompt, or table-facing behavior
- examples should show concrete scenes
- Offer, Cost, Line, Bond remain actionable

## Risk 4: Role Names Become Too Poetic

Mitigation:

- include translation sidebar
- make each Role function extremely clear
- use function labels in headings or callouts if needed

Example:

> Spark: the one who catalyzes action.
