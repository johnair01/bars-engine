# Chapter 3 Source Comparison - Character Creation v0.1

Status: design analysis; role naming decision accepted

Purpose: compare the current MTGOA character creation draft against the RPG source texts and identify what Chapter 3 needs before becoming book prose.

Primary draft compared:

- `Character Creation - v0.4.md`

Primary source lenses:

- Kids on Bikes: collaborative character identity, relationships, ordinary people under pressure
- Dungeon World: class fantasy, direct address, fiction-first mechanics
- Hearts Blazing: ensemble archetypes, motives, table-facing components
- Foundations: prompt clarity, visible artifacts, minimal friction

## Core Diagnosis

The current Chapter 3 draft is mechanically promising.

It already contains the right major components:

- student identity
- Allyship Gift
- House
- Ensemble Role
- self-sabotaging belief
- Home, Curiosity, and Avoided Schools
- school-owned stats
- Offer, Cost, Line, and Bond
- starting resources
- introduction script
- relationship questions

The issue is not content.

The issue is reader experience.

Right now, Chapter 3 reads like a strong design worksheet. The source texts suggest it should become an **admission ritual**: a sequence that makes the player feel seen, claimed, equipped, and implicated before the first mission begins.

## Comparison Matrix

| Source | What It Does Well | Current Chapter 3 Match | Gap For MTGOA |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kids on Bikes | makes ordinary characters playable through trope, motivations, fears, flaws, relationships | strong relationship questions and grounded student details | needs more relational setup before mechanics become dense |
| Dungeon World | makes class choice feel like identity heat and player recruitment | Houses, Schools, Gifts, and Roles have strong fantasy potential | needs direct-address prose that seduces the reader into each choice |
| Hearts Blazing | gives ensemble roles and motives that shape drama | Ensemble Roles and Beliefs are a strong inheritance | current Roles may need MTGOA-specific names and clearer table functions |
| Foundations | makes procedures clean, prompt-driven, and artifact-producing | step-by-step format is clear | needs an admission form / student record artifact that visibly accumulates answers |

## Kids on Bikes Lens

Kids on Bikes character creation works because it begins from recognizable human shape before asking players to care about rules.

Its character procedure emphasizes:

- trope
- strengths and flaws
- fears
- motivations
- relationships
- ordinary personal texture
- how the character fits into a shared place

The current MTGOA draft matches this best in:

- Name and Vibe
- age bands
- personal object
- Offer, Cost, Line, Bond
- relationship questions

The strongest Kids on Bikes-aligned material is the idea that each student is not neutral. They arrive with a gift, a cost, a line, a bond, and a reason they are not ready.

That is excellent.

The gap is that the draft currently moves into formal system categories very quickly. It gives the player a list of components before creating the feeling of being a student aboard a living school.

### What To Borrow

Chapter 3 should include:

- a short admission scene before Step 1
- a "what people already know about you" prompt
- relationship questions earlier, not only at the end
- a "who saw you arrive?" question
- a "what rumor followed you aboard?" question
- a sample student that feels like a real person, not a stat block

### Suggested Addition

Add a page called **Your Admission Record**.

It asks:

- What did the ship notice before you did?
- Who argued that you were ready enough?
- Who worried the school was making a mistake?
- What did you bring aboard that you have not unpacked?
- What part of you relaxed when the doors closed?

## Dungeon World Lens

Dungeon World makes character creation exciting by making classes feel like invitations.

Its class prose does not merely say what the Fighter or Wizard can do. It tells the reader:

- you are necessary
- you are dangerous
- your companions need you
- the world misunderstands you
- your choices matter immediately

The current MTGOA draft has many identity containers that could carry this same heat:

- Houses
- Schools
- Allyship Gifts
- Ensemble Roles
- Beliefs

But most of them are currently phrased in reference mode.

Example:

> Your House is your practice faction.

This is clear, but not yet seductive.

A Dungeon World-inspired version would make each choice feel like a recruitment letter:

> The Provisioners claimed you because you noticed the empty hands before you noticed the speeches. You know every crisis has a body, and someone is always pretending the body can wait.

The current Chapter 1 already does this well for Houses and Schools. Chapter 3 should reuse that voice, but make it actionable.

### What To Borrow

Chapter 3 should include:

- direct address for each House
- direct address for each School
- punchier Role identity passages
- clear "you gain" boxes after each identity invitation
- a sample of how a fictional choice maps to a mechanical choice

### Suggested Addition

Each major choice should follow this format:

1. **Identity invitation:** who this choice says you are
2. **Choose this if:** player-facing taste guidance
3. **You gain:** mechanical feature
4. **Ask yourself:** one personal prompt
5. **Ask the table:** one relationship or world prompt

This lets Chapter 3 keep Dungeon World's identity heat while staying more emotionally careful than heroic fantasy.

## Hearts Blazing Lens

Hearts Blazing is most useful for Chapter 3 because it understands ensemble drama.

Its character creation does not only ask, "Who are you?"

It asks, "What function do you serve in this story machine?"

The current MTGOA draft borrows this through:

- Ensemble Roles
- self-sabotaging beliefs
- once-per-episode starting moves
- relationship questions

This is one of Chapter 3's strongest design lanes.

The earlier draft Roles were:

- Ace
- Leader
- Engineer
- Rookie
- Veteran

These are highly legible because they echo Hearts Blazing directly. The tradeoff is that they feel imported from another genre rather than grown from MTGOA's school-ship identity.

The accepted MTGOA set is:

- Spark
- Anchor
- Scout
- Builder
- Witness

Those names feel more native to allyship play and are now the character creation defaults.

### Role Comparison

| Current Role | Strength | Possible MTGOA Rename | Why It May Fit Better |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ace | bold competence | Spark | less individualistic, more catalytic |
| Leader | group orientation | Anchor | less command-coded, more stabilizing |
| Engineer | tools and systems | Builder | broader than technical competence |
| Rookie | obvious question | Scout | curiosity and first sight without incompetence framing |
| Veteran | past experience | Witness | memory, survival, and perspective without superiority |

Decision:

Use the MTGOA-native names in the character creation rules.

Keep the Hearts Blazing-style labels only as a translation sidebar for tables that find TV ensemble language helpful.

The deeper issue is that Ensemble Role should not feel like a class. It should feel like your function in the crew's emotional and tactical ecology.

### What To Borrow

Chapter 3 should include:

- ensemble role selection as a group step, not only an individual step
- table check: no role needs to be unique, but the crew should notice overlaps
- role questions that create relationships immediately
- private Belief as the drama engine instead of hidden motive
- a reminder that Beliefs create tension, not betrayal

### Suggested Addition

After everyone picks a Role, ask:

- Who usually moves first?
- Who notices the cost?
- Who turns insight into a plan?
- Who says the thing everyone is avoiding?
- Who can make you stop performing?

Each answer names another student.

## Foundations Lens

Foundations is the strongest source for procedural clarity.

Its lesson for Chapter 3 is simple:

Every step should visibly change an artifact.

Chapter 3 already has good steps, but it should explicitly tell the player where each answer goes on the student record.

If the player chooses a House, they should write it in a visible place.

If they choose a Belief, the sheet should gain a trigger, temptation, wound move, and growth move.

If they define an Offer, Cost, Line, and Bond, those should become usable buttons in play.

### What To Borrow

Chapter 3 should include:

- a student record artifact
- a quick-build sidebar
- "write this on your sheet" instructions
- optional depth prompts clearly marked
- a sample completed student
- a final introduction script

The player should feel progress accumulating.

### Suggested Addition

Every step should end with a line like:

> Write this on your Student Record.

or

> Circle this on your Student Record, then answer one prompt aloud.

This sounds mundane, but it matters. It turns character creation into a visible act.

## Current Chapter 3 Strengths

## 1. Strong Core Components

The system already has the right identity stack:

- Gift: what comes naturally
- House: what work you notice first
- School: how you train
- Role: how you function in the crew
- Belief: how pressure distorts you
- Offer: what help you bring
- Cost: what help spends
- Line: what help must not become
- Bond: why the work has stakes

This is robust and thematically aligned.

## 2. Good Mechanical Hooks

The moves attached to Offer, Cost, Line, and Bond are especially promising because they make character traits actionable.

They prevent the sheet from becoming purely descriptive.

## 3. The Beliefs Are Strong

The six self-sabotaging beliefs are clear, emotionally legible, and campaign-rich:

- I'm not Good Enough
- I'm not Ready
- I Don't Belong
- I'm Insignificant
- I'm Not Worthy
- I'm Not Capable

These can become subclass-style engines.

They are probably the most MTGOA-original part of character creation.

## 4. The School Triangle Is Useful

Home School, Curiosity School, and Avoided School create a good training map.

They give the Guide permission to pull the character toward growth without forcing a linear advancement path.

## Current Chapter 3 Risks

## 1. Too Many Categories Before Emotional Buy-In

The draft asks the player to choose:

- Gift
- House
- Role
- Belief
- three School relationships
- six stats
- Offer
- Cost
- Line
- Bond

This is strong, but heavy.

The prose needs to make the accumulation feel like enrollment, not paperwork.

## 2. Role Names Were At Risk Of Feeling Off-Genre

Ace, Leader, Engineer, Rookie, Veteran are legible, but they pull strongly toward Hearts Blazing.

Decision:

- Spark
- Anchor
- Builder
- Scout
- Witness

Use these as the default Role names. Keep the older TV ensemble labels only as a translation sidebar.

## 3. Gifts And Schools Overlap

Current Gifts:

- Signal
- Spark
- Shelter
- Pattern
- Bridge
- Horizon

Current Schools:

- Body / Sense
- Line / Act
- Oath / Steady
- Pattern / Shape
- Bridge / Tend
- Horizon / Speak

There is conceptual overlap between Gift and School. This can be good if intentional, but confusing if not explained.

Recommendation:

- Gift = what came naturally before training
- School = how the ship teaches you to discipline that gift

Add this distinction explicitly.

## 4. Belief Subclasses Need More Texture

The Belief list is strong, but currently each Belief has only:

- trigger
- temptation
- wound move
- growth move

For book-level robustness, each Belief should eventually have:

- what it sounds like in your head
- how it helps you survive
- how it distorts allyship
- what it asks from the crew
- a wound move
- a growth move
- an advancement unlock
- a relationship question

## 5. Stats Need A Softer On-Ramp

The stat table is clear, but Chapter 3 should not make stats feel like abstract optimization.

Stats should be framed as:

> The six ways the Schools teach students to meet pressure.

Players should assign stats by answering:

- What do you trust under pressure?
- What do you avoid under pressure?
- What does your School train that you still resist?

## Recommended Chapter 3 Shape

## Page 1: Your Admission Record

Open with a short letter or scene.

Purpose:

- emotional onboarding
- character creation as school admission
- direct address

Core line:

> The ship did not accept the version of you that was already useful. It accepted the version that could still be trained.

## Page 2: What A Student Is

Explain the identity stack in plain language:

- Gift
- House
- School
- Role
- Belief
- Offer, Cost, Line, Bond

Make it feel like parts of a person, not character sheet jargon.

## Page 3: Quick Build Or Full Admission

Offer two routes:

- **Quick Build:** choose from packages and play in 15 minutes
- **Full Admission:** answer every prompt and build relationships

This borrows Foundations' clarity and reduces one-shot friction.

## Page 4: Name, Vibe, And Arrival

Keep the current Name and Vibe step.

Add:

- arrival scene
- rumor
- who noticed you first
- what you carried aboard

## Page 5: Allyship Gift

Explain:

> Your Gift is what arrived before training.

For each Gift:

- invitation
- starting edge
- what it risks
- one prompt

## Page 6: Choose A House

Reuse the high-flavor House prose from Chapter 1, but make it selectable.

For each House:

- choose this if
- you gain
- shadow
- House prompt

## Page 7: Choose Your School Triangle

Home School, Curiosity School, Avoided School.

Explain:

- Home teaches you
- Curiosity calls you
- Avoided challenges you

## Page 8: Assign Stats

Frame stats as trained approaches.

Use the current stat table.

Add examples:

- high Sense / low Speak
- high Act / low Tend
- high Shape / low Steady

## Page 9: Choose Ensemble Role

Make this a group step.

- Spark / Anchor / Builder / Scout / Witness

These are the MTGOA-native Role names.

## Page 10: Choose Your Belief

Make this the emotional subclass.

For each Belief:

- invitation
- trigger
- temptation
- wound move
- growth move
- relationship prompt

## Page 11: Offer, Cost, Line, Bond

Keep all four.

They are excellent.

Make them feel like "the four sentences every ally needs before entering the field."

## Page 12: Crew Questions

Relationship prompts.

Borrow Kids on Bikes here.

Ask each player to answer one trust question, one concern question, and one "what I need from you" question.

## Page 13: Introduce Yourself

Use the current script, but make it ceremonial.

Each student steps into the circle and says:

> I am...
> I usually help by...
> I am training with...
> Under pressure I start believing...
> My Line is...
> My Bond is...
> I am not ready because...
> I am here anyway.

## Page 14: Sample Completed Student

Essential.

This is one of the biggest missing pieces.

The source texts all reduce fear through examples. MTGOA needs a completed student who demonstrates how the choices braid together.

## Key Design Decision

Accepted:

Use MTGOA-native role names in the book:

- Spark
- Anchor
- Builder
- Scout
- Witness

Then include a sidebar:

> If your table likes TV ensemble language, think of these as Ace, Leader, Engineer, Rookie, and Veteran.

This lets the system inherit Hearts Blazing's clarity without sounding borrowed.

## Bottom Line

Chapter 3 already has the right bones.

From the source texts, it needs:

- Kids on Bikes' relational warmth
- Dungeon World's identity heat
- Hearts Blazing's ensemble clarity
- Foundations' artifact procedure

The rewrite should not add many new mechanics.

It should reorganize and revoice the existing mechanics so the player feels:

1. I have been admitted.
2. I know what kind of help I bring.
3. I know what my help costs.
4. I know how I distort under pressure.
5. I know who I need.
6. I know what I write on the sheet.
7. I am not ready.
8. I am here anyway.
