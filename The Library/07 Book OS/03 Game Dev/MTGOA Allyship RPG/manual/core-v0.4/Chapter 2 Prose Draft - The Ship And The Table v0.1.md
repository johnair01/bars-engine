## Page 1: The Table Is The First Ship

Before your students enter the mission field, the players build the table that will hold them.

This is not a formality.

The school-ship can cross impossible weather, hide between worlds, and carry six Schools of mastery through the dark, but it cannot make your table safe by itself. The ship is a fiction. The table is real.

So the first ship is the circle of people playing.

Every campaign begins by asking what kind of vessel this group needs. How intense can the story become? What subjects are welcome? What should stay offscreen? What kind of repair does the table want when play gets sharp? How much mess, comedy, tenderness, argument, danger, and wonder belongs in this version of the game?

These questions are not outside the game.

They are the first lesson.

Allyship depends on containers. A good container does not eliminate risk. It lets people take meaningful risk without having to guess whether anyone will catch what spills.

The table is where the players practice consent before the students do. It is where the group decides how much pressure belongs in the story. It is where everyone learns how to pause, rewind, clarify, offer, refuse, repair, and return.

The Guide does not own this container alone.

The Guide protects it. The players help build it. The rules give it language. The ship gives it ritual.

When in doubt, treat table care as ship protocol.

The mission can wait.

The table comes first.

## Page 2: Ship Protocols

Before character creation, establish the protocols your table will use when play touches something tender.

Use whatever safety tools your group already trusts. This game assumes at least four protocols:

- **Line:** a subject, image, behavior, or kind of scene that will not appear in play.
- **Veil:** a subject that may exist in the world, but stays offscreen, softened, summarized, or handled without detail.
- **Pause:** a request to stop the scene for a moment, check in, breathe, clarify, or choose a different direction.
- **Rewind:** a request to roll back a line, image, choice, or scene beat and replay it differently.

You do not need to justify a Line.

You do not need to defend a Veil.

You do not need to prove that a Pause was necessary.

You do not need to apologize for a Rewind.

The table should decide how these protocols are signaled. Some groups use words. Some use cards. Some use a gesture, a message in chat, or a shared document. Pick something ordinary enough that people will actually use it.

The Guide should write the table's Lines and Veils where everyone can see them. If a Line or Veil is private, the Guide records it privately and protects it without announcing who named it.

Protocols can be added during play.

That is not a failure of preparation. Sometimes the table learns what it needs by meeting the story.

### Discomfort And Harm

This game often asks characters to feel discomfort.

They may receive feedback. They may be wrong in public. They may discover that their offer was not what the field needed. They may have to repair harm, disappoint a friend, refuse a mentor, or stay present when shame tries to pull them out of contact.

Discomfort can be part of training.

Harm is not.

The table never has to prove toughness by continuing a scene that is hurting a player. If someone calls Pause, the work is to listen. If someone calls Rewind, the work is to change course. If someone names a new Line, the work is to protect it.

The mission does not outrank consent.

## Page 3: Tone Calibration

Every campaign of **Mastering the Game of Allyship** has the same core function:

students aboard a hidden school-ship train to become useful under pressure.

The vibe can change.

One table may play luminous academy fantasy: candlelit corridors, impossible libraries, dramatic rivalries, field uniforms, house ceremonies, and teachers who speak in riddles because apparently nobody in power can resist.

Another table may play fugitive science fantasy: a temple-ship fleeing empire, encrypted lessons, orbital ruins, emergency drills, and students whose training might decide whether a community survives.

Another may play intimate social drama: a small ship, a wounded crew, a city in crisis, and missions where the hardest move is telling the truth without turning it into performance.

All of these are valid.

Before building the ship, choose your campaign's tone together.

### Choose Three Tone Anchors

As a group, choose three anchors that describe what this campaign should feel like.

Pick from the list or write your own:

- hidden academy
- rebel temple
- hopeful crisis
- messy teen drama
- intergenerational repair
- mission-of-the-week adventure
- political intrigue
- strange magic
- luminous science fantasy
- cozy ship life
- dangerous field work
- public consequences
- secret grief
- found family
- spiritual training
- tactical organizing
- social mystery
- ceremonial wonder
- uncomfortable comedy
- earnest melodrama

Write the three anchors on the ship sheet.

When the table is unsure how to frame a scene, return to the anchors.

### Choose Your Pressure Level

Choose the campaign's pressure level:

- **Gentle:** mistakes matter, but the story leaves room for softness, humor, and recovery.
- **Charged:** missions create real consequences, and students regularly face hard choices.
- **High Pressure:** the field is unstable, institutions push back, and delay has visible cost.

For a first session or one-shot, choose Gentle or Charged.

High Pressure works best after the table trusts the protocols.

### Choose Your Play Shape

Choose how this campaign will be played:

- **One-Shot:** build only the ship details needed for one mission.
- **Short Arc:** play three to six episodes with one major field crisis.
- **Campaign:** build a recurring ship, ongoing relationships, rank progress, and multiple mission fields.

The procedures in this chapter work for all three.

For a one-shot, answer fewer questions.

For a campaign, linger.

## Page 4: Build Your School-Ship

Every campaign recreates the ship.

The four Houses remain. The six Schools remain. The core promise remains.

Everything else may change.

Your ship might be wood, iron, crystal, bone, memory, light, code, song, weather, or a thing no world can name correctly. It may sail through clouds, fold between planets, dock in dreams, orbit disaster zones, hide beneath a city, or appear only when a community has run out of official options.

The ship is not just scenery.

It tells the table what kind of story this is.

Answer these questions together. The Guide writes the answers on the ship sheet.

### 1. What Form Does The Ship Take?

Choose one or invent your own:

- skyship
- fugitive temple
- orbital academy
- living monastery
- spelljammer
- impossible train
- floating city
- hidden campus
- lighthouse between worlds
- rebuilt Jedi-like temple in exile
- wandering university
- vessel made from rescued homes

Describe the first thing a new student notices when they come aboard.

### 2. What Is The Ship Called?

Choose a name that people aboard the ship actually say.

Examples:

- The Unfinished
- The Saint of Doors
- The Third Bell
- The Orchard in Exile
- The Long Window
- The Weather School
- The House That Left Orbit
- The Vessel of Better Questions

Then answer:

Who refuses to use that name?

What do they call it instead?

### 3. Why Is The Ship Hidden?

Choose one:

- It fled a purge.
- It broke an old treaty.
- It protects students with dangerous gifts.
- It carries forbidden histories.
- It is hunted by an institution it once served.
- It failed a world and has never stopped moving.
- It can only appear where help has already been requested.
- It is hiding from the consequences of its own first mission.

Then answer:

Who would benefit if the ship were exposed?

### 4. What Keeps The Ship Moving?

Choose one:

- an ancient engine
- collective vows
- borrowed magic
- political necessity
- a living heart
- hidden coordinates
- grief
- debt
- hope
- a dangerous promise

Then answer:

What happens if that power source is neglected?

### 5. What Room Does Everyone Whisper About?

Choose one:

- the forbidden deck
- the room of failed missions
- the sealed observatory
- the old Headmaster's quarters
- the apology archive
- the empty classroom
- the mirror infirmary
- the engine chapel
- the door that opens only after a 6-

Then answer:

Who has been inside and refuses to discuss it?

## Page 5: The Headmaster

The Headmaster is the recurring elder of the campaign.

They are not simply the person who gives missions. They are the living argument at the center of the school. They decide when students are ready enough to risk the field. They carry the ship's official philosophy, its private contradictions, and at least one grief they have mistaken for wisdom.

The Headmaster should be impressive.

They should also be incomplete.

Create them together, or let the Guide create them using the table's answers.

### Headmaster Prompts

Name:

Pronouns:

Vibe:

Choose one visible gift:

- impossible calm
- dangerous honesty
- warmth that makes people confess
- tactical brilliance
- ceremonial authority
- deep listening
- inconvenient humor
- terrifying patience
- the ability to remember every student

Choose one philosophy:

- Help must follow consent.
- No one is ready. Training begins anyway.
- The cost of action must be named before action becomes noble.
- Repair matters more than reputation.
- Power must move toward the burden, not the spotlight.
- The truth needs timing, craft, and a witness.
- A promise is not sacred if it protects harm.

Choose one secret grief:

- a mission that saved the wrong thing
- a student they sent too early
- a world that refused help until it was too late
- a friendship broken by power
- a former mentor who became an enemy
- an apology they never delivered

Choose one blind spot:

- they mistake composure for readiness
- they overvalue sacrifice
- they distrust comfort
- they protect students by withholding too much
- they send Linekeepers where Bridge work is needed
- they see the system before they see the person
- they believe their grief makes them objective

Choose one political enemy:

- a council that wants the ship licensed
- a former student leading a rival academy
- an empire that calls the ship subversive
- a foundation that wants to fund and control it
- a movement that believes the ship arrives too late
- a School elder who thinks the Headmaster has lost the mission

The Headmaster should be able to say something true that makes the students braver.

They should also be able to be wrong in a way that matters.

## Page 6: Table Artifacts

This game creates artifacts.

Not as homework.

As proof that the story is changing.

During setup, prepare the following records. They may be printed sheets, index cards, a shared document, a whiteboard, a notebook, or a digital board.

### Student Records

Each player keeps a student record.

It tracks House, Home School, stats, Ensemble Role, Offer, Cost, Line, Bond, self-sabotaging belief, Growth, Adversity, Tension, and rank progress.

The student record asks:

Who are you becoming, and what keeps trying to stop you?

### Crew Sheet

The crew sheet belongs to the whole table.

It tracks team name, mission role, shared stake, bonds between students, current Tension, shared Clarity, and unresolved repairs.

The crew sheet asks:

What can this group do together that no one student can do alone?

### Ship Sheet

The ship sheet records the campaign's version of the academy.

It tracks ship name, form, tone anchors, pressure level, Headmaster, Houses, Schools, rumors, forbidden rooms, and shipwide clocks.

The ship sheet asks:

What kind of container is training these students?

### Mission Field Map

The mission field map records the world the ship has entered.

It tracks factions, resources, harms, hopes, institutions, missing voices, pressure clocks, and people who will remember what the students do.

The mission field map asks:

What was already happening before the ship arrived?

### Chronicle

The chronicle records what changed.

At the end of each session, write:

- what became visible
- who received support
- who paid a cost
- what was repaired
- what remains unresolved
- what each student learned
- what the mission field now believes about the ship

The chronicle asks:

What did our help actually do?

### Token Pool

Place tokens where everyone can reach them.

Use tokens, marks, cards, or digital counters for:

- **Adversity:** earned when a miss reveals the edge of your current capacity.
- **Growth:** marked when you name impact, resist a self-sabotaging belief, or complete a meaningful lesson.
- **Tension:** added when pressure, unresolved harm, or overextension builds.
- **Clarity:** gained when the table sees the field more honestly.

Tokens should be visible.

The table should feel the weather changing.

## Page 7: Shared Authority

The Guide does not invent the entire world alone.

This game works best when authority is shared clearly.

Shared authority does not mean every player can declare anything at any time. It means the table knows who can add what, when, and how the Guide turns those answers into pressure.

### Players Can Define

Players may define:

- details about their student
- details about their House relationships
- a rumor they have heard about the ship
- a room, teacher, rival, tradition, or daily ritual aboard the ship
- what their student notices in a scene
- what their Offer looks like
- what their Cost feels like
- what their Line protects
- why their Bond matters

When a player adds something, the Guide may ask:

"Who taught you that?"

"Who disagrees?"

"What does this cost?"

"Who else knows?"

"What would make that complicated?"

### The Guide Protects

The Guide protects:

- table protocols
- mission pressure
- consequences
- NPC integrity
- faction agendas
- the truth of the field
- the difference between intention and impact
- the question, "What does help require here?"

The Guide is allowed to be surprised.

The Guide is not allowed to make the players guess what kind of game they are playing.

If a player offers a detail that would break a Line, erase a meaningful consequence, flatten an NPC, or make the mission too easy, the Guide should redirect it.

Say:

"Yes, but here is the cost."

"That can be true if we also answer who is left out."

"That crosses a protocol, so let's find another version."

"I like the shape. Let us place it where it creates pressure instead of solving everything."

### Ask And Use Answers

The strongest questions create playable material.

Weak question:

"What is the city like?"

Stronger question:

"What does the city proudly show visitors, and what does it hide from them?"

Weak question:

"Who is your teacher?"

Stronger question:

"Which teacher believes in you for the wrong reason?"

Weak question:

"What is wrong here?"

Stronger question:

"Who has already named the problem and been ignored?"

Ask questions that create people, pressure, contradiction, and choice.

Then use the answers.

That is the pact.

## Page 8: Build The First Mission Field

If your table is starting from a published campaign packet, use the packet's field map and answer only the open prompts.

If your table is creating a mission from scratch, answer the following together.

Do not build a whole planet.

Build a field of pressure.

### Mission Field Prompts

1. What visible event greets the ship?
   - ceremony
   - protest
   - trial
   - shortage
   - celebration
   - evacuation
   - election
   - funeral
   - festival
   - disaster drill

2. What harm is being managed instead of repaired?

3. Who has the official story?

4. Who has been living with the real cost?

5. What resource is missing, locked, redirected, or exhausted?

6. Which institution wants the ship to see an orderly version of the crisis?

7. Which local person or group does not trust the ship?

8. What would bad help make worse?

9. What would real help require before it looks impressive?

10. What clock starts ticking when the students arrive?

Write brief answers.

Leave room to discover more during play.

The mission field should not be a puzzle with one correct solution.

It should be a living situation where different moves create different consequences.

## Page 9: First Session Setup Checklist

Use this checklist before the first scene.

### Table

- Choose safety protocols.
- Name Lines and Veils.
- Decide how to Pause and Rewind.
- Choose tone anchors.
- Choose pressure level.
- Choose play shape.

### Ship

- Choose ship form.
- Name the ship.
- Decide why it is hidden.
- Decide what keeps it moving.
- Name one whispered-about room.
- Create or select the Headmaster.

### Artifacts

- Prepare student records.
- Prepare crew sheet.
- Prepare ship sheet.
- Prepare mission field map.
- Prepare chronicle.
- Prepare visible token pool.

### Mission

- Choose published packet or build a field.
- Name the visible event.
- Name the official story.
- Name the hidden cost.
- Name the first pressure clock.
- Decide where the students arrive.

### Opening Question

When everyone is ready, the Guide asks:

**What does your student notice before anyone explains the mission?**

Each player answers once.

The Guide writes down the answers.

Then the bell sounds.

The doors open.

Play begins.

## Page 10: The Promise Of Setup

Setup is not the part before the story.

Setup is where the story learns what it is allowed to become.

By the time this chapter is complete, your table should have:

- a shared sense of safety
- a campaign tone
- a school-ship that belongs to this group
- a Headmaster with power and contradiction
- visible artifacts for play
- a clear split of authority between players and Guide
- a first mission field with pressure already moving

You do not need everything solved.

You need enough to begin honestly.

The students are not ready.

The table is ready enough.

That is how the ship works.
