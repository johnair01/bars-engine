# Game Masters Council - Open Question Rulings v0.1

Status: design rulings

Purpose: answer the roadmap open questions from the perspective of the Game Masters, specifically for the ideal reader profile.

Ideal reader:

**Integral Green / Diplomat / Enneagram 2 / burnout femme / Dimension 20 RPG player.**

This reader is emotionally literate, justice-oriented, allergic to cold mechanics, likely over-responsible in groups, and drawn to games where vulnerability becomes cinematic. They want magic, friendship, competence, style, and the feeling that the book understands why care can become exhausting.

They need:

- emotional permission without therapy cosplay
- mechanics that make care concrete
- vivid school fantasy
- enough structure to reduce social labor
- language that protects them from becoming the table's unpaid facilitator
- a route from overfunctioning into powerful, bounded action
- beauty, humor, and a little theatrical ache

The Council's standard:

## Chapter 6: Beliefs, Shadows, And Superpowers

## Question 1: Does every belief get one wound move and one growth move, or a small move tree?

Ruling:

Each Belief gets a small three-part move structure:

1. **Wound Move**: the easy move. It gives an immediate benefit when the Belief drives action under pressure, but creates long-term consequences.
2. **Growth Move**: the costly move. It asks for a real-time cost, delay, vulnerability, resource spend, or lost opportunity, but creates a sustainable outcome.
3. **Power Move**: the earned move. It is unlocked by spending Adversity and shows how the student's superpower becomes clean, bounded, and useful.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

One wound move and one growth move may feel too thin for a reader who wants character drama with Dimension 20-level emotional payoff. A full move tree may feel like homework. Three moves is enough to create an arc without turning the Belief into a character tax.

Design note:

The Wound Move should be tempting because it works now. The Growth Move should hurt a little because it interrupts the old survival pattern. The Power Move should feel cool because the player earned it through adversity instead of being granted it as a moral prize.

Mechanical pattern:

- **Wound Move**: take the easy benefit now; mark a future consequence.
- **Growth Move**: pay the real-time cost now; create a more stable outcome.
- **Power Move**: spend Adversity to unlock a clean superpower use.

Example:

Belief: **I'm Not Worthy**

Wound Move: **Overpay For Trust**

When someone trusts, praises, protects, or supports you and your Belief says you must earn it immediately, you may take +1 forward or gain 1 Clarity by overfunctioning right now.

If you do, choose one long-term consequence:

- mark 1 Tension
- give the Guide 1 Heat with a House, faction, or relationship affected by your overreach
- advance your Belief clock by 1
- create an obligation you must answer during downtime

Growth Move: **Let The Ledger Stay Open**

When you receive care and do not immediately repay it, say what it costs you in the moment:

- you lose time
- you let someone else lead
- you admit a need
- you leave a task unfinished
- you ask the table for support
- you accept that someone may be disappointed

Then choose one sustainable outcome:

- clear 1 Tension
- mark Growth
- restore 1 Trust with someone who needed you to stop overfunctioning
- prevent your Belief clock from advancing in this scene
- create a cleaner Bond question for downtime

Power Move: **Abundance Without Erasure**

After you have marked Growth from this Belief at least once, you may spend 3 Adversity to unlock this move for the episode.

When you resource someone from enoughness instead of self-erasure, name what you can offer and what you will not sacrifice. Then choose two:

- the support reaches the real need
- you do not take on the whole burden
- someone else gains room to contribute
- you clear 1 Tension or reduce 1 Heat caused by overfunctioning
- you create a durable resource the crew can use later

If you use this move in a way that violates the boundary you named, the Guide may advance your Belief clock or convert the support into an obligation.

## Question 2: Should belief advancement unlock mechanical bonuses, narrative permissions, or both?

Ruling:

Both, but narrative permission leads.

Belief advancement should unlock:

- a new way to use the student's superpower
- one mechanical option that reduces overfunctioning
- one narrative permission that changes how the student can be seen
- access to an earned Power Move purchased with Adversity

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader does not want healing reduced to a +1. They also does not want growth to be purely interpretive. The sweet spot is a small mechanical permission attached to a larger identity shift.

Good unlock:

> When you receive support without deflecting, you may clear 1 Tension or grant an ally +1 forward, your choice.

Bad unlock:

> Gain +1 to all empathy rolls.

The first teaches a pattern. The second rewards emotional usefulness as a stat.

## Question 3: How does the Guide keep belief scenes safe without flattening them?

Ruling:

Belief scenes should not each carry their own bespoke safety move.

Safety belongs at the Guide / table procedure level so it protects the whole game instead of becoming another move players must remember under emotional pressure.

Guides use three global safety rules:

1. **Consent Before Depth**: the player can always say whether the Belief is in play.
2. **Fiction Before Analysis**: the Belief must change a concrete situation, not become free-floating self-analysis.
3. **Exit Before Intensity**: every Belief scene needs a playable next action, pause, repair, or boundary.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader may be very willing to go emotionally deep, and may also over-consent to intensity for the sake of the group's experience. The game must protect them from making their pain the table's content engine.

Guide language:

> "Do you want your Belief in this scene, or should we keep this at the mission level?"

> "What does the Belief make harder right now?"

> "What action, request, repair, or boundary could move this forward?"

Design note:

Do not write "safety moves" inside individual Belief moves. Put safety procedures in Chapter 9 and reference them from Chapter 6.

## Question 4: How many superpower archetypes are needed for the core book?

Ruling:

Six core superpower archetypes.

- **Connector**: creates relationships, circuits, translation, belonging, and mutual recognition
- **Strategist**: sees the board, models consequences, sequences action, and finds leverage
- **Disruptor**: interrupts patterns, breaks false inevitability, and makes stuck systems move
- **Escape Artist**: finds exits, slips constraints, survives capture, and opens impossible routes
- **Alchemist**: metabolizes waste, grief, conflict, and failure into usable capacity
- **Storyteller**: shapes meaning, memory, morale, narrative, and public imagination

Why this serves the ideal reader:

Six is enough to feel mythic without becoming an encyclopedia. These are not team roles. Team Roles describe how a student functions in the crew. Superpowers describe the extraordinary capacity the student brings into the fiction.

Design note:

Superpowers should not be literal combat powers only. They are dramatic capacities that can be expressed socially, magically, strategically, emotionally, or physically. A Connector might create a psychic bridge or introduce the right two people. An Alchemist might transmute toxic runoff or turn a failed apology into a new ritual of repair.

## Question 5: Are superpowers selected separately, derived from Belief, or chosen through a prompt matrix?

Ruling:

Use a prompt matrix.

Players choose:

1. Belief
2. Superpower archetype
3. Power expression
4. Wound distortion
5. Clean use

Why this serves the ideal reader:

Deriving power directly from Belief risks making the wound feel deterministic. Choosing separately risks making the power feel disconnected. A matrix lets the player say, "This is what I carry, and this is what I can do with it."

Example:

Belief: **I Don't Belong**

Superpower Archetype: **Escape Artist**

Power expression: sense thresholds, hidden paths, locked exits, and rooms that reject people before anyone says no

Wound distortion: disappear before being excluded

Clean use: find the door others were taught not to look for

Team Role distinction:

The same student might be a **Builder** on the team and an **Escape Artist** by superpower. Their crew relies on them to build containers, but their mythic gift is finding exits from systems that were designed to hold people in place.

## Chapter 7: The Six Schools

## Question 1: How distinct should each School head's voice be from the Deputy / Regent voice?

Ruling:

Very distinct.

The Deputy / Regent introduces the chapter, explains the institution, and keeps the book usable. Then each School Head speaks in their own voice.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader wants to fall in love with mentors. School heads should feel like powerful NPCs with different philosophies, flaws, cadences, and invitations. A single neutral voice would flatten the school fantasy.

Required structure:

- Deputy / Regent frame: "Here is how Schools work."
- School Head address: "Here is what this path asks of you."
- Lieutenant teacher notes: "Here are the subskills."
- Council of Joannes marginalia: "Here is what students whisper after the speech."

## Question 2: How many School moves are needed in the core book before the system feels complete?

Ruling:

Each School needs:

- one starting discipline move
- one rank move for each of the six altitude ranks
- one shadow exam move

That is eight moves per School in the full design, but the core book can present:

- the starting discipline move
- two sample rank moves
- the shadow exam move
- guidance for the remaining rank moves

Why this serves the ideal reader:

The reader needs to feel that Schools are real paths, not decorative lore. But too many moves at once can become a wall. Give enough to prove the ladder, then let advancement chapters and digital tools carry the rest.

## Question 3: How directly do Schools interact with superpowers?

Ruling:

Schools train how superpowers are used under pressure.

Houses give the social practice. Team Roles describe crew function. Beliefs reveal the wound. Superpowers express the gift. Schools teach mastery.

Examples:

- Body trains a Disruptor to feel the charge before breaking the room open.
- Line trains a Connector to make contact without dissolving boundaries.
- Oath trains a Strategist to make plans that do not become cages.
- Pattern trains a Storyteller to shape meaning without turning people into symbols.
- Bridge trains an Escape Artist to cross difference without abandoning themselves.
- Horizon trains an Alchemist to transform failure without romanticizing harm.
- Horizon trains any archetype to act from long-view responsibility.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This lets the reader keep their power fantasy while learning boundaries. It says, "Your gift is real. Now let us help it stop eating you."

## Chapter 8: Episodes, Missions, And Downtime

## Question 1: Is ship exposure a global clock, a campaign clock, or both?

Ruling:

Both.

Use:

- **Ship Exposure** as a global campaign clock
- **Local Exposure** as a mission-specific clock

Why this serves the ideal reader:

The reader enjoys consequential worlds. Global exposure makes the ship feel alive and endangered. Local exposure keeps each mission grounded. The two-clock model creates drama without making every mistake catastrophic.

## Question 2: How many downtime actions should each student get between episodes?

Ruling:

Two downtime actions per student, plus one free relational check-in if the table wants it.

Downtime actions:

- recover
- train
- repair
- deepen a Bond
- pursue House scene
- pursue School scene
- tend Belief / superpower
- gather a resource
- update Chronicle

Why this serves the ideal reader:

Two choices create meaningful prioritization without turning downtime into admin. The free check-in protects the emotional ensemble feel without forcing players to spend scarce actions on basic care.

## Question 3: Should downtime be freeform scenes or structured move choices?

Ruling:

Structured choice, freeform scene.

Players choose a downtime action from a visible list. Then the table plays or summarizes the scene freely.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader likely loves character scenes but can overextend into endless processing. The menu gives containment; the scene gives heart.

## Chapter 9: Guide Rules

## Question 1: Should the Guide voice be the Deputy Headmistress, a Guide-facing Regent office, or a neutral rules voice?

Ruling:

Use the Deputy / Regent for framing, then a practical Guide Office voice for procedure.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

They need warmth and world texture, but Guide procedure must be crisp. The book should not make facilitation feel mystical. It should make it feel learnable.

Voice split:

- Deputy / Regent: philosophy, warnings, permission
- Guide Office: agenda, moves, clocks, scene procedure
- Marginalia: common table failure modes

## Question 2: How explicit should real-world facilitation advice be in a commercial RPG manual?

Ruling:

Explicit, but always tied to play.

Do not write essays about real-world allyship during Guide procedure. Give usable prompts, consent checks, and "when this happens, do this" tools.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader already cares. They do not need to be convinced to care. They need help not becoming a martyr, lecturer, or emotional custodian at the table.

Good:

> If a player starts explaining harm abstractly, ask who is affected in the scene and what they need before the next move.

Bad:

> Allyship requires listening to marginalized voices.

True, but too broad for Guide procedure.

## Question 3: Should Guide moves be cards?

Ruling:

Yes.

Guide moves should exist as:

- full chapter explanations
- quick reference spread
- printable / app cards

Why this serves the ideal reader:

Cards reduce cognitive load. They also let a Guide run the game without holding the whole moral architecture in their head.

## Chapter 10: Campaign Fields And Packet Design

## Question 1: Should campaign packet design be player-facing, Guide-facing, or publisher-facing?

Ruling:

Guide-facing in the core book, with player-facing handouts and publisher-facing notes in a later creator kit.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

The core reader wants to run or play soon. They do not need a full publishing apparatus yet. They need to understand what a packet does and how to use one.

## Question 2: How much of this belongs in the core manual versus a separate creator kit?

Ruling:

Core manual includes:

- what a campaign packet is
- how to read one
- how to make a simple mission field
- one complete starter packet
- three shorter mission seeds

Creator kit later includes:

- full packet design rules
- layout templates
- balancing guidance
- art direction
- publishing checklist

Why this serves the ideal reader:

Do not make the core book feel like homework for future designers. Make it feel like a door into play.

## Question 3: Should each packet feature one House and pressure one House by default?

Ruling:

Yes.

Every packet should define:

- featured House
- pressure House
- School spotlight
- Belief pressure
- superpower invitation

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This creates a clean emotional and mechanical shape. The featured House gives fantasy. The pressure House complicates the fantasy. The School gives growth. The Belief pressure creates personal resonance. The superpower invitation makes the whole thing feel like an RPG, not a seminar.

## Chapter 11: Advancement And Legacy

## Question 1: What is the exact advancement economy?

Ruling:

Use five advancement currencies / tracks, each with a different job:

- **Adversity**: short-term leverage from misses
- **Growth**: character advancement from metabolized impact
- **Trust**: relationship state with Houses, NPCs, factions, and crew
- **Heat**: consequence attention from Houses, factions, and mission fields
- **Legacy**: durable change recorded in the Chronicle

Advancement costs:

- 3 Growth: improve a stat conditionally or unlock a minor move option
- 5 Growth: unlock a House, Role, Belief, Superpower, or School move
- 7 Growth: advance School rank or complete major Belief rewrite
- Legacy milestones: unlock graduation, teaching, return appearances, and campaign-level changes

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader needs growth to feel earned and emotionally meaningful, not grindy. The economy should reward repair, boundaries, receiving care, and changed behavior as much as heroic action.

## Question 2: Does graduation end a character, transform them, or unlock a new mode of play?

Ruling:

Graduation transforms the character and unlocks Journeyman mode.

Graduated characters can:

- leave the ship to run their own allyship campaigns
- become recurring NPCs
- mentor new students
- return during major arcs
- anchor future campaign packets

Why this serves the ideal reader:

Ending a beloved character can feel punishing. Keeping them unchanged cheapens growth. Journeyman mode lets graduation feel like a beautiful threshold.

## Question 3: How does Legacy affect later campaigns?

Ruling:

Legacy becomes campaign memory.

Legacy can create:

- changed maps
- new contacts
- House traditions
- student rumors
- available resources
- warnings in future missions
- altered faction starting Trust / Heat
- named techniques taught to later students

Why this serves the ideal reader:

This reader wants their care to matter. Legacy proves that repair and courage leave marks beyond the scene.

## Chapter 12: Starter Missions

## Question 1: Should the core book include one full starter mission or four full starter missions?

Ruling:

One full starter mission, three rich mission seeds.

Recommended:

- full packet: **The Missing Water**
- rich seeds: **The Broken Concord**, **The Quiet Gate**, **The Borrowed Story**

Why this serves the ideal reader:

One full packet lets them run tonight. Three rich seeds show breadth without bloating the core book. Expanded versions can become paid campaign content.

## Question 2: Which starter mission should be the full worked example?

Ruling:

**The Missing Water.**

Why:

- resource scarcity is concrete
- Provisioners make care visible without abstraction
- the pressure House, Lanternbearers, introduces consent and visibility
- School of the Body grounds the game in felt need
- it teaches allyship through action, not discourse

## Question 3: How much real-world resonance should starter missions have?

Ruling:

High resonance, fictionalized structure.

The mission should feel recognizable without mapping one-to-one onto a specific real-world crisis.

Why this serves the ideal reader:

They want meaning. They also need enough imaginative distance to play safely and creatively.

## Ruling 2: The Reader's Emotional Contract

The book should repeatedly communicate:

> You are allowed to care without disappearing.

This should show up mechanically through:

- Open Up
- Line
- Belief Growth Moves
- bounded superpowers
- downtime recovery
- Guide procedures that distribute emotional labor
- Legacy that rewards durable change, not self-sacrifice

## Ruling 3: What Makes This Worth Buying

The ideal reader should feel they bought:

- a beautiful school-ship setting
- a safe but emotionally rich RPG engine
- a way to play allyship without being lectured
- characters with wounds, powers, Houses, and mastery paths
- campaign content that can keep expanding

The manual must feel like a magic school handbook that secretly knows why the nicest person at the table is tired.
