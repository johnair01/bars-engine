# Hard Fork - Engine and Campaign Content Model v0.3

Status: design correction after v0.2

Core decision:

**The four allyship domains are not districts. They are factional forces / practice agendas inside the engine.**

The actual world is campaign content.

That means the engine can power many campaign settings, and campaign content becomes a sellable layer in the value stack.

## Why the v0.2 District Model Was Wrong

The Wells, Loom, Thresholds, and Lanterns were useful as teaching placeholders, but they overbuilt the default world.

They made the engine pretend it already had a setting.

That creates three problems:

1. The world feels pre-decided before the fiction exists.
2. The four domains become geography instead of living pressures.
3. Paid campaign content has less room to be valuable because the core book already consumed the setting slot.

The fix:

- Core manual = engine, factions, procedures, safety, moves, campaign construction.
- Campaign modules = worlds, maps, factions, fronts, episodes, NPCs, crisis clocks, and vibe.

## New Product Architecture

### Core Artifact

**Mastering the Game of Allyship: Core Manual**

Contains:

- player rules
- guide rules
- safety tools
- role/playbook system
- allyship moves
- faction engine
- episode engine
- campaign creation procedure
- one fast-start scenario
- advice for writing campaign modules

Does not contain:

- a fully canonical default city
- four fixed districts
- a campaign-length setting that competes with future modules

### Campaign Content

Campaign modules are paid setting packets.

Each module contains:

- campaign premise
- world map or relationship map
- playable locations
- factions
- NPCs
- clocks
- episode deck
- fronts/threats
- starter characters or roles
- custom moves
- real-world practice bridges

This is the Scum-and-Villainy / Forged-in-the-Dark-style move:

The core engine teaches how to play.

The campaign packet gives you a sector to play in.

## New Core Worldbuilding Model

Replace **The Living City as fixed setting** with:

**The Table creates or selects a Field.**

A Field is the social world where allyship pressure happens.

Examples:

- a city recovering from disaster
- a sci-fi crew on the edge of empire
- a magical school with a hidden caste system
- a mutual-aid network under surveillance
- a fantasy kingdom after a revolution
- a summer camp with a secret
- a workplace facing a public crisis
- a temple, ship, neighborhood, caravan, guild, fandom, union, or family system

The Field has:

- a map
- a cast
- factions
- resources
- rumors
- lines of power
- a central contradiction
- a pressure clock

The Field is authored by:

- the campaign module, if you bought one
- the table, if playing homebrew
- the Guide, if running a prepared game

## The Four Practice Factions

The four domains become **Practice Factions**.

They are not always literal organizations. In some settings they may be guilds, crews, schools, movements, departments, spirits, gods, traditions, political blocs, or patterns of action.

Each Practice Faction wants a different kind of allyship response.

| Practice Faction | Domain | Wants | Shadow |
|---|---|---|---|
| The Provisioners | Gather Resources | Move capacity where depletion is real. | Charity theater, hoarding, savior logistics. |
| The Weavers | Skillful Organizing | Build roles, agreements, handoffs, and durable containers. | Process worship, control, bureaucracy. |
| The Linekeepers | Direct Action | Interrupt harm and draw necessary boundaries. | Escalation addiction, purity tests, reckless heroics. |
| The Lanternbearers | Raise Awareness | Make hidden patterns visible with consent and precision. | Optics, exposure, spectacle, discourse loops. |

Campaign modules may rename these.

In a sci-fi module:

- Provisioners become Quartermasters.
- Weavers become Coordinators.
- Linekeepers become Boarding Crew.
- Lanternbearers become Signal Officers.

In a magical school module:

- Provisioners become the Hearth Circle.
- Weavers become the Rulebinders.
- Linekeepers become the Wardens.
- Lanternbearers become the Mirror Choir.

In a neighborhood module:

- Provisioners become the Mutual Aid Table.
- Weavers become the Block Council.
- Linekeepers become the Street Team.
- Lanternbearers become the Story Keepers.

Rule:

**The Practice Factions are engine slots, not lore.**

Campaign content fills those slots with setting-native names, people, locations, and tensions.

## Faction Sheets

Each faction has:

- Name
- Practice Domain
- Public Face
- Real Need
- Current Project
- Resource
- Boundary
- Shadow Move
- Relationship to each other faction
- Clock

Example:

Name: The Mutual Aid Table

Practice Domain: Gather Resources

Public Face: "We get people what they need."

Real Need: dependable volunteers who do not burn out.

Current Project: food and medicine delivery during a heatwave.

Resource: local trust.

Boundary: no public stories about recipients without consent.

Shadow Move: praise resilience instead of reducing burden.

Clock: Volunteer Collapse, 6 segments.

## Faction Status

Track relationship between the player group and each faction.

Status ranges from -2 to +2.

- **+2 Allied:** they trust you with sensitive needs.
- **+1 Warm:** they will work with you if the ask is clear.
- **0 Unknown:** no meaningful relationship yet.
- **-1 Strained:** they have reason to doubt your impact.
- **-2 Hostile:** they believe your help will cost them.

Faction Status changes through impact, not intention.

## Campaign Module Template

A paid campaign module should include:

1. **Title and Vibe**
   - What kind of story is this?
   - What media shelf does it live near?
   - What emotional weather should the table expect?

2. **Premise**
   - One paragraph.
   - Who are the players?
   - Why are they together?
   - What pressure starts the campaign?

3. **The Field**
   - Map or relationship diagram.
   - 6 to 10 named locations.
   - 3 visible institutions.
   - 3 hidden pressures.

4. **Practice Factions**
   - Four setting-native versions of Provisioners, Weavers, Linekeepers, Lanternbearers.
   - Each with clocks, needs, boundaries, and shadow moves.

5. **Local Factions**
   - 3 to 6 additional factions that belong to this world.
   - These create the campaign's actual politics.

6. **Fronts / Threats**
   - 3 campaign fronts.
   - 2 session fronts.
   - Each with moves and clocks.

7. **Episode Deck**
   - 1 Pilot Episode.
   - 8 to 12 Midseason Episodes.
   - 2 Finale Episodes.
   - 4 Static cards.

8. **NPC Roster**
   - 12 named NPCs.
   - Each with desire, fear, leverage, and boundary.

9. **Custom Moves**
   - 3 to 6 setting-specific moves.

10. **Practice Bridges**
   - Session debrief questions.
   - Real-world reps.
   - BAR prompts.

11. **One-Shot Path**
   - A compressed route that can be played in 2.5 to 3 hours.

12. **Campaign Path**
   - A 4-session route.
   - An 8 to 12-session route.

## One-Shot Setup Fix

Hearts Blazing has a great vibe and strong ensemble logic, but its setup can become too wide and tedious for one-shot play.

MTGOA needs two setup modes.

### Fast Start

Use for one-shots.

Time: 20 minutes.

Procedure:

1. Choose a campaign packet.
2. Read the premise aloud.
3. Each player chooses a Role and Face.
4. Each player answers one Bond question.
5. The table chooses one Line and one Veil.
6. The Guide reveals the Pilot Episode.
7. Start in motion.

No full worldbuilding.

No open-ended setting committee.

No long archetype negotiation.

The campaign packet carries the world.

### Deep Start

Use for campaigns.

Time: 60 to 90 minutes.

Procedure:

1. Choose tone.
2. Build or read the Field.
3. Customize factions.
4. Draw the map.
5. Create characters.
6. Set relationships.
7. Establish clocks.
8. Choose the Pilot Episode.

Deep Start is for commitment play, not one-shots.
