# Buyer Needs 6GM Analysis

## Purpose

Run the Unstuck Deck buyer-needs model through the 6 Game Master faces before expanding the 48-card matrix into full cards.

The question is:

> Does the buyer's reason for wanting the deck align at every level of the system?

## Cast Statement

The buyer is not purchasing insight alone.

They are purchasing a guided way to move from:

> I can recognize the pattern, but I still cannot move.

to:

> This protective pattern makes sense, I can meet the part inside it, and I can take one honest next move.

## Short Verdict

The buyer-needs model is strong enough to drive the next 48-card pass.

It is strongest where the deck promises:

- precise recognition,
- dignity instead of shame,
- a clear Door/Gate route,
- and one contained next move.

The main risk is not lack of depth. The main risk is overloading each card with too much depth at once.

Use the 6GM analysis as a guardrail:

| Face | Alignment Verdict | Design Guardrail |
|---|---|---|
| Shaman | Strong | Keep felt signal before explanation. |
| Challenger | Needs edge | Every card must name the cost of staying protected. |
| Regent | Needs containment | Do not make each card carry the whole system. |
| Architect | Strong | Add buyer-need fields to the matrix before drafting full cards. |
| Diplomat | Needs care | Treat Gate names as parts, not identities. |
| Sage | Strong | Judge every card by dignified movement. |

## Shaman - Does This Honor Signal?

The Shaman face asks whether the deck honors felt reality, shadow, charge, and the signal before the story.

### Alignment

The buyer-needs model aligns strongly with Shaman because the core promise is signal translation:

> You are not broken. A real emotional signal got trapped inside a protective pattern.

That is Shaman-native language. It treats stuckness as meaningful before it treats stuckness as a problem.

### Risk

The card system could become too cognitive.

If every card turns into a precise diagnosis, the buyer gets more language but not more movement. This is especially risky for the primary avatar, who is already emotionally literate and over-languaged.

### Requirement

Every card must include one felt-sense, body, charge, or signal move before it explains the pattern.

The player should feel:

> Something in me was contacted before something about me was analyzed.

### Card-Level Test

Before the card gives meaning, does it help the player locate signal?

## Challenger - Does This Actually Move?

The Challenger face asks what is weak, false, over-softened, or avoiding contact.

### Alignment

The buyer-needs model correctly identifies that the buyer does not need more beautiful explanation.

They need a route out of the loop.

### Risk

The deck could become too affirming.

Because the anti-shame frame is so important, the cards may over-protect the buyer from the real cost of their stuckness. A card that only says "this makes sense" gives relief, but it may not create movement.

### Requirement

Every card must name the cost of staying protected.

The cost should be clean, specific, and non-shaming.

Examples:

- The signal stays noisy because it never gets tested.
- The truth stays safe because it never becomes contact.
- The system stays elegant because it never risks feedback.
- The relationship stays peaceful because you are not fully in it.

### Card-Level Test

Does the card tell the truth sharply enough that the buyer cannot use the card to keep looping?

## Regent - What Must Be Preserved?

The Regent face asks what must be protected, inherited, sequenced, and held.

### Alignment

The core structure is worth preserving:

- 6 Doors,
- 8 Gates,
- 48 journey cards,
- 4 meta cards,
- EA channel entry,
- 321 deepening,
- BAR capture.

This structure gives the buyer enough trust to enter a deep practice without feeling abandoned inside it.

### Risk

The card anatomy may become too heavy.

If every card tries to perform recognition, dignity, diagnosis, contact, movement, capture, 321, BAR, and integration all at full strength, the user may feel assigned more work than they can metabolize.

### Requirement

Every journey card needs a minimum viable practice:

1. Recognition
2. Protective intelligence
3. Cost
4. One move
5. Capture phrase

321 and BAR can be visible but modular. The card should not feel like a worksheet unless the player chooses the deeper layer.

### Card-Level Test

Can the player complete the card in 3-7 minutes and still receive a real shift?

## Architect - Does The Structure Hold?

The Architect face asks whether the system can actually produce 48 distinct cards without generic repetition.

### Alignment

The reverse-engineering formula is structurally sound:

```text
Buyer pain
  -> Door distortion
  -> Gate voice overworking or misassigned
  -> pattern intelligence
  -> smaller cleaner job
  -> one honest next move
  -> reusable pattern phrase
```

This formula can generate the 48 card jobs without collapsing into vague prompt-deck language.

### Risk

The current 48-card matrix has good card jobs, but it does not yet expose the buyer-need logic inside each cell.

That means a future drafting pass could write elegant cards that do not clearly answer why someone bought the deck.

### Requirement

Before writing full cards, refine the matrix with these fields:

| Field | Role |
|---|---|
| Buyer Pain | The lived stuckness that makes the card feel worth drawing. |
| Door Distortion | The developmental stuck style. |
| Gate Overwork | What the voice is doing too broadly or too intensely. |
| Pattern Intelligence | Why the pattern makes sense. For most Gates this is protective intelligence; for Vulnerable Child it is developmental intelligence. |
| Smaller Cleaner Job | What the voice should do instead. |
| One Move | The action, body move, conversation move, or reality test. |
| BAR Phrase | The reusable pattern language the buyer keeps. |

### Card-Level Test

Can another writer use the row to draft the same card without losing the buyer's need?

## Diplomat - Is This Safe Enough To Enter?

The Diplomat face asks whether the deck creates enough trust, consent, and relational safety for a buyer to encounter sharp material.

### Alignment

The anti-shame frame is strong.

The deck repeatedly says the pattern has intelligence, not defect. For most Gates this is protective intelligence; for Vulnerable Child it is developmental intelligence. That distinction is essential for the buyer, especially around Gates like Victim, Damaged Self, and Vulnerable Child.

### Risk

Some Gate names are intentionally strong.

The user has decided to keep Victim, Damaged Self, and Vulnerable Child for now. That can work, but the cards must introduce those Gates as voices or parts, not labels for the person.

Bad:

> You are in Victim.

Better:

> A Victim voice may be carrying the place where agency went offline.

### Requirement

Every card must preserve the player's dignity at the exact moment it names the difficult part.

Use part-language:

- "a part of you,"
- "this voice,"
- "the place in you,"
- "the pattern,"
- "the protective strategy."

Avoid identity-language:

- "you are,"
- "your problem is,"
- "your wound means,"
- "this proves."

### Card-Level Test

Could a tender, intelligent buyer read this card while already activated and still feel respected?

## Sage - What Principle Integrates The Work?

The Sage face asks for the principle beneath the whole system.

### Alignment

The strongest product principle is:

> The buyer is purchasing dignified movement.

Not insight.

Not diagnosis.

Not a spiritual label.

Not motivation.

They are purchasing a way to move while preserving the truth that their stuckness made sense.

### Risk

The system can become fascinated by its own map.

The architecture is rich enough that it could become the product instead of the engine. Publicly, the deck must stay simple:

> Find the Door. Meet the voice. Take the next honest move.

### Requirement

Every card should be judged by one question:

> Does this turn stuckness from verdict into signal, and signal into one honest move?

### Card-Level Test

After using the card, can the player say what changed in plain language?

## Cross-Face Alignment Table

| Buyer Need | Face That Protects It | Failure Mode If Missing |
|---|---|---|
| "That is me." | Shaman | Card feels generic or over-intellectual. |
| "This makes sense." | Diplomat | Card creates shame or defensiveness. |
| "Now I see the shape." | Architect | Card becomes a loose reflection prompt. |
| "This is costing me something." | Challenger | Card soothes the loop instead of interrupting it. |
| "I can do one thing." | Regent | Card over-assigns, overwhelms, or diffuses action. |
| "I can recognize this next time." | Sage | Insight does not become reusable pattern language. |

## Final Card Design Requirements

Each journey card must pass six buyer-alignment checks:

1. It sees the player specifically.
2. It preserves dignity before naming cost.
3. It shows the pattern intelligence.
4. It names the cost of staying protected.
5. It gives one embodied, relational, or reality-facing move.
6. It leaves a reusable phrase the player can recognize later.

## Recommendation

Proceed with the 48-card matrix.

The buyer-needs model is strong enough, but the next matrix pass should add the buyer-need layer before full card drafting.

Recommended next matrix columns:

| Door | Gate | Buyer Pain | Voice Overwork | Pattern Intelligence | Smaller Cleaner Job | One Move | BAR Phrase | Draft Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

This will make the card jobs easier and keep the deck aligned with the actual reason people buy it:

> They do not need another way to name the pattern. They need a practice that helps them move without turning the pattern into proof against themselves.
