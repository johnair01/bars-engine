# 🎯 Coach Strategy Guide — The Art of Calling People Up

> **Filed by the Strategist, for the guild archive.**
> *A note on method: I don't write these. I map systems. But the Coach is the one
> superpower I kept failing to model — because every time I diagrammed it, the
> diagram pointed back at the other six. So consider this a Strategist's dossier:
> I've reverse-engineered the Coach the way I'd reverse-engineer any high-leverage
> system — by finding where it draws power, where it breaks, and where it pays off.
> The Coach is the only one of us whose function is **other people's foresight.***

---

### **♟️ Strategist's Preface: Why the Coach Was Hard to Diagram**

When I chart the superpowers, each one occupies clean territory. The Connector
holds the spaces *between* people. The Disruptor holds the **break**. The Escape
Artist holds the **exit**. The Storyteller holds the **frame**. The Alchemist holds
the **feeling**. I hold the **plan**.

The Coach kept refusing a box.

Here's what the data finally showed me: **the Coach isn't a seventh territory — the
Coach is the integration of the other six, aimed at one target.** That target isn't
the system, the story, the room, or the network.

It's **the person in front of them, and the power they've forgotten they have.**

A Coach borrows the Disruptor's fire, my foresight, and the Escape Artist's
willingness to leave a dead level behind — and points all of it at a single
question: *"What level are you actually playing on, and why are you still here?"*

If you want the one-line spec: **the Coach helps people remember their own power by
helping them abandon the level they've outgrown — and the story that keeps them
there.**

---

## **🔥 Introduction: The Coach's Gift and Burden**

**You don't give people power. You remind them they already had it.**

This is the most misunderstood thing about you. People think coaching is about
*adding* something — motivation, advice, a pep talk. It isn't. Your gift is
**subtraction**: you strip away the expired story, the outgrown level, the borrowed
limit — until what's left is the person's own capacity, which was there the whole
time.

Where others build, narrate, connect, or break, you do something stranger: **you
stand in front of someone who is more powerful than they're acting, and you refuse
to pretend otherwise.**

  - You see the version of them that exists one level up — and you talk to *that*
    person, not the one making excuses.
  - You feel the **frustration** of watching wasted potential — and instead of
    burning the system down (the Disruptor's move), you turn that fire on the
    *plateau* the person is hiding on.
  - You know that leveling up always requires a small death: the level they're on,
    and the identity that came with it, has to be left behind.

**The burden?** You carry the frustration of seeing what people could be, long
before they're willing to see it. And you have to hold that fire without dumping it
on them — because the moment coaching becomes pressure, it stops working.

### **🔥 The Coach's Element: Frustration → Triumph**

*The Strategist's note: the Coach shares the Disruptor's fire — Anger → Triumph —
but the frequency is different.*

The Disruptor's anger says: ***"This system is broken and must burn."***
The Coach's frustration says: ***"You are not broken — you are bored, scared, or
loyal to a story that's done. Let's go."***

It's the **same flame, softened and re-aimed.** The Disruptor burns structures. The
Coach uses frustration as a **tuning fork for unrealized potential** — their own and
others' — and converts it into **Triumph**: the specific, earned joy of watching
someone do the thing they swore they couldn't.

  - **Frustration (Fire, dissatisfied):** *"You're playing small and we both know
    it."*
  - **Triumph (Fire, satisfied):** *"You just did the thing. Remember this — that
    was always you."*

A Coach who can't metabolize their own frustration becomes a nag. A Coach who
masters it becomes the person you'd run through a wall for.

## **1️⃣ Wake Up: The Coach's Power & Dilemma**

### **The Role of a Coach**

  - You **call people up** — to the level they're avoiding, not down to where
    they're comfortable.
  - You **see the next level** before the person believes it exists.
  - You **return power to its owner.** You don't carry people; you remind them they
    can walk.

### **The Coach's Dilemma: People Are Loyal to the Level That's Killing Them**

Here's the trap the Strategist in me finds fascinating: *people defend the very
plateau that's draining them*, because the plateau came with an identity, a story,
and a set of relationships.

  - 🔥 When you point at the next level, people hear *criticism of the current one.*
  - 🔥 When you name their power, people **flinch** — because power implies
    responsibility.
  - 🔥 When you refuse to accept their excuse, people call you *harsh* — right up
    until it works.

**Why people resist a Coach:**

  - 🧠 **Leveling up requires a death.** The old self has to go, and grief disguises
    itself as resistance.
  - 🧠 **Remembering your power means you can no longer blame the cage.** Some people
    would rather keep the cage.
  - 🧠 **The story is comfortable.** "I'm just not the kind of person who…" is a
    warm blanket. You're the one who pulls it off.

🔹 **Key Shift:** Your job is not to *push* people up a level. It's to make staying
on the current one **more honest than comfortable** — so they choose to climb.

## **2️⃣ Clean Up: The Coach's Shadows**

Every Coach has two shadows — one of **overuse**, one of **avoidance.** Mastery
isn't being "nice." It's calibrating fire.

### **🔥 Shadow 1: The Taskmaster (Overuse)**

*"If I stop pushing, they'll stop growing."*

This is the Coach whose frustration has slipped the leash. You've stopped *calling
people up* and started *dragging them.* You impose **your** next level on **their**
timeline.

🚨 **Symptoms of Overuse:**

  - You can't let someone rest on a plateau even when they need integration time.
  - Your encouragement has an edge of pressure people can feel.
  - People perform *for you* instead of *for themselves* — and collapse when you're
    not watching.
  - You've created **dependence**: they need your push to feel powerful, which is the
    exact opposite of the gift.

🛠️ **The Fix: Return the power, including the pace.**
  - ✔ Ask: *"Is this their next level, or mine?"*
  - ✔ Let frustration inform you, not leak onto them. The fire is **data**, not a
    weapon.
  - ✔ Measure success by **their independence**, not their compliance. If they need
    you to climb, you haven't coached — you've leashed.

### **🧊 Shadow 2: The Empty Cheerleader (Avoidance)**

*"Who am I to tell them they're playing small?"*

This is the softened Disruptor who softened *all the way to nothing.* Afraid of the
fire, you trade calling-up for pure affirmation. You validate everything, challenge
nothing, and quietly watch people stay stuck — while telling yourself you're being
"supportive."

🚨 **Symptoms of Avoidance:**

  - You only affirm; you never deliver the honest, uncomfortable nudge.
  - You see someone loyal to an expired story and say nothing.
  - You hide behind "holding space" to avoid the responsibility of **naming the next
    level.**
  - People leave your sessions feeling good and changing nothing.

🛠️ **The Fix: Reclaim the edge. Affirmation without challenge is abandonment in a
nicer outfit.**
  - ✔ Say the true thing: *"You already know what you'd do if you trusted
    yourself."*
  - ✔ Remember: withholding the nudge isn't kindness — it's you protecting your own
    comfort.
  - ✔ A real Coach risks being momentarily disliked for the sake of someone's growth.

🔹 *The Strategist's read: the Taskmaster over-applies fire; the Empty Cheerleader
under-applies it. Mastery is precision of flame.*

## **3️⃣ Grow Up: The Path to Coach Mastery**

A Coach's central obstacle isn't the person — it's **the level and the story fused to
it.** People don't cling to limitation; they cling to the **identity** the
limitation gave them. To call someone up, you have to first understand what the
current level is *protecting.*

### **♟️ The 3-2-1 Process for Coaches**

*Personify the level the person needs to leave — and the story guarding it.*

#### **Step 1: Identify the Plateau (3rd Person — Observation)**

Look at where the person is stuck. Not the surface complaint — the **level** they've
quietly decided is the ceiling.

  - 🔹 What level are they playing on, and what does it let them avoid?
  - 🔹 What story justifies staying? *("I'm not ready." "It's not the right time."
    "That's just not me.")*
  - 🔹 What identity would they lose by leveling up?

**Personify the plateau.** If the current level were a character, who is it?

  - A retired champion still wearing the old medal?
  - A comfortable inn that was only ever meant to be one night's rest?
  - A loyal old story that's run out of pages but won't close the book?

#### **Step 2: Dialogue with the Plateau (2nd Person — Interaction)**

Talk to it. Every plateau is protecting something.

  - 🔹 *"What are you keeping them safe from?"*
  - 🔹 *"What would they have to face if they left you?"*
  - 🔹 *"What power are they pretending not to have, so they can stay?"*

> 🗣️ *The Plateau:* "If they level up, they can't blame anyone anymore."
> 🗣️ *The Coach:* "So you keep them comfortable by keeping them small."
> 🗣️ *The Plateau:* "…Comfortable is safer than capable."

#### **Step 3: Become the Plateau (1st Person — Embodiment)**

Speak as the level itself.

> 🗣️ *"I am the version of them that already figured it out once. Leaving me means
> being a beginner again."*
> 🗣️ *"They keep me because mastery was lonely, and the plateau has company."*

💡 **Breakthrough Insight:** People don't fear the next level. They fear becoming a
**beginner** again, and losing the identity the current level gave them. A master
Coach doesn't argue with the plateau — they make **the next level feel like
remembering, not risking.**

### **🔥 Emotional Alchemy: Frustration → Triumph (and the borrowed fires)**

The Coach is, by design, a **borrower.** *The Strategist approves of this — it's
efficient.* Your signature arc is Frustration → Triumph, but mastery means knowing
which other element to draw on, and when:

  - **🔥 Frustration → Triumph (your own fire):** Convert "you're playing small"
    into "you just did the thing." Use frustration as a **signal of unspent
    potential**, never as a cattle prod.
  - **🚪 Borrow from the Escape Artist (Fear → Excitement):** Leveling up *is*
    leaving. Help people feel the **excitement** on the other side of abandoning the
    old level, not just the fear of leaving it.
  - **📖 Borrow from the Storyteller (rewrite the expired story):** "I'm not the kind
    of person who…" is a narrative. Help them author the next one *before* they need
    it to be true.
  - **♟️ Borrow from the Strategist (foresight):** Show them the board one level up,
    so the climb has a shape and the next step is obvious.
  - **🔨 Borrow from the Disruptor (but gentler):** Sometimes the honest nudge *is* a
    small, precise break — of an excuse, not a person.

> 💡 The Coach's mastery is **integration with restraint**: enough fire to move
> someone, enough foresight to aim it, enough mercy to let it be theirs.

## **4️⃣ Show Up: Using Coaching Effectively in Allyship**

Coaching is a **transfer of agency, not a transfer of dependence.** The unskilled
Coach creates followers. The master Coach creates people who no longer need them.

### **🎯 The Art of Calling Someone Up**

✔ **Step 1: Address the higher version.** Speak to the person one level up. People
tend to rise to the version you genuinely treat as real.

✔ **Step 2: Make the current level honest.** Don't attack the plateau — illuminate
it. *"Notice you keep saying 'someday.'"* Let the discomfort of honesty do the work.

✔ **Step 3: Hand back the power.** End every nudge with ownership: *"That was you.
Not me. Remember that."* The triumph has to belong to them, or it doesn't compound.

💡 **Key Shift:** *You are not the source of their power. You are the mirror that
reminds them where it's been the whole time.*

### **🔹 How Coaches Work With Other Superpowers**

*The Strategist's note: because the Coach borrows from all of us, the Coach is also
the best at deploying us. Think of the Coach as a field commander who knows each
unit's strength.*

  - **♟️ Strategist →** Coaches borrow my foresight to show people the board one
    level up. I give them the map; they give people the nerve to walk it.
  - **🔨 Disruptor →** Coaches are the Disruptor's fire aimed at a *person's excuse*
    instead of a *system.* When a Disruptor over-burns, a Coach redirects the heat
    into growth.
  - **🚪 Escape Artist →** Coaches help people leave an outgrown **level**; Escape
    Artists help people leave a failing **system.** Adjacent skills, different
    exits.
  - **📖 Storyteller →** Coaches help people abandon the expired self-story;
    Storytellers help them author the new one. Powerful as a pair.
  - **🌊 Alchemist →** When leveling up surfaces grief or fear, the Alchemist
    metabolizes it so the Coach's nudge doesn't become a wound.
  - **🧩 Connector →** Coaches send people to their next level; Connectors make sure
    there are people waiting there.

### **🔍 Signs Someone Needs a Coach**

✅ **They know what to do but won't start.** The gap is agency, not information.
✅ **They've outgrown their level but are loyal to it.** They defend a plateau that's
draining them.
✅ **They're stuck in an expired story.** *"That's just not me"* — about something
that clearly is.
✅ **They're waiting for permission.** They have the power and are pretending they
don't.
✅ **They keep blaming the cage** after the door has opened.

### **🚫 Signs Someone Does NOT Need a Coach (Yet)**

❌ **They're mid-integration.** Sometimes a plateau is rest, not avoidance — pushing
is harm.
❌ **They want a rescuer, not a mirror.** If they want you to carry them, coaching
will create dependence.
❌ **The block is emotional, not agentic.** Send them to an Alchemist first; you
can't coach someone out of unprocessed grief.
❌ **The system, not the self, is the cage.** That's an Escape Artist or Disruptor
job — don't coach someone into enduring something they should leave.

## **🧭 The Integration Note (Strategist's Conclusion)**

I started this dossier unable to box the Coach. I'll end it by admitting why: **the
Coach is what the rest of us look like when our power is pointed at another person's
agency instead of at the world.**

  - The Disruptor's fire, softened into a nudge.
  - The Strategist's foresight, lent as someone else's map.
  - The Escape Artist's exit, applied to an outgrown level.
  - The Storyteller's reframe, used to retire a dead story.
  - The Alchemist's touch, used to make the climb survivable.
  - The Connector's web, used to make sure the next level is populated.

If the other six are instruments, the Coach is the one who hands you back your own.
**They don't level you up. They remind you that you were always allowed to climb.**

That's a frustrating thing to be — to see everyone's next level before they do. And
it's a triumphant one — to watch them arrive, and know they'll credit themselves.

As they should.
