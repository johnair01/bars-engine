# Hearts Blazing — Face Agent Review
*Game Master faces: Challenger, Shaman, Regent*
*Source: Hearts Blazing v0.9 (Games by Play Date, Oct 2015)*
*Context: What can BARs Engine steal from this card-based collaborative storygame?*

---

## What Hearts Blazing Is

A 3-5 player collaborative storygame of speculative fiction. Players choose/are assigned one of five **Roles** (Ace, Leader, Rookie, Engineer, Veteran), each with a character **cliche deck**. A randomized **Plotline deck** of episode cards creates scenes. Players **bid cliches** on episodes to secure **keyword awards** tied to their hidden **Motive**. At the Finale, motives are revealed and the player who secured the most of their keywords narrates last.

**Core structural components:**
- 5 roles × ~12 cliche cards each (values: 6, 3, 3, 2, 2, 2, 1, 1, 1, -1, -1, -1)
- 40 episode cards (named like TV episodes, each In Focus on a specific role)
- 5 secret motives (Avenger/Guardian/Savior/Survivor/Opportunist), each tracking 2 keywords
- 5 keywords (Justice, Triumph, Honor, Safety, Endurance) that rotate across motives
- Frailty cards: -1 value, voluntarily played for depth, penalize if held at Finale

**The cliche card structure** — one title, three manifestation lines:
> "Eagle Eyes" (value 1): *Spot a crucial feature from a great distance or through heavy concealment. / Take advantage of a hidden approach or unseen path. / Understand the true feelings or motives of another.*

**Example episode card:**
> "Shore Leave" / In Focus: Veteran / "Finally, some much needed R&R. Too bad that the moment you hit home all hell broke loose." / 1st place: Honor, Justice / 2nd place: Endurance

## SHAMAN: The Mythic Register

### What Myth Is Hearts Blazing Actually Staging?

Hearts Blazing is not staging a sci-fi adventure. It is staging the **myth of the hidden covenant** — the story of people who cannot say why they are here until the moment of reckoning forces it out of them.

The structural tell is the secret Motive system. The Finale is not a plot climax — it is a **confession**. The game is a ritual container for the question: *What were you actually serving all along?*

The five Motives through an Integral lens:
- **Avenger** (Justice + Triumph) — the mythic wound made into a vow. Prometheus. Inigo Montoya. The self that cannot rest until the world is corrected.
- **Guardian** (Safety + Honor) — the self that subordinates its own survival to the protection of others. Pre-personal transcended into post-conventional service.
- **Savior** (Honor + Justice) — the messianic charge it did not ask for and cannot put down.
- **Survivor** (Endurance + Safety) — the self broken enough to know that making it through is the only honest ambition left.
- **Opportunist** (Triumph + Endurance) — the Trickster. The hidden motive is themselves. They must decide whether that is shameful or honest.

These are not personality types. These are **orientations toward suffering**. The antagonist is not the villain — the antagonist is the situation that strips away role performance and reveals what was underneath.

### Pop-Culture as Mythic Delivery System

When Hearts Blazing names Kara "Starbuck" Thrace as the Ace, it is not using her as an illustration. It is **invoking a pre-loaded mythic field**. What you receive with Starbuck that an abstract description cannot give you:
- The specific quality of someone who is the best at the thing destroying them
- The loneliness of the person whose competence is a wall against intimacy
- How her excellence and self-destruction are the same gesture

An abstract description says: "Skilled, independent, reckless." The cultural reference says: *you already know what this costs her*. The player inherits the psychic weight rather than constructing it from scratch.

The ontological move: character examples do not define the role — they **resonate** it into existence. Zoe Washburne and Daryl Dixon standing next to each other tell you the range of the Veteran. Their difference shows the range; their similarity shows the essence. Neither a rulebook definition nor a single example could do what the cluster does.

**This is directly relevant to BARs Engine.** The allyship community in Portland has its own canon — its own "Starbuck moments." The question is which cultural references carry the right charge for that community.

### The Frailty Mechanic as Shadow Work

The Frailty card is **voluntary descent**. The player knows there is a cost. They choose it anyway because the game has designed the descent to have value — narrative depth is the reward during play. The Finale penalty for holding the Frailty enforces the shadow integration rule: *you cannot carry it unexamined*. Either you work with it in the story, or it costs you at the end.

Compare to BARs Engine's shadow/cost distinction:
- **BARs treats shadow as something to metabolize outward into service.** The clean_up move transforms the cost into a contribution.
- **Hearts Blazing treats Frailty as something to be witnessed and released within story** — worked through narrative, not through a personal development arc.

Both are legitimate. Hearts Blazing is doing ritual drama. BARs Engine is doing integral practice. What BARs currently lacks is the **voluntary invitation of shadow as a mechanic**. There is no card that says: *I will take on a cost now because it will make the story richer.* BARs presents shadow as something you already have that you are working through — not something you can choose to pick up because depth requires it.

### Episode Cards as Ritual Containers

"Shore Leave", "Bloodletting", "Dark Omens", "Ships of Light" — these are not plot descriptions. They are **mythic frames**:
- **Shore Leave**: permission to put down the mission. What are you like when the role is not required?
- **Bloodletting**: the medieval practice of releasing what has accumulated. Something has been building; the release will be messy.
- **Dark Omens**: the moment before. The atmosphere of foreboding as its own ritual.
- **Ships of Light**: the encounter with something that exceeds the frame.

This is **genre as liturgy**. The TV episode format is a container the culture has agreed carries certain kinds of meaning. By naming into that container, Hearts Blazing lets players step into a pre-existing ritual form without constructing it from scratch. The episode title is a directive to the group's collective creative unconscious: *this is the kind of story we are in right now.*

### Concrete Language Recommendations

For BARs Engine's Portland/allyship context, the cultural referents that carry the right charge:

**Clean_up moves** could invoke characters known for public accountability with grace — the mythic register here is *the one who did not run*. Not heroic sacrifice — quiet, costly, steady repair.

**Grow_up moves**: characters who have visibly changed, who held a worldview and then revised it under pressure. *The one who was wrong and became useful.*

**Show_up moves**: characters whose competence is placed in service of something larger than their reputation. *The one who stopped performing and started contributing.*

**Quest/episode naming vocabulary** that carries mythic weight without pop-culture (respecting the community's relationship to shared references):
- Quality of relational weather: "After the Rain", "The Long Before", "What the Silence Holds"
- Threshold language: "At the Door", "Before the Circle Opens", "The Night Before the Thing"
- Community repair: "Coming Back Around", "What We Owe", "The Reckoning We Kept"
- Developmental descent: "Going Under to Come Through", "The Long Way Home", "What Grief Taught"

**The single most transferable mechanic**: A **Frailty-equivalent card** in BARs — perhaps "Carried Weight" or named after the specific shadow belief — that players voluntarily introduce as a cost they choose to metabolize. Not: *here is the shadow you already have.* But: *I am choosing to pick up this weight because the story requires it and I trust the system to help me work with it.*

## CHALLENGER: What to Steal, What to Leave

### Steal Directly

**1. The Cliche Card Naming Convention**

This is the highest-value thing in Hearts Blazing and BARs Engine is not doing it. Look at the contrast:

- *Hearts Blazing*: "Get to Da Choppa!", "Don't Tell Me The Odds", "True Grit", "Bubblegum and Tennis Rackets"
- *BARs Engine*: "Step Through (Excitement)", "Reclaim Meaning", "Commit to Growth", "Achieve Breakthrough (Triumph)", "Stabilize Coherence"

The BARs move names are accurate descriptions of emotional alchemy operations. They are also invisible. Nobody will ever say "I played Stabilize Coherence" and feel something shift.

The Hearts Blazing names work because they are already culturally loaded. "True Grit" does not describe endurance — it summons endurance from a place in the body where you have already experienced it through story, film, and myth. The name fires an existing pattern; it does not build a new one. That is neurological compression, not aesthetics.

**2. The Triple-Context Design**

Each Hearts Blazing cliche has three manifestations: combat/conflict, social/interpersonal, meta-narrative. BARs Engine has a single `description` per canonical move.

BARs Engine has the Integral quadrant structure to do this properly. A move like "Achieve Breakthrough (Triumph)" should have:
- An I/individual/interior expression (what it feels like internally)
- A We/collective/interior expression (how it reads in relationship)
- An It/individual/exterior expression (what behavior it produces)
- An Its/collective/exterior expression (what it changes in the system)

The system already thinks in quadrants. The cards do not. That is a gap.

**3. The Frailty Card (structure, not the word)**

The Frailty card makes the cost of shadow work into a **playable object**. The player is not being asked to "do shadow work." They are being asked to decide whether to play a card they are holding. The agency transfer is total.

BARs currently metabolizes shadow in the Shaman's codebase move (`nameShadowBelief` → insight BAR). That is the GM producing the artifact. The Frailty card inverts it: the player produces the artifact and the GM witnesses it. That is a fundamentally different power dynamic and it matters for the Portland context in particular.

The shadow belief named during 321 should have a card form that the player holds. The card should carry the named belief, the shadow name, and an explicit cost. The player must be able to *not* play it. The card being held-but-unplayed is a meaningful game state.

### What Does Not Translate

**The bidding economy**: Hearts Blazing uses bid values as resource allocation within competitive narrative episodes. BARs is not a bidding game. The emotional alchemy economy is personal throughput, not social bid for narrative control.

**The secret motive system**: The reveal mechanic requires competitive pressure to function. Without another player blocking your keywords, there is nothing forcing the revelation. More importantly: BARs is building toward self-knowledge, not revelation to others. Making shadow work a performative reveal in a group setting is precisely what the Portland community will allergically reject as psychological theater.

**The genre archetypes**: Ace/Leader/Rookie/Engineer/Veteran are genre archetypes from military/adventure fiction. BARs' 8 archetypes are developmental types, not character roles in a shared narrative. Do not try to port the genre logic.

### Pop-Culture as Compression Technology: The Substrate Problem

"Get to Da Choppa!" collapses an entire emotional scenario into three syllables. Every person who has seen that film or its derivative cultural moments carries the scenario already pre-loaded. The card name retrieves meaning; it does not create it.

Generic move names like "Declare Intention" require the player to build the scenario from scratch from the description text. That takes cognitive load that should be going into the actual play decision.

**But whose cultural references?** Hearts Blazing draws from action film, military, science fiction, and Western archetypes. These are broadly legible across a particular demographic and completely absent from others.

BARs Engine is building for Portland allyship workers. The cultural compression library useful to that community is different:
- Organizer folklore ("Hold the door", "Don't DARVO", "Speak from experience")
- Movement history references with weight in that room
- Somatic language common in Portland healing spaces
- The specific emotional textures of the allyship domains

The principle is directly applicable. The source library is not.

### Recommendations

1. **Build a second-register name for each of the 15 canonical moves.** Keep the current name as the canonical identifier. Add a cliche name from the correct cultural substrate that does emotional compression. The cliche name goes on the card face. This requires authorial work by the creator, not AI generation — the cliche names must come from lived cultural memory.

2. **Write three expressions for each of the 15 canonical moves** — what it feels like internally (I), interpersonally (We), and what it produces in a system (Its). Start with the 5 Transcend moves.

3. **Build a shadow card that stays in the player's hand.** The shadow belief named during 321 should have a card form the player holds. The card is a meaningful held object; being held-but-unplayed is a game state the system should track.

4. **Name the Portland allergy directly in the design doc.** The card design language should feel more like a ceremony than a game show. That is a tonal constraint, not mechanical. But it is the constraint that determines whether the community picks it up or puts it back down.

## REGENT: Structural Analysis

### Triple-Context Design — What Problem It Solves

Hearts Blazing's three lines per card solve **context collapse** — without them, "Eagle Eyes" would default to its most literal reading. The three lines pre-resolve translation across scene types without requiring a separate card per register.

**What BARs gains by not using this format**: the solitaire context means the player is generating their own application. A single description is sufficient when the card is a prompt-to-action. BARs descriptions are instructional ("do this"), not applicability-mapping ("this works here, here, here"). Appropriate for solo play.

**What BARs loses**: in group play, the single description creates asymmetric interpretation. Two players holding the same `moveType` card may play it into different contexts with no structural mechanism to validate either reading. Hearts Blazing's triple lines serve as a **shared interpretive contract**. BARs has no equivalent for multi-player card reads.

**Structural recommendation**: Add an optional `contextLines` JSON field to `CustomBar` (array, max 3 entries, keyed `solo`/`interpersonal`/`narrative`). Null for solo-only cards. Does not break the solitaire test; enables the interpretive contract for group play.

### Bid Intensity vs. BARs PlayCost

Hearts Blazing: 1 (everyday competence), 2 (reliable/capable), 3 (skilled/practiced), 6 (defining moment / identity-level). The 6-value cards are identity statements, not capability claims.

BARs Engine's `playCost` is binary: 1 or 2. This maps only to Hearts Blazing's 1–2 band. BARs has no encoding for the 3 (skilled, repeated mastery) or 6 (identity-constituting, once-per-arc) registers.

This is a material underspecification. The `moveType` axis provides developmental register; `playCost` provides economic cost. But **developmental register and intensity register are orthogonal** — a wake_up card can be everyday (1) or identity-constituting (6). The current schema encodes one axis and omits the other.

**Structural recommendation**: Add `intensity` as a discrete enum field: `everyday` (1), `capable` (2), `skilled` (3), `defining` (6). Decouple from `playCost`. A `defining` card should be rare (one per arc, enforced) and produce a separate log category.

### The Frailty Mechanic — What BARs Shadow Cards Are Not Doing

Frailty does three structural things simultaneously:
1. Voluntary self-handicap with immediate tactical cost and deferred narrative payoff
2. A bid resource that can be played strategically (throw an episode to prep Finale position)
3. Held Frailties penalize at Finale, forcing shadow material into narrative expression before the game ends

BARs' `clean_up` is the closest analog, but clean_up in BARs is a **positive action** (playing it produces progress, earns reward). There is no card in BARs that functions as a voluntary negative — something that reduces current throughput in exchange for long-term structural benefit.

`nameShadowBelief` (Shaman face, barType `insight`) is a partial equivalent: naming a shadow belief has no immediate reward but produces developmental value. But it is not played into a cost economy. **It does not reduce anything.** BARs has shadow acknowledgment but no shadow cost — the acknowledgment has no downward pressure on current play capacity. This is a structural gap if BARs intends to model the actual phenomenology of carrying unresolved shadow material, which does reduce capacity.

**Structural recommendation**: Create a `ShadowLoad` model (or field on `PlayerQuest`) that accumulates when shadow-material bars are held unplayed. Shadow load reduces the `reward` on the next completed bar by a calculable amount. The Shaman's `nameShadowBelief` action should optionally create a `ShadowLoad` entry rather than just an insight bar.

### Episode Cards — In Focus as Structural Container

Hearts Blazing episode cards assign an In Focus role, rotating narrative weight across the arc. This makes some players' bids more strategically loaded — the In Focus player has greater narrative interest in winning the episode.

BARs lacks a per-period "In Focus" designation. `kotterStage` and `campaignRef` identify the campaign phase, but not the current focal player, arc, or developmental line. All players in a BARs campaign are equally in-focus at all times, which reduces strategic differentiation.

**Structural recommendation**: Extend the Regent's `declarePeriod` function to optionally designate a focal `moveType` or `allyshipDomain` for the period. Cards of the focal type earn a bonus multiplier (+1 reward). Creates structural differentiation without requiring player-level arc assignment.

### Keyword/Motive — Player-Level Alignment Accumulator

Hearts Blazing's hidden Motive system reveals player orientation through bidding patterns — the information leak is structural (bidding visible; interpretation hidden).

BARs has no player-level alignment accumulator. `allyshipDomain`, `lockType`, and `emotionalAlchemyTag` attach to quests, not to players. The hexagram system (`hexagramId`) assigns cosmological orientation per quest but is explicit and GM-determined.

If BARs supports group play with players pursuing different developmental orientations, a player-level alignment accumulator that becomes legible over time would serve the same structural function: **making the developmental arc visible without requiring the player to explicitly declare it.**

**Structural recommendation**: Create a `PlayerAlignment` model that tracks keyword counts per player — which `moveType` values completed, which `allyshipDomain`, which `gameMasterFace`, which `emotionalAlchemyTag`. This accumulates silently. At campaign Finale or milestone, it becomes visible. This is the structural equivalent of Motive revealed at Finale — developmental orientation readable from play history.

## Cross-Face Synthesis

### Three Things Hearts Blazing Has That BARs Does Not

**1. Pre-loaded psychic weight through cultural referent.** BARs' card titles are functional-descriptive or metaphorical. They tell you what the mechanic does. They do not invoke a felt charge. The cliche name is compression technology that does neurological work a description cannot.

**2. Voluntary shadow as a mechanic.** BARs has shadow costs but they tend to be encountered, not chosen. There is no equivalent of the Frailty card: the move where you say, *I am going to introduce my own limitation into this because the story requires it.* This would be a significant addition — the capacity to consciously pick up shadow material as a form of depth-seeking rather than metabolizing shadow material you already carry.

**3. Mythic naming at the episode/quest level.** BARs quest names tend to be task-oriented or domain-specific. Hearts Blazing's episode names work because they define a narrative atmosphere the whole group enters. The title names the felt register, not just the content.

### The Deepest Structural Parallel

What Hearts Blazing is ultimately staging — and what BARs Engine could do more explicitly — is the encounter between **the role you perform** and **the person underneath the role**. The game asks: when the situation finally strips away what you have been doing, what is revealed?

BARs Engine is asking the same question through wake_up / clean_up / grow_up / show_up. The difference is register. Hearts Blazing stages this as drama — it happens in the story. BARs stages this as practice — it happens in the player.

The opportunity for BARs is to let more of the drama in: to name the mythic weight of what is being worked, so players feel the archetypal resonance of the developmental work, not just its functional value.

**The move is not to become Hearts Blazing. The move is to let the cards know what myths they are carrying.**
