# You already care. That was never the problem.

## The problem is that nobody ever taught you the *moves.*

You've read the books. You've sat in the trainings. You know the language. And still, in the
moment that actually matters — the meeting, the family dinner, the group chat — you freeze, or you
overcorrect, or you go quiet and hate yourself for it later.

That's not a character flaw. **It's a skill gap.** Allyship got handed to you as an *identity to
defend* instead of a *practice to get good at* — so every misstep feels like a verdict on your soul
instead of a rep you can run better next time.

*Mastering the Game of Allyship* puts down the verdict and picks up the game.

## Allyship is a game. Not a game you win — a game you get better at.

Not "game" as in trivial. Game as in: **it has moves, and you can learn them.** It has a board
(where the work happens), roles (what you're built for), and a way to tell whether your last move
actually helped. Once you can *see* the moves, the paralysis breaks — because you always know what
the next one is.

The whole system runs on four moves you already half-know:

- **Wake Up** — notice what you'd rather not.
- **Clean Up** — repair the harm you carry; clear your own channel.
- **Grow Up** — build the capacity the moment needs.
- **Show Up** — put your body where your values are.

Master the throughput of those four and you stop being someone who *believes* in allyship and start
being someone who is *reliably useful* to it.
